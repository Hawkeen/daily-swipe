Episode of the day · 🎧
Kingdom of Fossil Water
In Our Time — “The Garamantes” · BBC Radio 4 · Misha Glenny · 11 June 2026
A desert empire that mined water older than farming itself.
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The hook
In the driest place on Earth, a civilisation grew grapes, figs and wheat — for a thousand years.
Greek and Roman writers dismissed the Garamantes as barbarians on the desert’s edge. Archaeology has overturned that: towns, cities, irrigation and a Saharan trade network — right in the hyper-arid Fezzan of southern Libya.
4 / 10 →
The core idea
Not nomads — a sophisticated desert state, built on engineering that borrowed water from the deep past.
The Garamantes flourished from roughly 400 BC to 700 AD, peaking in the 2nd–3rd centuries AD — often clashing with Rome to the north. Their power rested on an audacious technology for pulling underground water to the surface across the open desert.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 5
A thousand miles of tunnels
They dug foggaras — gently sloping underground channels that tapped the aquifer and let gravity carry water to the fields. Surveys credit them with hundreds of miles of tunnel, served by an estimated 100,000 vertical maintenance shafts, roughly one every 30 feet.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
Cities, not campsites
Excavations at the capital, Garama (modern Germa), and across the Fezzan revealed eight major towns and around twenty other settlements — with forts, tombs and cemeteries. The water let them farm dates, olives, wheat, barley, sorghum and millet in the sand.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
Brokers of the Sahara
Their oases sat astride routes crossing the desert, making them key middlemen in trans-Saharan trade between sub-Saharan Africa and the Mediterranean world. Far from isolated, the Garamantes were enterprising merchants plugged into a continental exchange.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
They were spending a one-time gift
The Sahara was once green, with lakes; the water in the aquifer was “fossil water,” laid down tens of thousands of years earlier and not being replenished. Over six centuries the Garamantes are estimated to have drawn billions of gallons from a reserve that could never refill.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
The well runs dry
As the water table dropped, the foggaras had to be cut ever deeper at ever greater cost in labour. The system that built the kingdom eventually strained, contracted and failed — and the civilisation faded by the late first millennium AD. A society undone by exhausting the very resource it was built on.
10 / 10 ✓
The takeaway
“The new archaeological evidence is showing that the Garamantes were brilliant farmers, resourceful engineers, and enterprising merchants who produced a remarkable civilization.”
— David Mattingly, archaeologist (University of Leicester)
Concrete next step
🏜️ Listen on a walk, then hold the thought: a desert empire fell by draining unreplenishable water — the same “fossil water” many regions pump today.
