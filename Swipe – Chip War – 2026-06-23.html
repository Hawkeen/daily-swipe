The hook
China now spends more money each year importing chips than it spends on oil.
A single category of imports outranks the fuel that runs the country. Miller’s point: semiconductors have quietly become the most strategic resource on Earth — and almost no one outside the industry noticed until the supply chain started to crack.
4 / 10 →
The core argument
Whoever controls the chip controls the 21st century — the way oil ruled the 20th.
Miller argues that today’s economy and military rest entirely on semiconductors, yet the ability to make the most advanced ones is concentrated in a tiny number of firms clustered around the Taiwan Strait. That concentration — what reviewers called “weaponized interdependence” — is the central fault line of modern geopolitics.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 5
One island is the choke point
Taiwan produces roughly 90% of the world’s most advanced logic chips, almost all of it through a single company, TSMC, which alone makes about 37% of the planet’s new computing power. A blockade or quake near the Taiwan Strait would freeze the global economy.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
TSMC’s trick: build, don’t design
TSMC became the world’s most advanced chipmaker with a radical model: it designs no chips of its own and only manufactures designs handed to it by other firms. By focusing solely on fabrication, it honed processes no rival could match — turning a humble “foundry” into the most indispensable factory on Earth.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
Born of the Cold War, raised by the state
The transistor arrived at Bell Labs in 1947; the chip industry then grew up inside a superpower arms race for self-guiding weapons. Miller shows that government support and protection — American, Japanese, Taiwanese, Chinese — was instrumental at every step. Chips were never a purely private market.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
China’s bind
Since the early 2000s China has been the biggest challenge to US dominance, pouring money in to cut its dependence on foreign — especially Taiwanese — chips. Yet it still imports more chips by value than oil. That dependence is both a vulnerability and a motive for the most expensive industrial push in its history.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
The open question the book leaves
Reviewing it for Foreign Affairs, economist Barry Eichengreen praised Miller’s storytelling but noted the book was finished before the wave of US export controls. It draws no conclusion on whether those controls can actually slow China’s chip industry — or whether they will provoke Beijing into forcing the issue over Taiwan.

The takeaway
Semiconductors are the new oil — and the world’s most critical supply runs through one contested island.
— the central thesis of Chris Miller, Chip War (2022)
Concrete next step
⚙️ Read the TSMC chapters back to back — they explain in plain English why one Taiwanese factory, not any government, may be the single most strategically important place on the planet.
