From Choke to Clutch
She made her name studying why people choke under pressure. Now she runs a university under maximum pressure.
Freakonomics Radio · Stephen Dubner with Sian Beilock, president of Dartmouth · 3 July 2026
Swipe →
5 / 11 →
The Hook
Why do the most skilled people fail exactly when it matters most?
Sian Beilock, a cognitive scientist by training, built her career answering that question in the lab. Then she took one of the most scrutinized jobs in American higher education — the presidency of Dartmouth — and turned her own research into a survival manual.
6 / 11 →
The Core Idea
Choking is not a character flaw. It is a mechanism — and mechanisms can be managed.
In the episode, Dubner visits the psychologist who mapped how pressure sabotages skilled performance — and asks whether those insights transfer from golf greens and exam halls to leading a university through higher education’s most scrutinised era.
7 / 11 →
Finding 1 / 4 · The mechanism
Worry hijacks your mental scratch pad
Beilock’s research locates choking in working memory — the prefrontal “scratch pad” that holds task-relevant information. Under pressure, worries come online and co-opt exactly those resources. The result: normally strong working memory gets overburdened, and talented people lose the brain power they need to excel.
8 / 11 →
Finding 2 / 4 · The evidence
The AstroTurf lab: experts putt 20% worse
Beilock lined a lab with AstroTurf and put golfers under pressure — prize money, an audience. Expert golfers became about 20 percent less accurate on three-to-five-foot putts. The diagnosis: “paralysis by analysis.” Experts choke when they start consciously monitoring movements that should run on autopilot.
9 / 11 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The fixes
Distract yourself — or just go faster
Two counterintuitive remedies from the lab: focus on a meaningless detail (the dimples on the golf ball) so the analytical mind has nothing to interfere with — or speed up, denying the brain time to overthink. In testing, golfers who moved more quickly improved their performance by about a third.
10 / 11 →
Finding 4 / 4 · The test case
From the lab bench to the president’s office
Beilock wrote the book on pressure — literally: “Choke” (2010) distilled her lab’s findings for performers of every kind. The episode’s live experiment is her own career: applying the science of clutch performance to a job where every decision — budgets, protests, politics — is made in public, under pressure.
11 / 11 ✓
The Takeaway
Under stress, “the prefrontal cortex stops working the way it should” — Sian Beilock, on the moment skill gives way to choking
