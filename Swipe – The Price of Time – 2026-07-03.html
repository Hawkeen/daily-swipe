Book of the day · 📖
The Price of Time
The real story of interest — and what happens when money becomes free
Edward Chancellor · financial historian
Allen Lane / Penguin, 2022 · ~5 min read
Swipe →
3 / 11 →
The Hook
What if the cheapest money in history was the most expensive mistake?
We were taught that low interest rates are a gift — cheaper loans, rising markets, easier growth. Chancellor turns that on its head: when the price of money falls to nothing, capital gets mispriced everywhere, and the bill arrives slowly, for years, in ways we struggle to trace back to the cause.
4 / 11 →
The Core Idea
Interest is the price of time — set it at zero and finance turns absurd.
Chancellor’s argument: interest is not just a cost of borrowing but the signal that disciplines all investment. When central banks drive rates to zero or below and print money without limit, that signal breaks — producing asset bubbles, misallocated capital, and, paradoxically, weaker growth rather than stronger.

Finding 1 / 5 · Ancient roots
Interest is as old as civilisation
Chancellor traces interest from its origins in ancient Mesopotamia, through the usury debates of Restoration Britain, to John Law’s ill-fated Mississippi scheme and the global credit booms of the 21st century. The lesson repeats across millennia: the manias arrive whenever the price of time is pushed too low.
6 / 11 →
Finding 2 / 5 · The Fed’s habit
A bias for cheap money, baked in since 1913
Chancellor criticises Federal Reserve policy for a tilt toward low rates dating back nearly to its 1913 founding. By working to suppress economic volatility, the Fed encouraged the build-up of financial leverage — and that easy money, he argues, is what ignited the 2008 financial crisis.
7 / 11 →
Finding 3 / 5 · The paradox
Cheap money can smother growth
The counter-intuitive core: extremely low rates don’t just inflate asset prices — Chancellor holds them largely responsible for weak economic growth, rising inequality, elevated debt and pension crises across the West. Ultra-cheap credit turns out to be less a stimulant than a slow sedative.
8 / 11 →
Finding 4 / 5 · Zombies
Firms that should have died, kept alive
One channel Chancellor keeps returning to: when credit is nearly free, unproductive ‘zombie’ companies survive on refinancing instead of failing. Capital that should flow to better uses stays trapped, and the economy’s overall productivity quietly erodes.
9 / 11 →
Finding 5 / 5 · Who pays
‘Financial repression’ and its winners
Very low rates act as a hidden transfer: savers are punished while owners of assets — houses, stocks — are rewarded, widening inequality. Reviewers single this out as the book’s sharpest theme: ‘financial repression’ that quietly reshapes who gets richer.
10 / 11 →
Why this matters in 2026
The bubble question is back
In June 2026, veteran investor Jeremy Grantham used an Odd Lots episode to warn he sees the pattern of a bursting bubble again — this time named ‘AI’. Chancellor’s history is the long-run companion to that anxiety: cheap money and euphoric markets have always travelled together.

The Takeaway
“When the price of time is set at nothing or turns negative, and central banks print money without limit, finance becomes absurd.” — Edward Chancellor
