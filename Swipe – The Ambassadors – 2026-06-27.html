The Ambassadors
Hans Holbein the Younger · oil on oak · 1533 · National Gallery, London (NG1314)
A double portrait with a secret hidden in plain sight.
Swipe →

The Hook
There is a skull in this painting — but you’ll only see it from the side.
Across the lower foreground floats a strange elongated smear. Step to the centre-right of the picture and it snaps into focus: a human skull, staring back.
4 / 9 →
What it’s about
A dazzling display of skill — and a coded meditation on death.
This monumental double portrait is at once a virtuoso show of realist technique and a coded meditation on mortality, knowledge and the religious upheavals of the Reformation. It celebrates everything humans can know — then quietly undercuts it.
5 / 9 →
Finding 1 / 4 · The sitters
Two Frenchmen in Tudor London
Painted in 1533, the picture shows Jean de Dinteville and Georges de Selve — two French envoys in England. It hangs in the National Gallery in London, where it is among the most studied works in the whole collection.
6 / 9 →
Finding 2 / 4 · The trick
A skull, distorted on purpose
That diagonal form is a perspective anamorphosis — an intentionally distorted image that only resolves when viewed from an oblique angle. Seen from the right, it becomes a skull: a memento mori, a reminder of death.
7 / 9 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The shelves
A catalogue of human knowledge
The upper shelf carries a celestial globe, a portable sundial and instruments for charting the heavens and measuring time; the lower shelf a lute, a hymn book, a book of arithmetic and a terrestrial globe. Together they celebrate the reach of human learning.
8 / 9 →
Finding 4 / 4 · The hidden cross
Can knowledge save us from death?
The skull undercuts all that learning — but look to the top-left corner. Half-hidden by the green curtain stands a crucifix, standing for resurrection and the promise of eternal life. Holbein poses the question; faith offers his answer.

The Takeaway
A painting that flatters human achievement from the front — and reminds you of your own mortality from the side.
