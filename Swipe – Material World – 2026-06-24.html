Material World
Ed Conway — Material World: The Six Raw Materials That Shape Modern Civilization · WH Allen / Knopf · 2023
~500 pages · by the economics & data editor of Sky News
We think we live in a weightless digital age. We have never dug up more stuff.

3 / 10 →
The hook
In 2017, humanity dug more material out of the earth than in all of history before 1950.
Conway’s starting provocation: even as our lives feel ever more digital and “weightless,” the twenty-first century rests on a vast, invisible mountain of physical stuff — and almost no one in the knowledge economy can tell you where it comes from.
4 / 10 →
The core argument
Six raw materials quietly built the modern world — and the battle to control them will decide our future.
Conway follows sand, salt, iron, copper, oil and lithium from the mine to the device in your hand. These fundamental materials, he argues, “have created empires, razed civilizations and fed our ingenuity and our greed for thousands of years.” Without them, the modern world simply would not exist.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 5
Sand: the foundation hiding in plain sight
Sand forms the foundations of the modern world — it is the silicon substrate from which we make glass, concrete and even the silicon chips of yesterday’s swipe. The least glamorous material on the list turns out to be the bedrock of cities and computing.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
Salt: the molecule life can’t do without
Salt is the chemical without which life on this planet couldn’t exist — and it still provides the backbone for much of the modern chemical and pharmaceutical world. From road de-icing to the building blocks of medicines, the humble crystal is everywhere in the supply chain.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
Iron: the skeleton of the built world
Iron is the skeleton of everything we build — the material from which we make steel, and therefore the bones of bridges, buildings, railways and machines. Conway treats it as the physical scaffolding of industrial civilization itself.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
Copper: wiring the green transition
Copper forms the electrical networks of the world — and it matters more than ever as we steam towards a green-energy transition that runs on electricity. Every wind turbine, EV and grid upgrade is, at bottom, a demand for more copper.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
Lithium: the new oil, the new choke point
Lithium and copper serve the same role in the emerging energy economy that coal and oil served before. And control has become a matter of national security: China’s dominance in lithium processing, Conway notes, is a modern echo of British coal supremacy or American oil dominance in the 20th century.
10 / 10 ✓
