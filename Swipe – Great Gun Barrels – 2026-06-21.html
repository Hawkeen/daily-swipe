Great Gun Barrels
ChinaTalk — "Paul Kennedy on Great Powers" · Jordan Schneider with Paul Kennedy · 10 June 2026
The Yale historian of The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers on Anglo-German antagonism and the China echo

The hook
In 1903, a German politician asked the Reichstag the question that still hangs over US–China.
Eduard Bernstein, on Anglo-German trade: "When do we consider the position of one land to another in the manner of jealous, acquisitive tribes, which if one robs, the other is robbed, and when do we consider it from the standpoint of a peaceful exchange of nations?" Eleven years later, his audience picked the first answer.
3 / 10 →
The core argument
The question is not whether a rising power threatens the incumbent — but which rising power they choose to fear.
Britain in 1900 was being outgrown by two rising industrial powers: Germany and the United States. The "sane and intelligent decision-makers in Whitehall" decided to befriend one and contain the other. Kennedy's interest is in the variables that tipped the choice: geography, common language, ideological similarity, naval posture. The China echoes, he says, are obvious — but the comparison is not exact.

Finding 1 / 5
3,000 miles vs. 15 hours' steam
Kennedy's first variable is distance. The United States was a rising great power on the other side of the Atlantic — 3,000 miles away, with no live border dispute by 1903. The Imperial German fleet was "15 hours steaming across the North Sea in the middle of the night."
"Is there ever a time, is there ever a place," Kennedy asks Schneider, "where two very large powers with two very large armies or navies or air forces have felt comfortable with another power so close?"
5 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
Language, ideology, and the Anglo elite
The other variables Kennedy walks Schneider through: a common language tied British elites and American politicians (Teddy Roosevelt onward) into "a longstanding English cultural world"; and ideology — a constitutional democracy vs. an autocratic Wilhelmine Germany run by a Kaiser who personally directed armies and navies.
"It was relatively easy for the British to see the rise of the United States as not a threat" — even though both were eating into British market share.
6 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
"Great gun barrels pointing at England"
Around 1908, the newspaper magnate Lord Northcliffe toured the Krupp steelworks in the Ruhrgebiet. Kennedy quotes Northcliffe's letter back to a colleague verbatim in the interview:
"Every one of those great chimneys pointing up into the sky are essentially great gun barrels pointing at England."
It is, Kennedy notes, an "extraordinary quotation if you think about it" — the instant translation of industrial capability into military intent. That mental move, he argues, is the engine of every Great Power transition that ends badly.
7 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
When trade interdependence flipped from virtue to vulnerability
From the 1860s the Cobden–Mill view held that interdependence prevented war. Britain shipped coal to Portugal, Portugal shipped port wine back. By 1914 the mood had inverted: "I don't like the idea of being reliant upon those German magnetos, I'd rather produce them myself just in case."
Kennedy notes that the 1944 planners of Bretton Woods, the IMF, the World Bank and the GATT-era trade system explicitly tried to re-anchor the Cobden view of interdependence as the foundation of peace. The current US move toward self-sufficiency and supply-chain control is, in that frame, the same psychological reversal of 1908 — just with semiconductors instead of magnetos.
8 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
The Bismarck concession — and why China today isn't 1914 Germany
Kennedy's counterfactual: imagine the Kaiser had said, "I understand the neuralgia of the number one as we become more and more a success story. I am going to be superbly clever here, and I'm going to temper down the shape and the size of the imperial German navy." That kind of self-restraint required "inordinate intelligence, which only, I think, Bismarck could have or show."
On China today: even with a much larger PLA Navy, "the vastness of the Pacific still helps to temper or relativize the size and nature of that threat" — which is why Kennedy resists a mechanical 1914 analogy.

Why this matters in 2026
Tariff truce, chip control, and a 188-entity blacklist
May 2026: the US and China agreed to lower tariffs (US down to 30% on Chinese goods; China to 10% on US goods). January 2026: NVIDIA permitted to sell its H200 AI chip into China, but capped at 50% of US customer volumes and with third-party testing per shipment.
June 2026: the US Department of Defense added 188 new entities to the List of Chinese Military Companies — including Alibaba, Baidu, BYD, COSCO and China Mobile. The "industrial-as-military" reading of a rival's commerce is back; Northcliffe at Krupp is the pattern.

The line that stays
"The more dependent you are upon others and the more dependent they are upon you, the more likely you are going to sit down and resolve any quarrels you have around a table in an amicable fashion."
— Paul Kennedy, paraphrasing Cobden, ChinaTalk 10 June 2026
