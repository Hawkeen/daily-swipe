Dancing by Chance

Dance · Merce Cunningham, the choreographer who handed his decisions to coins, dice and the I Ching — and changed modern dance for good

USA · 1919–2009 · ~200 dances, 800+ Events · ~6 min read

The Hook

What if the choreographer’s most important move is a coin toss?

Cunningham shook dice, tossed coins and consulted the I Ching to decide which movement came next — often his dancers learned the order of a piece only on the night of the performance. It looked like an abdication of artistic responsibility. He saw it as the opposite: contact with forces larger than his own mind.

The Core Idea

Chance operations: free the dance from your own likes and dislikes.

The method came from his lifelong partner, composer John Cage, who used the I Ching’s 64 hexagrams and repeated coin tosses to decide what sound came next. Cunningham took it into dance: instead of asking what he thought should follow a movement, he asked what chance suggested — and discovered works, he said, that could never have arisen from traditional collaboration.

Finding 1 / 5 · 1953

Suite by Chance: charts and a coin

Suite by Chance (1953) was his first work made entirely through chance procedures: charts listed elements of space, time and position, and a coin toss determined each one. Later, Canfield (1969) assigned each movement to a playing card drawn at random.

1953 was also the year he formed the Merce Cunningham Dance Company at Black Mountain College — with dancers including Paul Taylor and Carolyn Brown, and musicians John Cage and David Tudor.

Finding 2 / 5 · The rupture

Dance and music, deliberately divorced

With Cage, Cunningham famously asserted that dance and its music should not be intentionally coordinated — each was made independently and met only in performance.

He also discarded narrative and traditional form: no story, no beginning, middle or end — “non-representational” choreography that simply emphasises movement, breaking with both ballet and his teacher Martha Graham, in whose company he had danced as a soloist from 1939.

Finding 3 / 5 · The stage

No front, no centre, no soloist’s spot

In Cunningham’s works the “front and centre” spot traditionally coveted by soloists no longer exists. Dance can happen on any part of the stage, viewed from any angle; the audience must choose among many centres of activity.

He saw randomness and arbitrariness as positive qualities — because they exist in real life.

Finding 4 / 5 · The machine

From the I Ching to the computer

Chance made him fearless about technology. From 1991 he choreographed with the software LifeForms (later DanceForms); motion-capture experiments with digital artists Paul Kaiser and Shelley Eshkar produced Hand-drawn Spaces (SIGGRAPH, 1998) and the stage work BIPED (1999).

His collaborators ran from Robert Rauschenberg (resident designer 1954–64) and Andy Warhol — whose Silver Clouds floated through RainForest (1968) — to Radiohead and Sigur Rós, who scored Split Sides (2003).

Finding 5 / 5 · The legacy

A company planned its own ending

Cunningham presented his last work, Nearly Ninety, at 90 in April 2009, and died that July. His Legacy Plan — the first of its kind in dance — sent the company on a final world tour and then closed it: the last performance was on 31 December 2011 at New York’s Park Avenue Armory, with his works passing to the Merce Cunningham Trust.

The conversation continues: the exhibition Five Friends: John Cage, Merce Cunningham, Jasper Johns, Robert Rauschenberg, Cy Twombly ran at Museum Brandhorst in Munich and closed at Museum Ludwig in Cologne in January 2026.
