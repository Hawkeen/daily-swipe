Book of the day · 📖

Nexus

A Brief History of Information Networks from the Stone Age to AI

Yuval Noah Harari · Fern Press / Random House · 2024 · ~6 min read

Swipe →

5 / 14 →

The hook

If information were the same as truth, more of it would make us wise. It hasn’t.

Humanity has never had so much information — and yet we teeter toward ecological collapse, war and self-destruction. Harari asks: if we are so wise, why do we keep acting so self-destructively? His answer starts with a mistake about what information actually is.

6 / 14 →

The core idea

Information networks — not ideas or individuals — shape the fate of societies.

From clay tablets and the Bible to newspapers and algorithms, the systems through which humans store and share information decide how power is organised. We are now transferring control of those networks to an alien, non-human intelligence — without understanding what it will do with them.

Finding 1 / 6 · The naive view

The Silicon Valley error

Harari names a “naive view of information”, common in tech circles: that information equals truth, and that more information automatically yields more knowledge and wisdom. This optimism, he argues, is precisely what leaves us unprepared for what powerful information technology can do.

8 / 14 →
Finding 2 / 6 · What information does

Connection, not representation

In Harari’s telling, information’s primary role in history is not to represent a pre-existing reality but to connect — to tie together disparate things, “whether couples or empires.” Most information isn’t true; its job is to create new realities by binding people into networks.

Finding 3 / 6 · The cautionary tale

The witch-hunt as a network

His central warning is the European witch-hunts. Between the 15th and 18th centuries a sophisticated information network — theologians, lawyers, judges, printed manuals and professional witch-hunters — manufactured an entire false reality that tortured and killed tens of thousands. A network can spread lies at scale as efficiently as truth.

10 / 14 →
Finding 4 / 6 · Order vs. truth

Every network trades truth for order

Information networks must balance two goals: finding the truth and maintaining order. Too much order — Stalinism, Nazism — and the network enforces powerful myths while crushing self-correction. The healthiest systems, Harari argues, keep strong mechanisms for catching and fixing their own mistakes.

11 / 14 →
Finding 5 / 6 · The new members

AI is not a tool — it’s an agent

Earlier technologies — the printing press, the radio — still needed humans to decide what to print or broadcast. AI is different: it can generate ideas, forge stories and make decisions by itself. For the first time, our information networks are gaining members that are neither human nor under human control.

12 / 14 →
Finding 6 / 6 · The debate

Sweeping — and contested

Reviewers admired how a historian who thinks in millennia managed to “capture the zeitgeist,” calling the framing strikingly original. Critics counter that the vast scope flattens detail and that the analogies between witch-hunts, Stalinism and AI are looser than they look. Provocation, not proof, is the book’s mode.

13 / 14 →

The takeaway

“Information has no essential link to truth.”

— Yuval Noah Harari, Nexus

If information mostly connects rather than reveals, then the flood of it won’t automatically make us wiser — and the machines we’re now inviting into the network don’t care about truth at all. The task, Harari says, is to build networks that can still correct themselves.
