Hyperscaler Debt Is Now a Rates Story

The AI boom is increasingly built on borrowed money — so Big Tech’s buildout now rides on interest rates

Odd Lots · Bloomberg · Joe Weisenthal & Tracy Alloway · July 2026 · ~5 min read

Swipe →

5 / 14 →

The hook

For years, AI was paid for with profits. Now it’s paid for with bonds.

The AI boom is increasingly built on debt — and investor demand is starting to plunge just as the hyperscalers ramp up their bond blitz. What was a cash-flow story has quietly become a credit story.

6 / 14 →

The core idea

When capex outruns cash flow, the buildout becomes a bet on interest rates.

Aggregate capex for the big five, after buybacks and dividends, now exceeds projected cash flows — so they lean on debt markets to bridge the gap. Once you fund AI with bonds, the cost and availability of that debt (i.e. rates) drives whether the whole thing pencils out.

7 / 14 →
Finding 1 / 5 · The scale

$600 billion in a single year

Big Five hyperscaler capex surged to about $602 billion in 2026 — a 36% jump year-over-year, with roughly 75% of it tied to AI. This is one of the largest private investment waves in corporate history.

8 / 14 →
Finding 2 / 5 · The gap

Even $1 trillion in cash isn’t enough

Even with more than $1 trillion in combined cash, the four biggest AI spenders can no longer cover their capital spending from cash flow alone. The unspoken contract with investors — that AI would be self-funded — has broken.

9 / 14 →
Finding 3 / 5 · The debt wave

AI debt has doubled

Big Tech’s AI-related debt has doubled to roughly $350 billion, and forecasts point to around $570 billion of global AI-related debt issuance in 2026. The bond market, not the income statement, is now doing the heavy lifting.

10 / 14 →
Finding 4 / 5 · The casualty

Buybacks go quiet

Share buybacks have quietly become the casualty. In the first quarter, only Microsoft bought back stock — at $3.4 billion, its smallest buyback in nearly a decade. Cash that once went to shareholders now goes into data centres.

11 / 14 →
Finding 5 / 5 · The pushback

Bond buyers start to balk

Amazon’s $25 billion bond offering on 7 July was the softest hyperscaler launch since Meta’s $30 billion sale in October 2025. The order-book “cover ratio” plunged from nearly 5× in February 2026 to below 2× in July — a sign investors may demand wider spreads to keep absorbing supply.

12 / 14 →
Why this matters in 2026

The whole market is watching the cover ratio

In mid-July 2026, reporting warned that AI-related debt was heading toward $570 billion even as investor demand softened. If a rate move or a weak bond auction reprices AI credit, the effects ripple far past Big Tech — which is exactly why it’s now “a rates story.”

13 / 14 →

The takeaway

“Hyperscaler debt is now a rates story.”

— Odd Lots, Bloomberg (title of the July 2026 episode)

The AI trade used to be about chips and models. Increasingly it’s about balance sheets: who will lend the hyperscalers the next half-trillion dollars, and at what price.
