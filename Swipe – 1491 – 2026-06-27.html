Book of the day · 📖
1491
New Revelations of the Americas Before Columbus
Charles C. Mann · Alfred A. Knopf · 2005
The hemisphere Columbus “discovered” was already full.
Swipe →
3 / 9 →
The hook
In 1491, there were probably more people living in the Americas than in Europe.
Mann’s book gathers a generation of new research to argue that almost everything most of us learned about the pre-Columbian Americas — empty, primitive, untouched — is wrong.
4 / 9 →
The core argument
Three textbook ideas about the pre-Columbian Americas are wrong.
Mann sets out to debunk three widely held beliefs: that the continents were sparsely populated, that social and technical development was limited, and that the inhabitants left the environment untouched. The evidence, he argues, points the other way on all three.
5 / 9 →
Finding 1 / 4 · Population
The “high counters” were closer to right
Mann sides with the scholars who estimate large pre-Columbian populations. Numbers, he argues, were probably much higher than traditionally assumed — in 1491 there were likely more people in the Americas than in Europe.
6 / 9 →
Finding 2 / 4 · Cities
Tenochtitlán out-classed Europe’s capitals
Unlike any European capital of the time, the Aztec capital Tenochtitlán had running water, beautiful botanical gardens and immaculately clean streets. Civilisation and settlement ran wider and deeper than the “primitive” cliché allows.
7 / 9 →
Finding 3 / 4 · Arrival
They got here earlier — and in waves
Humans probably reached the Americas earlier than the textbook story says, over several waves of migration — not solely across the Bering land bridge in one relatively short crossing.
8 / 9 →
Finding 4 / 4 · Land
A continent that was already landscaped
Far from leaving the land pristine, Native Americans transformed it so thoroughly that Europeans arrived in a hemisphere already massively “landscaped” by human beings — a worked, managed environment, not a wilderness.
9 / 9 ✓
The takeaway
“Indians were here far longer than previously thought… and in much greater numbers.”
— Charles C. Mann, 1491
