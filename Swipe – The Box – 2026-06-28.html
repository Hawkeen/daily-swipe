Book of the day · 📖
The Box
How the shipping container made the world smaller and the world economy bigger
Marc Levinson
Princeton UP · 2006, 2nd ed. 2016 · ~540 pp · ~5 min read
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The Hook
Globalisation wasn’t built by treaties. It was built by a metal box.
Levinson’s provocation: the modern era of world trade was made possible not by politicians cutting tariffs and quotas, but by an unglamorous steel container. One critic called it the real-world equivalent of the internet revolution — hiding in plain sight on every dock and motorway.
4 / 10 →
The Big Idea
Make shipping cheap, and you reshape the whole world economy.
The book tells the dramatic story of the container’s creation, a decade of struggle before it was adopted, and the sweeping consequences of the sharp fall in transport costs it brought. Once moving goods became almost free, industry could put factories far from its customers — and the geography of the global economy changed for good.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 5 · The first voyage
April 1956: 58 boxes on an old oil tanker
Modern containerisation began when trucking entrepreneur Malcolm McLean had fifty-eight containers loaded onto a refitted oil tanker that sailed from Newark, New Jersey, to Houston. From that modest beginning grew a huge industry that made the boom in global trade possible.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5 · A decade of struggle
The box didn’t just happen
Adoption took huge sums from investors and ambitious ports, years of high-stakes bargaining with labour titans Harry Bridges and Teddy Gleason, and delicate negotiations on standards — so that almost any container could travel on any truck, train or ship. The plain box was, in fact, a breathtaking systems innovation.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5 · Proof of concept
Vietnam convinced the world
For years the container was an impractical-seeming idea. Ultimately it took McLean’s success in supplying U.S. forces in Vietnam to persuade the world of its potential — a classic tale of trial, error and creative destruction.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5 · Winners and losers
It rewrote economic geography
Drawing on neglected sources, Levinson shows how the container devastated traditional ports such as New York and London while fuelling the growth of previously obscure ones such as Oakland. Whole cities rose or fell on whether they embraced the box.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5 · The workshop of the world
Cheap boxes made Asia the world’s factory
By making shipping so cheap that industry could locate factories far from its customers, the container paved the way for Asia to become the world’s workshop — and brought consumers a previously unimaginable variety of low-cost goods from around the globe.
10 / 10 ✓
The Takeaway
“Without the container, there would be no globalization.” — The Economist, on Levinson’s case for the humble steel box.
