The hook
“I sometimes think I died, and this is heaven.”
After serious illness, Emin says her studio is her second life. The retrospective takes its title from that feeling — an exhibition reviewers describe as being, above all, about facing the end: her own mortality, and ours.
4 / 9 →
What the show argues
The most personal art can be the most universal.
For 40 years Emin has made work out of her own life — love, loss, the body, survival — with an unflinching honesty critics call “unapologetically human.” A Second Life argues that this raw self-exposure isn't navel-gazing but a way of holding up society's greatest ills, and life's hardest facts, for everyone to feel.

Finding 1 / 3
Forty years, every medium
The show brings together more than 100 works spanning painting, video, textile, neon, sculpture and installation — career-defining sensations alongside pieces never exhibited before. It is a full traverse of a practice that has refused to stay in any one form.

Finding 2 / 3
The turn to mortality
The exhibition's emotional centre is its reckoning with death. Reviewers describe a show that “throws a spotlight on the artist's unapologetically human approach and raw engagement with what it means to be alive” — work that moves between despair, anger, hope and even amusement as it stares at the end.

Finding 3 / 3
The reviews are not hedging
Critics have been unusually unanimous, calling it “Tate Modern's best exhibition in years” and “art as it should be: passionate, real, and moving.” For an artist once dismissed as a tabloid provocateur, A Second Life reads as a full critical vindication.

Why this matters in 2026
A summer of big retrospectives — and a deadline
2026 is a year dominated by large-scale surveys of established figures, and A Second Life is among the season's biggest — running at Tate Modern until 31 August 2026. It sits alongside Anish Kapoor at the Hayward in a London summer unusually crowded with major shows.
