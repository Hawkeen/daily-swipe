The hook
“AI is indeed a classic market bubble.”
Six words from an 87-year-old who has spotted three of the last four crashes: Japan in 1989, dot-com in 2000, US housing in 2008. Now he’s back on Bloomberg with a new memoir titled, with characteristic dry humour, The Making of a Permabear. The signs he watches for, he says, are flashing again.
3 / 10 →
The core argument
Every revolutionary technology — railways, electricity, the internet, AI — arrives with a real future and a fake price tag.
Grantham’s six-decade pattern: the tech is genuinely transformative; the early capital deployment is wildly excessive; the gap between expectation and cash flow becomes untenable; the correction is brutal. AI is “genuinely transformative” and “pricing in a utopian financial scenario.” Both can be true at once — that’s precisely what a bubble is.

Finding 1 / 5
Why Grantham gets a hearing at all
Co-founder of GMO, a Boston asset manager with a six-decade record of calling bubbles. He flagged the Japanese equity bubble of the late 1980s, refused to buy into dot-com mania in the late 1990s (clients left in droves), and warned about US housing before 2008.
In 2024 he declared a “superbubble” across multiple asset classes. The new memoir — co-written with financial historian Edward Chancellor — is his attempt to bottle the pattern recognition.
5 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
2000 wasn’t about bad ideas. It was about good ideas at impossible prices.
Grantham’s point about the dot-com crash: the internet really did remake commerce, media, and labour — just not at 1999 prices, and not in 1999. Fibre optic was over-built; capacity sat unused for half a decade; survivors got rich on the wreckage.
His argument on the show: same script for AI. The technology is real. The capex into data centres is staggering. The gap is between “eventually massive” and “massive in 2026.”
6 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
Grantham reaches further back: 19th-century railway mania
In the 1840s, British investors poured savings into hundreds of railway IPOs. The trains, of course, would change everything — and they did. But many lines were duplicated, many companies bankrupt, many investors wiped out, even as the surviving network became the spine of the Victorian economy.
His pattern, repeated three times on the show: genuinely transformative + speculative overreach = brutal correction. Pick any of the three, the other two follow.

Finding 4 / 5
The four symptoms he’s watching for — and seeing
As reported in coverage of the episode, Grantham’s dashboard for late-stage bubble:
• Retail euphoria in highly-leveraged instruments — zero-day options, leveraged single-stock ETFs.
• Narrowing breadth: the index is held up by a handful of mega-cap names while the median stock declines.
• Divergence from bonds: equities pricing in rate cuts the bond market refuses to confirm.
• The “new paradigm” narrative: pundits insisting historical valuation rules no longer apply.
8 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
Big Tech, he says, has just reached “a watershed change”
The episode summary itself foregrounds it: Grantham believes we’re past the peak of the bubble’s respectability phase. The concentration is the tell — capital piling into a handful of names whose valuations have “detached entirely from historical price-to-earnings ratios.”
His framing: the underlying technology survives. The current price level may not. Two questions, two answers.

Why now
The week the episode aired, two numbers landed that should make any investor pause
Tracy and Joe open the recording (16 June) with a headline: SpaceX, fresh off the largest IPO in history at a $1.8 trillion valuation, briefly overtook Microsoft at its intraday high; over three trading sessions its cap pushed past Amazon to roughly $2.65 trillion.
Meanwhile Nvidia’s market cap sits near $5.11 trillion as of 19 June. Two of Grantham’s four symptoms — concentration in a handful of mega-caps, a “new paradigm” narrative around AI — are unambiguously in evidence.
