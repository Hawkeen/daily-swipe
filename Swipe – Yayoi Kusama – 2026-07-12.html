The Hook
As a child she hallucinated dots and flowers engulfing her body. She has spent ninety years painting her way out — and into — that vision.
Yayoi Kusama (*1929, Matsumoto) is one of the most renowned artists of our time — her polka dots, pumpkins and Infinity Mirror Rooms appear millions of times over on social media. Cologne’s Museum Ludwig is marking its fiftieth anniversary with a retrospective of her entire oeuvre.
6 / 13 →
The Core Idea
Self-obliteration: the dot as a way of dissolving the self into the universe.
Kusama’s practice revolves around nature in constant transformation — becoming and decay — and the boundlessness of a universe in which everything that exists eventually perishes. The polka dot pattern covering objects and people expresses that worldview as much as her mirror rooms do. As she put it in 1998: “You know, if there’s a cat, I obliterate it by putting polka dot stickers on it. … And I obliterated myself by putting the same polka-dot stickers on myself.”

Chapter 1 / 5 · The escape
Out of Matsumoto, into New York
Kusama grew up in the confines of rural, patriarchal postwar Japan and fled to New York in the late 1950s — landing amid Flower Power and the anti–Vietnam War protests. Using art as activism, she made headlines with provocative happenings, like the “Anatomic Explosion” on Brooklyn Bridge in August 1968, where dot-painted performers held a banner reading “Kusama. Self-Obliteration”.
8 / 13 →
Chapter 2 / 5 · The return
Working through anxiety, in paint and prose
Returning to Japan in 1973, Kusama began to work through her existential anxieties — often in visceral novels and poems, a side of her work the exhibition deliberately shows. Her powerful mature output includes the vibrant, richly coloured painting cycles such as “My Eternal Soul” (2009–21). For Kusama, art is the expression of a life — microcosm and macrocosm revealed through the personal.

Chapter 3 / 5 · Cologne only
Boats, black light and rooftop flowers
The Cologne venue shows large-scale installations absent from the show’s Basel edition: Kusama’s very first installation, “Aggregation: One Thousand Boats Show” (1963); “I’m Here but Nothing” (2000/2026), a living room bathed in black light and countless fluorescent dots; and the monumental painted bronze “Flowers That Speak All about My Heart Given to the Sky” on the museum’s roof terrace, facing Cologne Cathedral.

Chapter 4 / 5 · The infinite
An Infinity Mirror Room fills the largest hall
An immersive installation developed especially for the show — with an integrated Infinity Mirrored Room from 2025, its polka-dot lights reflected endlessly — occupies the museum’s biggest space. Kusama’s cosmology in one sentence, from a much-quoted statement: “Our earth is only one polka dot among a million stars in the cosmos. Polka dots are a way to infinity.”

Chapter 5 / 5 · The Cologne connection
Kusama has been in this collection since 1976
Museum Ludwig owns Kusama’s “Compulsion Furniture” (1966), a gift of the Ludwig collection in 1976 — making this retrospective a homecoming of sorts for the museum’s fiftieth birthday. The show is organised with the Fondation Beyeler and the Stedelijk Museum Amsterdam, with a catalog published by Hatje Cantz.

Why this matters in 2026
Three weeks left
The Cologne exhibition closes on 2 August 2026 — after that, the retrospective travels to the Stedelijk Museum Amsterdam (12 September 2026 – 17 January 2027), in a different configuration without the Cologne-only installations. For anyone within reach of the Rhineland, this is the closing window on the most comprehensive Kusama show Germany has seen.

The Takeaway
“In my more than seventy years as an artist, I have always been in awe of the wonder of life. More than anything, this strong sense of the life force in artistic expression is what has supported me and gave me power to overcome feelings of depression, hopelessness and sadness.” — Yayoi Kusama
