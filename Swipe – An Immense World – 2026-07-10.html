Book of the day · 📖
An Immense World
How Animal Senses Reveal the Hidden Realms Around Us
Ed Yong · science writer, Pulitzer Prize winner
2022 · ~6 min read
Swipe →
5 / 12 →
The Hook
You are not seeing the world. You are seeing your world.
A tick tracks warmth and butyric acid. A songbird reads the Earth’s magnetic field. A mole “sees” with its nose. Each creature is sealed inside its own perceptual bubble — and mistakes that bubble for reality itself.
6 / 12 →
The Core Idea
One planet, countless worlds.
Yong’s organising idea is the Umwelt — a term the zoologist Jakob von Uexküll coined in 1909 for the slice of the world an animal can actually sense. No creature perceives everything; each taps a different fraction of reality. To understand animals, we have to stop framing their lives in terms of our senses and step into theirs.

Finding 1 / 5 · The sensory bubble
Every animal edits reality
The book moves sense by sense — surface vibrations, smell and taste, light, colour, pain, heat, contact and flow, sound, echoes, electric and magnetic fields. Each animal’s Umwelt keeps what matters to it and ignores the rest. Our own feels total and natural, but it is just one bubble among millions.

Finding 2 / 5 · Selective attention
The frog embryo that ignores an earthquake
Some frog embryos hatch in a hurry if a snake arrives with a hard bite — yet ignore rain, wind, footsteps, even an earthquake. Perception isn’t about detecting everything; it’s about detecting the right thing. An Umwelt is as much a filter as a window.
9 / 12 →
Finding 3 / 5 · Senses we don’t have
Reading the electric world
Whole senses lie outside human experience. Strongly electric fish — electric eels can discharge up to about 860 volts — stun prey and defend themselves, while sharks and other hunters passively detect the faint electric fields that every animal in water gives off. To them, hiding is almost impossible.

Finding 4 / 5 · Touch as vision
The mole that hunts by feel
Image: US National Park Service / Wikimedia Commons. Public domain.
The star-nosed mole’s nose carries 22 fleshy tentacles studded with more than 25,000 Eimer’s organs. It touches whatever it meets about a dozen times a second and can detect and swallow prey in as little as 120 milliseconds — effectively “seeing” the dark with its skin.
11 / 12 →
Finding 5 / 5 · The human footprint
We are drowning out other worlds
The final chapters turn to sensory pollution: our light and noise are scrambling animal Umwelten — disorienting migrating birds, sea turtles and insects. Having spent the book expanding our sense of other minds, Yong asks us to dim the lights and quiet the noise so those minds can keep working.

The Takeaway
“Every animal can only tap into a small fraction of reality’s fullness. Each is enclosed within its own unique sensory bubble, perceiving but a tiny sliver of an immense world.” — Ed Yong
