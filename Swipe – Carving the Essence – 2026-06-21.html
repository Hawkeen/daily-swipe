The hook
In 1928, a New York court had to decide whether a 4-foot piece of polished bronze was a bird — or a kitchen utensil.
U.S. Customs had classified Brâncuși's Bird in Space under "Kitchen Utensils and Hospital Supplies" and levied a 40% duty on it. One customs witness told the New York Evening Post: "If that's art, hereafter I'm a bricklayer." The case ended up rewriting what counted as sculpture in the eyes of the law.

The core argument
Brâncuși taught modern sculpture how to subtract. Everything since runs through him.
Wikipedia, the Centre Pompidou, and the Neue Nationalgalerie all reach for the same phrase: the patriarch of modern sculpture. He took the bird and removed wings, feathers, beak, and feet — leaving only motion. He took the kiss and removed embrace, faces, bodies — leaving only the seam. The retrospective in Berlin is an argument about how radical that subtraction was, and how much of contemporary art is downstream of it.

Finding 1 / 6
Hobiţa, Bucharest, Paris — and never back
Born 19 February 1876 in the Romanian village of Hobiţa, Constantin Brâncuși trained at the Bucharest School of Fine Arts. In 1904 a Romanian Ministry of Public Instruction grant took him to Paris, where he enrolled in the studio of Antonin Mercié at the École des Beaux-Arts.
He stayed for the rest of his life — became a French citizen in 1952 specifically so he could leave his Paris studio and its contents to the French state. He died in Paris on 16 March 1957, age 81.

Finding 2 / 6
1907 — the turn to direct carving
Around 1907, Brâncuși made a decisive break: he abandoned modelling (the soft clay-and-cast tradition Rodin had perfected) in favour of direct carving — working the stone or the wood itself, leaving the marks of the tool, treating the material as a partner rather than an intermediate.
This is the move that anchors his whole oeuvre. The Berlin show traces it through the central motifs he then spent fifty years reducing further: The Kiss, Sleeping Muse, Mademoiselle Pogany, Endless Column, and the bird series.

Finding 3 / 6
The same bird, sixteen times, over two decades
The Bird in Space (originally Pasărea în văzduh) series began in 1923. By 1940 Brâncuși had created the form over fourteen times: seven in marble, nine in polished bronze. The piece has no wings, no feathers, no head you would recognise — just an "elongated body, and the head and beak reduced to a slanted oval plane" (the Met's description). The point was the movement, not the bird.
In 2005, one of these birds set the then-record auction price for any sculpture: $27.5 million. The Berlin retrospective includes Bird in Space as one of its anchor works.

Finding 4 / 6
Brâncuși v. United States (1928)
October 1926: nineteen Brâncuși sculptures arrive in New York harbour aboard the steamship Paris, intended for the Brummer Gallery. Customs refuses to classify Bird in Space as art and imposes a 40% duty (~$230 at the time, ≈ $3,100 today). Edward Steichen, who had bought the bird, files an appeal funded by Gertrude Vanderbilt Whitney.
In November 1928, Judges Young and Waite rule for the artist. Waite's decision: the bird "is beautiful and symmetrical in outline... it is the original production of a professional sculptor and is in fact a piece of sculpture and a work of art." It is the first court decision in U.S. history to accept non-representational sculpture as art.

Finding 5 / 6
The Endless Column — geometry that wants to go on forever
In 1918 Brâncuși carved the first version of the Endless Column from oak: a succession of truncated pyramids stacked into one another to form a rhythmic geometry that suggests the possibility of infinite expansion.
It is the move he keeps making across motifs — finding the unit that, when repeated, becomes potentially infinite. The Berlin retrospective foregrounds this serial logic, partly through the partial reconstruction of his studio, where works were arranged and rearranged together as what he called groupe mobiles.

Finding 6 / 6
The studio left to a country, shown outside Paris for the first time
When Brâncuși died in 1957 he bequeathed his entire Paris studio — sculptures, tools, plinths, photographs — to the French state, on condition that it be preserved as a single unit. It became part of the Musée National d'Art Moderne (Centre Pompidou).
The Berlin exhibition is the first time a partial reconstruction of that studio has been shown outside Paris since the 1957 bequest. The objects, in other words, are out of their permanent room for the first time in nearly seventy years.

Why this matters in 2026
Seven weeks left, and then the studio goes back in its room
The show closes 9 August 2026. From today (21 June 2026), that is roughly seven weekends. Over 100,000 people have already been through it; weekend slots are getting tight.
