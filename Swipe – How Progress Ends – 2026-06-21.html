The hook
Computing. The internet. AI. And productivity growth is still stagnating.
That is the gap Mark Leonard opens the episode with. The headlines describe a world transformed by computation. The official statistics describe a productivity slowdown that has been running for the better part of two decades. Frey's whole project is to take that gap seriously instead of explaining it away.
3 / 10 →
The core argument
Progress is neither inevitable nor guaranteed. For most of human history, stagnation was the norm.
Frey's case, as presented in the episode and the underlying Princeton book: technological breakthroughs are necessary but not sufficient. What turns a breakthrough into society-wide progress is the institutions, incentives and political conditions that let a country adapt to the new thing and scale it. When those fail, even the cleverest economy stalls — and that, statistically and historically, is the more common outcome.

Finding 1 / 5
A graveyard of leading technological powers
Frey's historical case list — the points his book and the conversation keep returning to — reads like a roll call of confident empires:
Song China: gunpowder, the compass, movable type — then a stalled trajectory. The Dutch Republic: 17th-century shipping and finance powerhouse, eclipsed in the 18th. Victorian Britain: the workshop of the world, overtaken by 1900. The Soviet Union: a brief surge of state-directed catch-up, then collapse. Japan: rapid post-war growth, then thirty stagnant years. The pattern is what Frey is interested in — not the moments of bloom, but the failures to keep adapting.

Finding 2 / 5
"The Great Flattening"
Frey's diagnosis of the present: globalisation and digital technologies have concentrated innovation in a few very large firms, leading to a slowdown in productivity and creativity. He calls it The Great Flattening.
The mechanism: decentralisation is what fosters the exploration of new technologies, while bureaucratic scale is what is needed to scale them. When everything tilts toward one side, the system can keep producing impressive demos and shipping mediocre productivity numbers — at the same time.
6 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
China's open-weight gamble vs. American AI leadership
The episode explores why China's embrace of open-weight AI models could challenge American technological leadership. Open weights spread the frontier downward — to universities, smaller firms, foreign developers — rather than locking it inside three or four closed labs.
In Frey's frame, this is a real-time experiment in decentralised exploration being run against concentrated scaling. It is the kind of structural choice that historically separates the empires that keep adapting from the ones that quietly stop.

Finding 4 / 5
Why Europe keeps losing the digital race
Mark Leonard pushes Frey specifically on Europe: why does a continent that is still genuinely competitive in manufacturing keep struggling in digital industries?
Frey's answer points back to institutions — capital markets, labour mobility, the fragmentation of the single market, the regulatory weight on early-stage scaling — rather than to any single villain. The point that lands hardest in an ECFR audience: Europe's manufacturing strength does not automatically translate into digital strength. That assumption is itself the bug.
8 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
Progress lives in the balance between innovation and implementation
The LSE Review of Books summary of Frey's argument is the clearest one-line version: progress is in the balance between innovation and implementation. Either alone is sterile.
An economy that only invents and never industrialises (think: many late-stage academic systems) loses the gains. One that only scales and stops exploring (think: late Soviet planning, or — increasingly — hyper-concentrated digital platforms) calcifies. The policy task is to keep both channels open at once. Most countries, most of the time, do not.
9 / 10 →
Why this matters in 2026
$700 billion of AI capex into a stagnant productivity number
In 2026 the five largest US hyperscalers are committing roughly $660–690 billion of capital expenditure on AI infrastructure (Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, Oracle), with some Wall Street estimates pushing the number toward $800–900 billion. Goldman Sachs projects $1.6 trillion annual AI capex by 2031.
Frey's question is the one staring back from the spreadsheets: will any of this show up in measured productivity, or are we lining up another chapter for the historical roll call? The 30 minutes with Leonard is the most compressed version of that argument you can listen to.
