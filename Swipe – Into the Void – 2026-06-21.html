Into the Void

The hook
What does it look like when an object absorbs 99.965% of all visible light?
Like a shape-shaped hole in reality. The Hayward show is the largest UK gathering of Kapoor's Vantablack works yet — mysterious objects coated in what is, by absorption, the blackest known substance in the world, mystifying viewers "with their extraordinary light-absorbing properties." Critics call the result "work that moves, frightens and stuns" (The Guardian, ★★★★★).
3 / 10 →
The core argument
Kapoor's sculpture is about what isn't there — the void, the mirror, the inflated emptiness.
Three families of work fill the Hayward: flawless steel mirror sculptures that warp the viewer back at themselves; Vantablack voids that look like holes; and seemingly depthless openings set into floors and walls "drawing us in with a thrilling sense of vertigo." Across all three, the subject is perception — what the eye does when its usual cues for depth, scale, and edge are removed.

Finding 1 / 5
Mumbai, Doon, Hornsey, Chelsea
Sir Anish Mikhail Kapoor CBE RA was born in Mumbai on 12 March 1954 — Indian Punjabi Hindu father (a hydrographer in the Indian Navy), Iraqi Jewish mother. After The Doon School he moved to the UK to train at Hornsey College of Art, then Chelsea School of Art and Design.
He has stayed in Britain since. The Hayward staged his first major UK survey almost 30 years ago, in 1998. The 2026 show is the return.

Finding 2 / 5
The Bean, the Orbit, Leviathan
Kapoor's big public objects are the work most people have walked past or under. Cloud Gate (2006) in Chicago's Millennium Park — 33 × 66 × 42 feet, 168 stainless steel plates, 110 short tons, no visible seams. Inspired by liquid mercury, locally nicknamed The Bean. Sky Mirror at Rockefeller Center (2006) and Kensington Gardens (2010).
Leviathan at the Grand Palais (2011). ArcelorMittal Orbit in London's Olympic Park (2012). The Hayward show puts the iconic series alongside new work where you can see the line of thought running through them.

Finding 3 / 5
Vantablack: not paint, a process
In interviews Kapoor is careful about what Vantablack actually is. "This black material, the blackest material in the universe, isn't a paint. So it's not just a thing you paint on. It's a highly technical, complicated physical process… I've been working with it for nearly ten years now and in ten years I've only made a few objects."
On what the coating does to a sculpture: "the Vantablack version is more about introspection, about becoming disoriented, lost, and enveloped in an overwhelming void of nothingness." The Hayward stages this in dedicated low-lit rooms.

Finding 4 / 5
A six-metre inflatable, monumental reds, suggested blood and entrails
At the heart of the show are three monumental works, each filling an entire section. Visitors first enter "a gallery completely transformed by an atmospheric new commission: an inflatable installation that fills the six-metre-high space, challenging our sense of scale and self."
A pair of new monumental installations appear in Kapoor's signature red. The Hayward's own content warning is unusually frank: "This exhibition contains artworks suggestive of blood and entrails." Wallpaper* and The Telegraph both single out the "visceral" recent paintings.

Finding 5 / 5
Turner Prize, Padma Bhushan, knighthood, Genesis Prize
Kapoor's recognition stack reads like a survey of every kind of art establishment: Premio Duemila at the 44th Venice Biennale (1990), the Turner Prize (1991), the Unilever Commission at Tate Modern's Turbine Hall, the Padma Bhushan from India (2012), a knighthood in the 2013 Birthday Honours, an honorary doctorate from Oxford (2014), and the 2017 Genesis Prize.
Notably, the Genesis Prize was given partly "for his many years of advocacy for refugees and displaced people" — a thread that runs through his public posture as much as the studio work.

Why this matters in 2026
Open now · 18 weeks · Rugoff's last act
The show opened Tue 16 June 2026 and runs through Sun 18 October 2026 — eighteen weeks. Tickets are £22 (members free). The Telegraph, Independent and Guardian have all filed early reviews (★★★★, ★★★★, ★★★★★). The Art Newspaper interviewed Kapoor in conjunction with the opening on 15 June.
It is also Ralph Rugoff's final exhibition as Director of the Hayward Gallery after 20 years, the centrepiece of the Southbank Centre's 75th anniversary programme, and the largest Kapoor survey in the UK in nearly three decades. None of those things converge again.
