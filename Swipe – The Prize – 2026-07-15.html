Book of the day · 📖

The Prize

The Epic Quest for Oil, Money, and Power — the definitive history of the substance that made the modern world

Daniel Yergin · Simon & Schuster, 1990 · 912 pages · Pulitzer Prize 1992 · ~6 min read

The Hook

We are all, whether we notice or not, citizens of the age of “Hydrocarbon Man”.

That is Yergin’s name for our era — and his book is its biography. Published in December 1990, four months after Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait and one month before the Gulf War began, The Prize arrived just as the world was violently reminded what it runs on. It became a bestseller, then a Pulitzer winner, and has been called the “definitive” history of oil — even a “bible”.

6 / 12 →

The Core Thesis

Oil — and every struggle over its discovery, acquisition and distribution — created the modern world.

Yergin builds the case along three threads: the relationship of oil to the rise of modern capitalism; the intertwining of oil, politics and international power; and the relationship between oil and society in the age of “Hydrocarbon Man”. The canvas runs from the first well drilled in 1850s Pennsylvania, through two world wars, to Iraq’s invasion of Kuwait and Operation Desert Storm.

Finding 1 / 4 · The title

Churchill’s gamble gave the book its name

As First Lord of the Admiralty before the First World War, Winston Churchill argued for converting British warships from Welsh coal — safe in Britain’s own mines — to fuel oil, which had to be carried by sea from distant countries, in peace or war.

Committing the Navy irrevocably to oil, Churchill admitted, was to “take arms against a sea of troubles”. But if the risks could be surmounted: better ships, better crews, more intense forms of war power — “mastery itself was the prize of the venture.”

Finding 2 / 4 · The cast

Wildcatters and rogues, Churchill and Ibn Saud

This is history told through people: oil tycoons and wildcat drillers alongside statesmen — Winston Churchill and Ibn Saud, George Bush and Saddam Hussein.

The through-line is that oil decided the twentieth century’s conflicts, its economic systems and its balance of power — from the rise of Standard Oil to the OPEC era and the tanker wars.

Finding 3 / 4 · The craft

Ten years, 700 sources, 80 interviews

The book was ten years in the making. Its bibliography lists some 700 books, articles and dissertations, sixty government documents, four oil-company archives (Amoco, Chevron, Gulf, Royal Dutch Shell) — and 80 personal interviews with key figures such as James Schlesinger and Armand Hammer.

Library Journal’s verdict: enough insight to satisfy the scholar, enough drama and colourful personality to capture everyone else — and “though lengthy, the book never drags”.

Finding 4 / 4 · The afterlife

A Pulitzer, a PBS epic — and a 2024 epilogue

In 1992 The Prize won the Pulitzer Prize for General Nonfiction; it has been translated into fourteen languages. In 1992–93 it became an eight-hour PBS documentary narrated by Donald Sutherland, seen by a reported 20 million Americans.

Yergin continued the story in the 2011 sequel The Quest. In 2024 an unabridged audiobook appeared with a new epilogue narrated by the author — reflecting on the book’s relevance more than 30 years on.

Why this matters in 2026

This month’s oil market is a chapter Yergin could have written.

After the US–Iran war disrupted the Strait of Hormuz, the IEA’s July 2026 report shows global supply rebounding by a sharp 4.1 mb/d in June to 98.8 mb/d as Gulf flows resumed — yet output remains some 9.4 mb/d below pre-war levels.

Brent hit a four-year high above $126 in late April, plunged to ~$68–72 by early July — then jumped back towards $77–79 when the ceasefire was breached on 7–8 July. Meanwhile seven OPEC+ countries agreed to add another 188,000 b/d in August, the fifth straight monthly increase. Oil, politics and power: still one story.
