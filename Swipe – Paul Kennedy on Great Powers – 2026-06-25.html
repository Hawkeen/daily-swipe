Episode of the day · 🎧
Paul Kennedy on Great Powers
ChinaTalk · host Jordan Schneider · with historian Paul Kennedy · 10 June 2026
The author of The Rise and Fall of the Great Powers on power transitions, then and now.
Swipe →
3 / 9 →
The hook
Are nations “jealous, acquisitive tribes” where one’s gain is another’s loss — or partners in “a peaceful exchange”?
Schneider opens with a German socialist’s question from a 1903 Reichstag debate. Kennedy’s whole career is an answer: which view wins, and when, decides whether great powers trade or fight.
4 / 9 →
The core thesis
Great-power standing rests on relative economic strength — and the system has no referee.
Since the nation-state emerged around 1500, states have operated in what Kennedy calls an anarchic international system. As their relative shares of wealth and production shift, rising and declining powers must each decide whether the other’s rise is a threat — or an opportunity.
5 / 9 →
Finding 1 / 4
“Anarchy” doesn’t mean chaos
Kennedy is precise about the word: an anarchic system “doesn’t necessarily mean everyone slaughtering everyone else, but simply the absence of a higher authority controlling global affairs.” Roughly 190 nation-states interact with no world government above them — and that structural fact is why understanding the great-power system matters.
6 / 9 →
Finding 2 / 4
Same problem, opposite responses
Around 1900, two rising powers — the United States and Imperial Germany — were both eroding Britain’s relative share. Yet Britain drew ever closer to America while growing ever more suspicious of Germany. Why would the same sane decision-makers in Whitehall befriend one rival and fear the other?
7 / 9 →
Finding 3 / 4
Geography, language, ideology
Kennedy’s three answers: geography — a rival 3,000 miles across the Atlantic worries you less than one “15 hours steaming across the North Sea”; common language and a shared Anglo culture among American elites; and ideology — a democratic America versus an autocratic Germany. Distance and kinship made the American rise feel safe.
8 / 9 →
Finding 4 / 4
Perception, not just power
Whether a rising power is read as threat or partner turns on “ideology and cultural assumptions” as much as raw capability. That’s why one of the episode’s headline themes is a warning against the lazy analogy: China today is not Germany of the late 19th and early 20th centuries.
9 / 9 ✓
The takeaway
“‘Anarchy’ here doesn’t necessarily mean everyone slaughtering everyone else, but simply the absence of a higher authority controlling global affairs.”
— Paul Kennedy, on ChinaTalk
