A Radical Vision for Israelis and Palestinians
A peace plan that keeps two states — but tears down the wall between them
The Ezra Klein Show · 7 July 2026 · with Rula Hardal & May Pundak · ~6 min read
Swipe →
5 / 11 →
The Hook
After decades of dead-end talks, what if the border itself is the problem?
The old debate pits “two states” against “one state.” Klein’s guests reject the choice. Their initiative, A Land for All, keeps two sovereign states — but lets people move freely across the line between them.
6 / 11 →
The Core Idea
Neither separation nor unification.
A Land for All proposes a confederation: two independent states, Israel and Palestine, bound by open borders. It rests on holding two truths at once — that both peoples have a claim on all the land, and that both want to control their own political destiny.
7 / 11 →
Finding 1 / 4 · The frame
Two states, one homeland
The plan establishes two sovereign states — two heads of state, two governments — along a border based on the pre-1967 “Green Line.” It is emphatically not a single binational state, and not a clean partition either. Sovereignty stays double; the homeland becomes shared.
8 / 11 →
Finding 2 / 4 · The border
A line you can cross
Freedom of movement and residence is the default. Citizens of each state could tour, work, study or live across the border — a Palestinian in Tel Aviv, an Israeli settler as a resident of Palestine. The Green Line marks sovereignty, not a wall.
9 / 11 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The hardest knot
Jerusalem as an open city
Rather than split the city, the plan makes Jerusalem an open city under shared sovereignty — capital of both states, run by a joint municipal body drawn from its eastern and western boroughs, with each national group holding a veto on major issues.
10 / 11 →
Finding 4 / 4 · The messengers
A Palestinian and an Israeli, together
The plan is carried by its two co-directors: Rula Hardal, a Palestinian citizen of Israel with a political-science doctorate from Hannover, and May Pundak, an Israeli lawyer and activist. They joined Klein to argue why this design might succeed where so much else has failed.

The Takeaway
“A theory of peace based on neither separation nor unification.” — A Land for All
