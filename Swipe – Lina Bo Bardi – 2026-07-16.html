Lina Bo Bardi

Architecture · The Italian-Brazilian who floated a museum above São Paulo — and built for the people who’d never entered one

Rome 1914 – São Paulo 1992 · MASP, SESC Pompéia, the glass easels · ~6 min read

Swipe →

The Hook

She lifted a whole museum eight metres into the air — so the ground could stay a public square.

The plot on Avenida Paulista came with a condition: the view of downtown São Paulo had to be preserved. Bo Bardi’s answer was radical — suspend the museum’s main body from four concrete pillars, leaving 74 metres of free span underneath, the largest in the world at the time. The void beneath MASP became one of the city’s great civic spaces.

The Core Idea

Architecture is a social issue before it is an aesthetic one.

Born in Rome in 1914, Bo Bardi moved to Brazil after World War II with her husband, the critic and dealer Pietro Maria Bardi, to build MASP’s collection and home. Her stated position: “Architecture and architectural freedom are above all a social issue that must be seen from inside a political structure, not from outside it.” Her buildings were designed to serve the people and the city — not just the cultural elite.

Finding 1 / 4 · 1968

MASP: the floating museum

Constructed between 1956 and 1968 with engineer José Carlos Figueiredo Ferraz, the building hangs from four pillars connected by two huge concrete beams, 8 metres above the ground. It was inaugurated on 7 November 1968 — by Queen Elizabeth II, during her visit to Brazil.

Bo Bardi described her materials plainly — visible concrete, whitewash, tempered glass, black rubber floors — and her aim more plainly still: “I didn’t search for beauty. I’ve searched for freedom.”

Finding 2 / 4 · The display

Paintings on glass: the crystal easels

For MASP’s picture gallery she invented a legendary display device: a thick tempered-glass sheet set into a concrete base, with the painting hung on the transparent panel — like a canvas on a painter’s easel. Visitors walk among the works and choose their own path; no wall dictates the order of art history.

The museum abandoned the easels in the late 1990s — just as foreign institutions began to notice them. They have since been restored to MASP, and were shown at the Venice Art Biennale in 2024.

Finding 3 / 4 · 1982

SESC Pompéia: a factory kept alive

Asked to build a leisure centre on the site of an old barrel factory, she refused to erase the industrial past. She preserved the raw concrete sheds and inserted libraries, theatres, workshops and cafés into their robust shells — a multi-functional building for the local community, inaugurated in 1982.

The complex became the model for a whole idea of culture: not a temple you visit, but a place where a neighbourhood lives.

Finding 4 / 4 · The legacy

The misunderstood modernist, rediscovered

Long overshadowed in the modernist canon, Bo Bardi is now celebrated for exactly what once made her hard to place: a blend of modernism and brutalism put to radically inclusive social use.

MASP itself keeps growing around her: in March 2025 the museum opened the 14-storey Pietro Maria Bardi tower next door, linked underground to her 1968 building — doubling the exhibition space while leaving the floating museum untouched.
