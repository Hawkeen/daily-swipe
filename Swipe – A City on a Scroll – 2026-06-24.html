Creative topic · 🎨
A City on a Scroll
Zhang Zeduan — Along the River During the Qingming Festival · ink & colour handscroll · Northern Song dynasty, 12th century
Unroll five metres of painted silk and a whole city comes to life.
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The hook
In a single hand-painted scroll, just 25 cm tall, there are 814 people — a whole capital city caught mid-bustle, 900 years ago.
No single vantage point, no frozen instant. As you unroll it, you walk through the city: the countryside, the river, the great bridge, the crowded streets — an entire society moving at once.
4 / 10 →
Why it matters
It’s less a landscape than a census in ink — a panoramic snapshot of everyday life under the Song.
The painting reveals the lifestyle of every level of society, rich and poor, and the economic life of both countryside and city. Its value goes far beyond the artistic: it is an essential document for understanding daily life and culture in the Song dynasty.

Finding 1 / 5
A picture you read, not just see
Image: Zhang Zeduan (detail), public domain, via Wikimedia Commons.
It’s a handscroll — about 25.5 cm tall but roughly 5.25 metres long. You don’t hang it on a wall; you unroll an arm’s width at a time, right to left, so the city reveals itself like a slow journey rather than a single framed view.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
The headcount
Scholars have actually counted what’s in it: about 814 humans, 28 boats, 60 animals, 30 buildings, 20 vehicles, 8 sedan chairs and 170 trees. Merchants, boatmen, officials, fortune-tellers and porters all share one continuous scene — a teeming social inventory.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
The city is Bianjing
The setting is Bianjing — today’s Kaifeng — the dazzling capital of the Northern Song and one of the largest cities on Earth at the time. The scroll is our clearest window onto its streets, shops and river commerce before the dynasty fell.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
What does “Qingming” even mean?
Scholars still argue over the title. “Qingming” may name the spring Qingming Festival — or it may simply mean “peace and order.” Tellingly, the scroll shows a bustling, prosperous street scene, not the festival’s solemn tomb-sweeping rites — which is why some read it as a portrait of a city at peace.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
A national treasure — and the only one
It is the only known surviving masterpiece by Zhang Zeduan, classified as a Chinese National Treasure and held in the Palace Museum, Beijing. So beloved that it was copied and reimagined for centuries — including an elaborate 18th-century Qing court version.
10 / 10 ✓
The takeaway
Nine hundred years before the camera, one painter froze an entire living city — 814 people deep — into five metres of silk you read like a film.
