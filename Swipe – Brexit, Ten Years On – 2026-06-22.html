The hook
There was no crash. There was a slow leak.
Ten years after the 2016 referendum, the case Tooze and Abadi lay out is that Brexit didn't sink the UK in one dramatic moment. It made the economy smaller than it would otherwise have been through a gradual, cumulative drag on trade, investment and productivity — a cost that keeps compounding rather than announcing itself.

The core of the episode
Two data points, one decade.
The show's format is simple: each week Tooze and Abadi pick a couple of numbers that explain the world. As the tenth anniversary of the Brexit vote approaches, the data they reach for tell a consistent story — the costs landed exactly where economists expected, just larger and slower than the headlines of 2016 implied.
5 / 9 →
Finding 1 / 3
An economy 6–8% smaller
By the end of 2025, estimates put Brexit's hit to UK GDP at roughly 6% to 8% — a figure that has accumulated steadily rather than arriving all at once. Bloomberg Economics has framed the annual drag as somewhere from £100 billion to over £200 billion. The cost was larger than many analysts predicted before the vote.
6 / 9 →
Finding 2 / 3
Goods stumbled; services surprised
The drag isn't uniform. UK goods exports are estimated around 10–15% lower than they would otherwise have been, underperforming the rest of the G7. But services told a different story: resilient through the decade, with the UK among the fastest-growing services exporters in the G7. The thing that was supposed to suffer most held up best.
7 / 9 →
Finding 3 / 3
The quiet costs: investment and certainty
Beyond trade, the estimates point to investment reduced by roughly 12–13%, with employment and productivity each down around 3–4%. A recurring culprit is uncertainty itself: prolonged policy ambiguity is judged to have added importantly to the size of the hit — a tax paid in postponed decisions.

Why this matters in 2026
An anniversary, paired with D-Day
June 2026 marks ten years since the referendum — the moment to ask whether the predictions held. In the same episode, Tooze and Abadi pair Brexit with the anniversary of the Allied invasion of Normandy: two very different June reckonings with Britain's relationship to the continent, one military, one economic.
