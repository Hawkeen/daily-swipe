The Hook
What if the most optimistic building is the one that looks temporary and half-finished?
Radić’s buildings can seem unstable, provisional, deliberately unresolved — and that is the point. The 2026 Pritzker jury saw in exactly that quality something structured, optimistic and quietly joyful: shelter that admits uncertainty instead of pretending to master it.

The Core Idea
Architecture at the crossroads of uncertainty and memory
In March 2026 Radić became the 55th Pritzker laureate — architecture’s highest honour. The jury placed his work at the crossroads of uncertainty, material experimentation and cultural memory: raw stones, translucent shells and fragile forms that feel found rather than imposed.

Finding 1 / 5 · The maker
A Santiago architect, mostly working at home
Born in Santiago in 1965, Radić earned his degree from the Pontifical Catholic University of Chile in 1989 and opened his own firm in 1995. After winning a civic competition in Concepción in 2000, he was named the best Chilean architect under 35 — a career built largely in and from Chile.

Finding 2 / 5 · The breakthrough
A translucent shell resting on quarry stones
His 2014 Serpentine Pavilion in London — a semi-translucent, cylindrical fibreglass shell balanced on large quarry stones, covering 350 square metres — thrust him onto the global stage. Its roots lay in his “Castle of the Selfish Giant” (after Oscar Wilde) and Restaurant Mestizo, itself part-supported by boulders.

Finding 3 / 5 · The range
From museums to a charcoal burner’s hut
His work spans public buildings (the Museo Chileno de Arte Precolombino, Restaurant Mestizo, the Vik winery) and houses (Copper House 2, the Pite House, the House for the Poem of the Right Angle) down to tiny, seemingly fragile structures like the extension to a charcoal burner’s house.

Finding 4 / 5 · The verdict
Unfinished on purpose — yet reassuring
The jury noted that his buildings appear temporary, unstable or deliberately unfinished, yet they provide structured, optimistic and quietly joyful shelter. It is a paradox he leans into: architecture that looks provisional but holds you steady.

Finding 5 / 5 · The lineage
The fifth Latin American to win
Radić is the fifth Latin American architect to take the Pritzker in its 47-year history. He follows a recent run of very different laureates — Liu Jiakun (2025), Riken Yamamoto (2024) and David Chipperfield (2023) — extending the prize’s reach beyond the usual centres of the profession.

Why this matters in 2026
The award is brand new
The 2026 Pritzker was announced on 12 March 2026, naming Radić “the artist of unspoken architecture.” It is the freshest verdict on where the profession is looking — away from monumental certainty, toward the provisional, the hand-made and the site-specific. A good moment to discover his work before the retrospectives arrive.
