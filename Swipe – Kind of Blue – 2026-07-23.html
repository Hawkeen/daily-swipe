Kind of Blue

Miles Davis threw out the chords — and made the best-selling jazz record of all time

Miles Davis · Columbia Records · released 17 August 1959 · ~6 min read

Swipe →

5 / 13 →

The hook

Take away the chords, and jazz found “a new language of music.”

Bebop, the dominant jazz of the day, was built entirely on fast-moving chord changes. Kind of Blue did almost the opposite — and the pianist Chick Corea credited its style with creating a whole new musical language.

6 / 13 →

The core idea

Improvise on scales, not chords — simple frameworks, maximum freedom.

Following the modal experiments of Milestones (1958), Davis based Kind of Blue entirely on modality — improvising over a few scales rather than a rushing sequence of chords. Fewer constraints on the harmony meant more room for melody and mood.

Finding 1 / 5 · The theory

George Russell’s way out of bebop

In 1953 George Russell published the Lydian Chromatic Concept of Tonal Organization, moving improvisation away from chords and the traditional major and minor scales. Davis, tiring of the “manic style of bop,” found in Russell’s modal ideas a way out.

Finding 2 / 5 · The sessions

Two days in a converted church

The album was recorded on 2 March and 22 April 1959 at Columbia’s 30th Street Studio in New York, and released on 17 August 1959. It is the record on which Davis abandoned hard bop and committed fully to modality.

Finding 3 / 5 · The method

Frameworks, not scores

In his liner notes, pianist Bill Evans wrote that Davis handed the group frameworks “exquisite in their simplicity” — sketches that gave the players just enough to improvise on, with sure reference to a primary conception. Much of the album was captured with little rehearsal.

Finding 4 / 5 · “So What”

The sound of a new language

The style of “So What” and the album’s other pieces has been credited with creating, in Chick Corea’s words, “a new language of music” — groundbreaking precisely because the reigning form, bebop, was entirely based on chord changes.

Finding 5 / 5 · The reach

From the Doors to Steve Reich

The influence spilled far beyond jazz. Beginning with the Byrds, the Doors, Carlos Santana and the Allman Brothers, most rock improvisation became modal, and Davis’s 1959 leap helped set the stage for minimalist composers like Steve Reich and Philip Glass — reaching, it’s said, from Pink Floyd to Kendrick Lamar.

Why this matters in 2026

Still selling, freshly remastered

Sixty-seven years on, Kind of Blue remains the best-selling album in jazz history — and on 7 May 2026 a newly remastered edition appeared, remastered at Sony Music Studios Tokyo. A record made in two 1959 afternoons keeps finding new ears.
