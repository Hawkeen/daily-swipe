The hook
“Illiberalism is winning. What has surprised me is how weak liberalism has felt in response.”
Ezra Klein opens by admitting that he — “a professional liberal” — can’t even tell you what liberalism’s vision is, or who its leaders are. So he’s been reading the canon. Rosenblatt’s book stopped him cold: for almost 2,000 years before the word “liberalism” existed, there was a virtue called liberality — and we’ve quietly forgotten what it meant.
3 / 10 →
The core argument
Liberalism began as a moral creed, not a rights creed. The individualist version we argue about today is a 20th-century invention.
Rosenblatt: it “wasn’t just about a constitutional form; it wasn’t just about individual rights. It was actually more about moral development and about certain character development that they felt was so very important — and that a good constitution should promote.”
Early liberals “believed in the power of religion to reform society, emphasized the sanctity of the family, and never spoke of rights without speaking of duties.”

Finding 1 / 5
“Liber” meant free — but also generous
In ancient Rome, the noun liberalitas was an attitude expected of the citizen: devotion to the commonwealth, gift-giving, reciprocity. Cicero, Seneca, John Locke, John Donne and George Washington all wrote about it as a virtue.
Klein: for almost 2,000 years it meant “demonstrating the virtues of a citizen, showing devotion to the common good and respecting the importance of mutual connectedness.” A liberal arts education existed to cultivate that virtue — not to land you a job.
5 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
The word “liberalism” was coined in 1811 — as an insult
“Liberalism” appeared around 1811–1812, in the wake of the French Revolution. It was “first a pejorative,” coined by the enemies of the liberals — often written with an accent (libéral) to mark it as something foreign and dangerous.
Catholic counter-revolutionaries called liberals selfish, anarchist, sexually deviant. For most of the 19th century, the most powerful enemy of liberalism in Europe was the Roman Catholic Church. (Rosenblatt notes today’s post-liberals are reviving exactly that vocabulary.)
6 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
The Cold War stripped out the duties
Rosenblatt: after fascism and Communism, any state interested in “forming citizens” started to sound terrifying. So liberals retreated: “We are not about the state forming citizens. We are about individual rights — about property rights, in particular.”
That’s when liberalism became an Anglo-American creed of individualism — and the older language of generosity, character and shared obligation “dropped out of our conversation.”
7 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
Constitutions to stop the next Napoleon
Benjamin Constant and the early liberals had cheered Napoleon as the saviour of the revolution — then watched him become a despot who “used wars to divert attention” and “lined the pockets of his friends.” The shock produced what we now call constitutional liberalism: separations of power, free press, free religion.
Their second lesson: institutions alone aren’t enough. “Without an educated, critically minded, alert citizenry, the people will fall prey to unscrupulous actors and demagogues.”
8 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
Tolerance wasn’t niceness — it was a wager on progress
The early liberals were mostly Protestants — Mme. de Staël, Benjamin Constant — and they wanted toleration because they thought religions left to compete in a free market would purify each other. Adam Smith argued the case in The Wealth of Nations.
Rosenblatt: this “optimism is a really important, central aspect of liberalism: if you accept this tolerance, progress will be the result — people will improve, society will improve.” Today, she admits, “that seems so naïve.”
9 / 10 →
Why now
For the first time in 50 years, the US no longer counts as a liberal democracy
The V-Dem Institute’s 2026 Democracy Report downgraded the United States from “liberal democracy” to “electoral democracy” — the first time in over 50 years. Six of the ten newly “autocratizing” countries this year are in Europe and North America.
The report: “The speed with which American democracy is currently dismantled is unprecedented in modern history.” Rule of law is deteriorating in 22 countries, including the US. Klein’s opening line — “illiberalism is winning” — is now a measurable fact.
