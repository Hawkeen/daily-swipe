Rare Earths: What is To Be Done?
How Beijing weaponised the elements the modern economy can’t run without
ChinaTalk · with Farrell Gregory & Joris Teer, hosted by Aqib Zakaria
June 2026 · ~5 min read
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The Hook
One country refines about 90% of the world’s rare earths. That isn’t a market — it’s a lever.
Rare earths aren’t actually rare in the ground. The chokehold is in processing: China controls over 90% of global refining and chemical separation capacity, and roughly 91% of separation output — far above its share of mining.
4 / 10 →
The Core Idea
A dominance turned into a tool of economic coercion.
The episode’s argument: Beijing turned its grip on rare-earth processing and magnets into leverage — and the West is struggling to respond because subsidies alone cannot conjure an entire refining and magnet-making ecosystem overnight.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 4 · Where the grip is
Not the mine — the refinery
China’s dominance is greatest in the separation and refining stages, not in digging ore. Its position rests on processing know-how, tolerance for the pollution refining creates, huge economies of scale, and an integrated magnet-manufacturing chain that rivals have not matched.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 4 · Focus the list
The ~25 minerals that actually matter
Guest Farrell Gregory argues policymakers spread themselves too thin across sprawling “critical mineral” lists. The sharper move: concentrate on the roughly 25 materials China can genuinely weaponise, rather than treating everything as equally urgent.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 4 · Why cash isn’t enough
Subsidies alone won’t break the hold
Because China can flood markets and lean on overcapacity, a Western plant can be undercut the moment prices fall. Without coordinated action — buyers, floors, allies moving together — new supply chains struggle to survive their first price war.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 4 · The weapon fired
Export controls, on and off
In April 2025 China imposed rare-earth and magnet export controls; a “Busan pause” later suspended the October 2025 measures for a year — until 10 November 2026 — while preserving the April controls and the licensing machinery. The lever stays cocked even when it isn’t being pulled.
9 / 10 →
Why this matters in 2026
The squeeze is being felt now
S&P Global expects rare-earth supply bottlenecks to persist through 2026, with premiums outside China rising. CSIS warns that Beijing’s new magnet restrictions directly threaten US defence supply chains — magnets go into everything from F-35s to guided missiles.
