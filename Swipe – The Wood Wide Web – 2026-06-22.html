The hook
In one small patch of woodland, a single tree was found linked to 47 others.
The links are fungal threads. More than ninety percent of plants depend on mycorrhizal fungi, which can wire trees into shared underground networks researchers nicknamed the “wood wide web.” The term was coined in the late 1990s — just as the internet's World Wide Web was being mapped.
4 / 9 →
The core argument
Fungi don't sit between the categories. They dissolve them.
Sheldrake's argument is that fungi throw our concepts of individuality and even intelligence into question. They digest the world where it lies and then absorb it into their bodies; they link plants in underground networks; they can change our minds, heal our bodies and even help remediate environmental disaster. Once you take fungi seriously, he writes, the neat boundary between “one organism” and “another” starts to blur.

Finding 1 / 4
The trees are already on a network
The threads that spread a fungus are called mycelium — a branching, interconnecting web that entangles plant roots, other fungi and food sources. Through it, trees can be linked into shared mycorrhizal networks: in one studied patch of woodland, a single tree was connected to 47 others.
The “wood wide web” coinage of the late 1990s landed exactly as the human internet was being built — and the metaphor has shaped how we picture forests ever since.
6 / 9 →
Finding 2 / 4
Mycelium is a verb, not a noun
Sheldrake asks us to think of mycelium not as a thing but as a process — “an exploratory, irregular tendency,” an “anarchic filigree” that is at once like a stomach, a skin and a sensory organ, and like none of them.
A mycelial network, he writes, is “a map of a fungus's recent history” — and a reminder that all life-forms are, in the end, processes rather than things.

Finding 3 / 4
“We are all lichens”
A lichen is not one organism but a partnership — a fungus living with an alga or a cyanobacterium. Sheldrake cites a landmark paper on the symbiotic view of life that puts it bluntly: “There have never been individuals. We are all lichens.”
Once you accept that, the human body — host to its own trillions of microbes — looks less like a single self and more like a negotiated colony.
8 / 9 →
Finding 4 / 4
They eat what we cannot throw away
Because fungi digest the world chemically, some can break down materials we struggle to dispose of. Sheldrake describes the harnessing of fungi that can digest plastic, explosives, pesticides and crude oil in a wave of break-through technologies.
It is the practical edge of the book's bigger claim: that fungi can heal our bodies and help remediate environmental disaster — and are already transforming how we understand whole ecosystems.
