Book of the day · 📖

The Man Who Solved the Market

How Jim Simons launched the quant revolution — and built the most profitable trading machine ever, without ever reading an earnings report

Gregory Zuckerman · Portfolio / Penguin, 2019 · ~6 min read

5 / 13 →

The hook

The greatest money-maker in modern financial history wasn’t a Wall Street trader. He was a codebreaker and a geometer.

Zuckerman argues that no other investor — not Warren Buffett, Peter Lynch, Ray Dalio, Steve Cohen or George Soros — can touch Jim Simons’s record. And Simons got there by ignoring almost everything Wall Street believed mattered.

6 / 13 →

The core idea

Markets hide faint, repeating patterns — and mathematics, not intuition, can find them.

Simons insisted on a single, monolithic trading system, run not by bankers but by mathematicians, physicists and computer scientists who sifted enormous datasets for recurring anomalies. Fundamentals, gut feel and star stock-pickers were beside the point.

Finding 1 / 6 · Three lives

Geometer, codebreaker, then trader

Simons had a three-phase career. As a world-class mathematician he co-developed the Chern–Simons theory and chaired the maths department at Stony Brook. He then broke codes for the Institute for Defense Analyses in the Cold War — honing the pattern-recognition that would define his third act. Only in 1978, at 40, did he leave academia to start Renaissance Technologies.

8 / 13 →
Finding 2 / 6 · Medallion

66% a year — a record no one has matched

The Medallion Fund, launched in 1988, has posted roughly 66% annualised returns before fees — numbers never replicated by any other fund. Since the early 1990s it has been closed to outside investors: access is restricted to Renaissance’s own employees.

9 / 13 →
Finding 3 / 6 · Not fundamentals

They never asked what a company was worth

Where Buffett bought undervalued businesses, Renaissance began with trend-following — buying what was rising, selling what was falling — and evolved into hunting statistical anomalies across markets. The team was built from scientists, not Wall Street veterans, precisely so no one would be tempted to “understand” the companies.

10 / 13 →
Finding 4 / 6 · The edge

Trading human reaction, not value

Asked where the profits really came from, Simons pointed not to companies but to people:

“We make money from the reactions people have to price moves.”

11 / 13 →
Finding 5 / 6 · Humility in the machine

Trust the model — but never too much

The 1998 collapse of Long-Term Capital Management reinforced an existing mantra at Renaissance: never place too much trust in a trading model, because all formulas are fallible. The system was king, but it was watched with suspicion.

12 / 13 →
Finding 6 / 6 · The test of a signal

“Does this correspond to reasonable behaviour?”

For all the machine-learning mystique, Simons applied a sanity check to the patterns his models found. As he put it, the team would ask whether a signal “corresponds to some aspect of behavior that seems reasonable” — a reminder that even the purest quant was hunting for real human footprints in the data.

The takeaway

“We ask, ‘Does this correspond to some aspect of behavior that seems reasonable?’”

— Jim Simons, in Gregory Zuckerman’s The Man Who Solved the Market
