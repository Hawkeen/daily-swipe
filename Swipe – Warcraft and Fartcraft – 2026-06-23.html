The hook
A title that sounds like a joke is the diagnosis: war, plus a lot of hot air.
In Every's framing, the present world economy is being shaped by two forces that the macro chartbook doesn't price well: kinetic conflict in the Middle East, and economic statecraft dressed up as financial innovation. The episode is the show's attempt to take both seriously at once.
4 / 10 →
The core argument
Geopolitics is no longer a side story for markets — it's the chart.
The episode connects three threads: Iran (a hot war and an oil price), China (the other end of any Iran outcome), and a Washington push to convert dollar stablecoins into a tool of US foreign policy. Every and Johnston argue the inflation outlook now hinges less on the Fed than on who controls the barrels and the rails.

Finding 1 / 4
Iran is now the macro variable
The show notes describe the episode as covering “all things Iran from geopolitics to inflation outlook to what it means for China.” The conversation treats the Iran question as one input that propagates through oil, shipping, and currency markets simultaneously.
Rory Johnston is on as the oil specialist — author of the Commodity Context newsletter — to keep the crude side of the story honest.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 4
Stablecoins as economic statecraft
The other half of the episode treats President Trump and Treasury Secretary Bessent's stablecoin statecraft ambitions as a serious macro story. Every has been making this case in print all year — his MacroVoices appearance the week before was titled, in full, “USD Stablecoins in The Age of Economic Statecraft.”
The thesis in shorthand: a dollar-pegged stablecoin used widely abroad is a way to extend dollar reach without minting a single new Federal Reserve note.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 4
The China angle: same coin, other side
Every's framing is explicitly Sinocentric: any answer in Tehran feeds an outcome in Beijing. Iran sanctions tighten or loosen China's energy import bill; a USD-stablecoin rail shifts the cost of routing payments outside the SWIFT-and-CHIPS world Beijing has been trying to exit.
In other words, Iran policy and dollar-stablecoin policy are two surfaces of the same US-China contest, not two unrelated stories.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 4
Inflation isn't only a Fed story anymore
The standard inflation script runs through the Fed, the labour market, and services prices. The episode argues that energy and weaponised payments have re-entered the model — and that you can no longer write an inflation outlook for the second half of 2026 without pricing both Iran's barrels and the dollar's rails.
That's the diagnosis hidden in the silly title: warcraft sets the price of oil; fartcraft is the inflated rhetoric of US economic statecraft. Both feed the CPI.
9 / 10 →
Why now
The intent declaration of 17 June
Less than two weeks after this episode, on 17 June 2026, the US and Iran signed a 14-point declaration of intent to end the war (Pakistani PM Shehbaz Sharif mediating). The conversation in MacroVoices #535 set up the macro side of that outcome — what the oil curve, the dollar, and Chinese energy flows do after a ceasefire as much as before.
It's also, in retrospect, the last large MacroVoices ahead of the agreement — useful as a baseline of how the macro community was pricing things at the peak of the war.
