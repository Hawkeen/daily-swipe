Book of the day · 📖
The Alignment Problem
Machine Learning and Human Values
Brian Christian
W. W. Norton · 2020 · ~480 pp · ~6 min read
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The Hook
We build systems that do exactly what we say — and discover that’s not at all what we meant.
The “alignment problem” is the difficulty of ensuring an AI system’s goals actually match our human objectives and values.
4 / 10 →
The Big Idea
Getting machines to do what we want has become one of computing’s most urgent questions.
Drawing on interviews with the researchers building these systems, Christian shows how to make machine-learning models capture our norms, understand what we intend, and — above all — do what we actually want, rather than the literal thing we specified.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 4 · One coin
Today’s bias and tomorrow’s catastrophe are the same problem
Christian connects the dots between fixing racist algorithms now and preventing a sci-fi robot apocalypse later, arguing they are two sides of the same coin: in both cases, a system optimises something subtly different from what we truly value.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 4 · COMPAS
A flawed proxy in the courtroom
He tells the story of journalist Julia Angwin’s ProPublica investigation of COMPAS, a tool that predicts a defendant’s risk of reoffending. Trained on biased proxies such as rearrest rates, it disproportionately flagged Black defendants as higher-risk than white ones — a textbook failure of alignment.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 4 · Structure
Prophecy, Agency, Normativity
The book is built in three parts. Prophecy covers what models predict and how they go wrong; Agency covers systems that act and learn through reward; Normativity covers how to give machines a sense of human values.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 4 · Learning values
Infer values by watching behaviour
A key hope is inverse reinforcement learning — instead of telling an agent what to optimise, infer its goals from what it does. The catch: behaviour doesn’t transparently reflect values. People act irrationally, under constraints, and different value systems can produce identical behaviour.
9 / 10 →
Why this matters in 2026
Frontier models are now cheap, open and everywhere
In April 2026 China’s DeepSeek released a preview of its open-source V4 model — downloadable, runnable locally and modifiable by anyone. As powerful systems spread beyond any single lab’s control, Christian’s question — whose values, specified how? — stops being theoretical.
10 / 10 ✓
The Takeaway
“We conjure a force, autonomous but totally compliant, give it a set of instructions, then scramble like mad to stop it once we realize our instructions are imprecise or incomplete.”
— Brian Christian, The Alignment Problem
