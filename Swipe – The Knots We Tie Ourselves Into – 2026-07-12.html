Episode of the day · 🎧
The Knots We Tie Ourselves Into
Sam Harris meets Alain de Botton on the psychology of unhappiness — and why the secular world struggles with ecstasy, ritual and consolation
Making Sense #483 · Sam Harris & Alain de Botton · 1 July 2026
Swipe →
5 / 11 →
The Hook
We school children for years in maths and cloud formations — and barely an hour in shame, rage or how to love.
De Botton’s life project — The School of Life — starts from that imbalance. In this conversation, two very different secular thinkers circle the same question: where is a modern person supposed to learn how to be less unhappy?
6 / 11 →
The Core Thesis
Emotional insight is neither unnecessary nor unteachable — treating it that way is the modern world’s strangest blind spot.
De Botton — writer, psychotherapist and founder of The School of Life — argues that we are “left to find our own path around our unfeasibly complicated minds”, as if each generation should rediscover the laws of physics by themselves. Emotional education is his proposed remedy.
7 / 11 →
Finding 1 / 4 · The gap
Ecstasy and ritual, awkwardly missing
A central thread of the conversation is the secular world’s discomfort with ecstasy and ritual — the structured experiences of awe and consolation that religions once organised. Harris and de Botton also discuss psychedelics as a tool for self-discovery, and what remains of Freud’s legacy in how we understand the unconscious knots of the title.
8 / 11 →
Finding 2 / 4 · Love as a skill
Not a feeling you fall into
“The emotionally intelligent person knows that love is a skill, not a feeling,” de Botton writes, “and will require trust, vulnerability, generosity, humour, sexual understanding and selective resignation.” Unhappiness, in this view, is less a fate than a set of unexamined habits — knots that can, with work, be untied.
9 / 11 →
Finding 3 / 4 · Against meritocracy
When success becomes a verdict
The episode takes up de Botton’s case against meritocracy — the idea that if the successful deserve their success, the unsuccessful must deserve their failure — alongside what Harris’s show notes call “the cancel culture purity trap” and the psychological meaning of death. The common thread: systems that promise justice can quietly manufacture shame.
10 / 11 →
Finding 4 / 4 · The mirror
AI as the ultimate mirror
Among the more unexpected turns: de Botton’s framing of AI as “the ultimate mirror” — a technology that reflects our psychology back at us. For a psychotherapist, the interesting question is not what the machine knows, but what our conversations with it reveal about the knots we tie ourselves into.

The Takeaway
“The emotionally intelligent person knows how to hope and be grateful, while remaining steadfast before the essentially tragic structure of existence.” — Alain de Botton, The School of Life
