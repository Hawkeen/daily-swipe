The Great Wave
Katsushika Hokusai — Under the Wave off Kanagawa · colour woodblock print · c. 1831 · from Thirty-six Views of Mount Fuji
The most reproduced image in art history fits in your hands.
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The hook
It isn’t a tsunami. The towering wave is a symbol of impermanence — and sacred Mount Fuji is the tiny triangle beneath it.
Three fishing boats vanish under a claw of water. The mountain — emblem of endurance and stability — sits small and still in the distance. The whole picture is a contrast between the permanent and the fleeting.
4 / 10 →
Why it endures
A Buddhist meditation on human fragility — built from Japanese tradition, European perspective and a brand-new imported blue.
Hokusai made it around the age of seventy. The towering, claw-like wave symbolises the Buddhist idea of mujō (impermanence); the tiny boats beneath it stand for human fragility. The composition fuses traditional ukiyo-e with graphical perspective developed in Europe.

Finding 1 / 5
The spiral that hides a mountain
Image: Hokusai (detail), public domain, via Wikimedia Commons.
The cresting wave forms a great spiral in the centre, its foam-fingers reaching over the boats. Read the curve and your eye is funnelled to the small, still cone of Mount Fuji in the background — the calm centre of a violent scene.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
The secret ingredient: a blue from Berlin
That intense blue is Prussian blue — a synthetic pigment created by accident in Berlin in 1706, known in Japan as Berlin ai. Bright, lightfast and one of the most stable pigments ever made, it reached Japanese print shops cheaply only by the late 1820s — and revolutionised Japanese prints.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
Mixed, not just swapped in
The printers didn’t simply substitute the exotic new blue for traditional indigo. They mixed the two for a bold outline, and printed one pigment over the other to deepen the bright Prussian blue without dulling its hue — craft, not just chemistry.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
Not a unique painting — a print run
It was never a one-off canvas. It’s a woodblock print: scholars estimate the first-edition run at roughly a thousand impressions, of which only about a hundred first-edition copies are thought to survive today — scattered across museums in the US, UK, France, Japan and the Netherlands.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
East meets West in one frame
The image is a true hybrid: a synthesis of traditional Japanese prints and the graphical perspective developed in Europe, coloured with a European pigment. A picture often read as quintessentially “Japanese” is, at its core, a meeting of two visual worlds.
