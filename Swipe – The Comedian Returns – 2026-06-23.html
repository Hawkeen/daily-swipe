The hook
A duct-taped banana hung on the rotunda is what we now mean by Pop.
Maurizio Cattelan's Comedian (2019) — the work that sold three times in 2019 and again at auction in 2024 — joins Warhol and Lichtenstein in the Guggenheim's new survey. Pop didn't end in 1972; it learned to swallow itself and become the joke.
4 / 10 →
The core argument
Pop was never just a 1960s American story.
The show's framing is explicit: the historic works on view trace how 1960s artists turned to “advertising techniques, reproduction, and mass media,” while contemporary works by 18 living artists — many recently acquired — engage with and offer critical perspectives on the visual forms and language of popular culture. The point is the through-line, not the museum trophy.

Finding 1 / 4
The classic cast, the canonical works
Tower 5 opens with the 1960s heart of the show: quintessential works by Chryssa, Richard Hamilton, Roy Lichtenstein and Andy Warhol. The selection reads like a definition of the movement at the moment the museum was first collecting it.
Other names in this section: John Chamberlain, Jim Dine, Marta Minujín, Claes Oldenburg with Coosje van Bruggen, and Lucas Samaras.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 4
A giant shuttlecock returns
One of the show's draws is logistical: Claes Oldenburg & Coosje van Bruggen's Soft Shuttlecock (1995) is back on view in New York for the first time in 25 years. It is a literal piece of Pop's monumental scale being lowered into the rotunda.
Pair it with Yayoi Kusama's INFINITY MIRRORED ROOM – DANCING LIGHTS THAT FLEW UP TO THE UNIVERSE (2019), a major loan: two ways of asking the rotunda to absorb a single, total object.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 4
The opening is staggered, on purpose
The Guggenheim opens the show in two waves: the Pop historic rooms and Kusama's Infinity Mirrored Room on 5 June; the contemporary galleries — including Cattelan's Comedian (2019) — on 26 June. The staging itself echoes the show's argument that “1960” and “now” are one continuous frame.
It runs through 10 January 2027 — seven months across the rotunda and the tower galleries.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 4
A new generation, brought in deliberately
Recent acquisitions hang next to the canon: Mohamed Ahmed Ibrahim, Farah Al Qasimi, Alex Da Corte, Lauren Halsey, Lucia Hierro, Yee I-Lann, Tommy Kha, Baseera Khan, Shinro Ohtake, Wendy Red Star, Cara Romero, Liu Shiyuan and Sheida Soleimani — among others.
Alex Da Corte's ROY G BIV (2022) is shown as a video installation with recurring live performances — exactly the kind of work where Pop's lineage becomes hard to draw.
9 / 10 →
Why now
The Pop comeback is a museum project
Apollo and the New York Sun have framed the opening as Pop “storming back” onto the museum's signature spiraling staircases. The 2025–26 announcement cycle made clear that the institution sees Pop not as a closed period but as a living lineage it intends to keep buying into.
In the same year Tate Modern is rerunning Frida Kahlo and the Hayward is rerunning Anish Kapoor, the Guggenheim is making Pop the umbrella that swallows both the icon-making and the institutional critique.

The line that stays

— Guggenheim press text, Guggenheim Pop: 1960 to Now
Concrete next step
🎨 If you go: start in Tower 5 with the 1960s rooms, then walk the rotunda chronologically up to Cattelan, Da Corte and Halsey on the top ramps. Allow at least 90 minutes — the contemporary section is where the curatorial argument lives.
