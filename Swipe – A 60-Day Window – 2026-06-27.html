The Hook
Can a 60-day truce hold open the world’s most important oil chokepoint?
After this year’s war and ceasefire, Washington and Tehran are trying to convert a pause into a deal — with the Strait of Hormuz, sanctions and Iran’s nuclear programme all on the table at once.
4 / 9 →
The Core
A framework, not yet a peace.
Around 15 June 2026, US and Iranian officials reached a preliminary memorandum of understanding — an initial framework, not a final agreement — setting a 60-day ceasefire window during which the hardest questions are meant to be resolved.
5 / 9 →
Finding 1 / 4 · The deal so far
Reopen the strait, ease the squeeze
The framework covers reopening the Strait of Hormuz to commercial shipping and lifting restrictions on maritime traffic, alongside talks on sanctions relief and the possible release of up to $25 billion in frozen Iranian assets — contingent on future compliance.
6 / 9 →
Finding 2 / 4 · The chokepoint
“Iran does not control the Strait”
When the IRGC announced it was again closing Hormuz in response to strikes, the US military denied it, insisting the waterway remained open and that “Iran does not control the Strait of Hormuz.” Control of the narrows is as much rhetorical as physical.
7 / 9 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The nuclear knot
Down-blend, freeze, monitor
The sides gave themselves 60 days to reach a technical agreement on how to down-blend Iran’s highly enriched uranium and to freeze and monitor its programme. They set up dedicated oversight, sanctions and nuclear working groups to do it.
8 / 9 →
Why this matters now
One incident can unravel it
The truce is brittle: on 26 June 2026 President Trump said Iran had fired one-way attack drones at the strait, one striking the upper deck of a large cargo ship — an alleged violation of the 60-day extension. A single strike can put the whole framework back in doubt.
