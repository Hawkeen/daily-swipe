The hook
No one is “self-made.”
The episode unpacks Alasdair MacIntyre, the philosopher behind After Virtue, who argued that the modern image of the lone, self-authoring individual is a myth. We inherit our identities — and our morality — from the communities, traditions and stories we are born into.
4 / 10 →
The core argument
Modern moral language is in ruins — and we can only rebuild it through stories and traditions.
MacIntyre’s After Virtue argues that we kept the vocabulary of ethics — “good,” “duty,” “virtue” — while losing the shared framework that gave it meaning. The repair is to see the self not as a free-floating chooser but as embedded in narratives, practices and traditions.

Finding 1 / 4
Why moral arguments never end
MacIntyre’s diagnosis is “emotivism”: in modern culture, moral claims have collapsed into expressions of personal preference — saying “this is wrong” means little more than “I dislike this.” With no shared standard, debates over justice or rights become interminable shouting matches.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 4
“Man is a story-telling animal”
For MacIntyre, we make sense of our lives as narratives with a beginning, middle and direction. His famous formulation: I can only answer “What am I to do?” if I can first answer “Of what story or stories do I find myself a part?” Identity is narrative before it is choice.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 4
Virtues live inside practices
Virtues, MacIntyre argues, are not invented by lone individuals; they are the qualities that let us pursue the goods internal to shared “practices” — and they are carried forward by living traditions. The self-made person is a myth precisely because character is something we inherit and sustain together.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 4
One of the great dissenters of modern ethics
Alasdair MacIntyre (1929–2025) spent a career arguing against the liberal, individualist mainstream of moral philosophy. After Virtue (1981) became one of the most influential ethics books of the century — a sustained case that we cannot understand morality apart from community and history.

Why this matters in 2026
A thinker revisited after his death
MacIntyre died in 2025. In spring 2026 Stephen West devoted a run of Philosophize This! episodes to him — Ep. 245 “The Rival Moral Approaches of the Modern World” (12 April) and Ep. 246 “The Myth of the Self-Made Person” (26 April) — making his case newly accessible to a mass audience just as debates over individualism rage on.
