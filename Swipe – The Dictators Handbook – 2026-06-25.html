Book of the day · 📖
The Dictator’s Handbook
Bruce Bueno de Mesquita & Alastair Smith · Why Bad Behavior Is Almost Always Good Politics · PublicAffairs · 2011
A cold, clarifying theory of how power really works — in palaces and parliaments alike.
Swipe →
3 / 9 →
The hook
No emperor, no king, no CEO ever rules alone. Find out who a leader truly depends on, and every “irrational” act snaps into focus as smart politics.
The same logic governs a tyranny, a democracy, a corporation and a family. Power is power.
4 / 9 →
The core thesis
Leaders do whatever keeps them in power. And staying in power means keeping happy the small group whose support is essential — nothing more.
How big that essential group is — large in a democracy, tiny in a dictatorship — explains almost everything about how a country is run, and for whose benefit.
5 / 9 →
Finding 1 / 4
Three groups behind every leader
The nominal selectorate (“interchangeables”) — everyone with a nominal say. The real selectorate (“influentials”) — those who actually choose the leader. And the winning coalition (“essentials”) — the few whose support the leader cannot survive without. Everything turns on that last group.
6 / 9 →
Finding 2 / 4
Rule one: keep the coalition small
Of the authors’ rules for survival, the first and most important is to keep your winning coalition as small as possible. Fewer essentials means each can be bought more cheaply and replaced more easily — and a large pool of interchangeables waiting in the wings keeps them all in line.
7 / 9 →
Finding 3 / 4
Small coalition vs. large coalition
Dictatorships rest on a small coalition, so leaders buy loyalty with private rewards — cash, plum jobs, the freedom to be corrupt. Democracies rest on a large one, so leaders must deliver public goods — roads, schools, growth — to satisfy the many. Grow the coalition and policy is forced to serve a wider slice of the population.
8 / 9 →
Finding 4 / 4
Bad behaviour, good politics
Corruption, repression and neglect aren’t aberrations or signs of an evil personality. For a leader who depends on only a few essentials, they are the rational route to staying in power. Self-interest, not ideology, is the engine — which is exactly why the theory predicts behaviour so well.
9 / 9 ✓
The takeaway
“No emperor, no king, no sheik, no tyrant, no CEO, no family head, no leader whatsoever can govern alone.”
— Bueno de Mesquita & Smith, The Dictator’s Handbook
Concrete next step
👑 Pick any leader in today’s news and ask: who are their essentials? Their seemingly baffling choices usually make sense once you know.
