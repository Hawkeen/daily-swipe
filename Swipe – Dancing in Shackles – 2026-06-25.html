Episode of the day · 🎧
Dancing in Shackles
Sinica (Kaiser Kuo) — Yi-Ling Liu on The Wall Dancers: Searching for Freedom and Connection on the Chinese Internet · 25 Feb 2026
China’s internet, told through five lives caught between freedom and the firewall.
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The hook
What does it feel like to create something real on the most surveilled internet on Earth?
Yi-Ling Liu’s answer is a phrase journalists used in the early 2000s for working under the censor’s eye: “dancing in shackles.” Constraint and creativity, locked together — not one crushing the other.
4 / 10 →
The core idea
The Chinese internet isn’t simply a cage. It’s a tense, living dance between the state and the people who use it.
Liu describes the dynamic as “a dynamic push and pull between state and society” — a tango set to an erratic rhythm of subversion and acquiescence. Users push; the state pushes back; sometimes it even pulls them close.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 5
Five lives, one web
The book traces China’s online life through five people: a rapper, a gay-rights app founder, a feminist activist, a science-fiction writer, and an internet censor. Each one navigates the same forces — creative and constrictive — from a different seat.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
Even the censor is a character
Putting a censor among the protagonists is the book’s key move. Control isn’t a faceless wall; it’s done by people with their own judgement, hesitations and incentives — which is exactly why the line between the permitted and the forbidden keeps shifting.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
Hip hop and sci-fi, the same arc
Liu tracks the parallel fates of two creative scenes — rap and science fiction — under the state’s alternating embrace and clampdown. Both got promoted when useful and squeezed when inconvenient, showing how official favour can be as shaping as censorship.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
The end of techno-utopianism
There was an early utopian glow to Chinese netizen culture — a belief the web would inevitably open things up. The book charts how that optimism faded, replaced by a harder, more ambivalent sense of what online life can and can’t change.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
The two internets are converging
One of the conversation’s sharpest threads: the Chinese and US internets are converging. As Western platforms grow more centralised, algorithmic and policed, the gap with China narrows — making the Chinese experience less an exotic exception than a preview.
10 / 10 ✓
The takeaway
“A dynamic push and pull between state and society — a tango [set] to an erratic rhythm of subversion and acquiescence.”
— Yi-Ling Liu on the Chinese internet, The Wall Dancers
Concrete next step
📱 Next time you read “China’s internet is just censored,” ask who’s dancing — and whether your own feed is more shackled than you think.
