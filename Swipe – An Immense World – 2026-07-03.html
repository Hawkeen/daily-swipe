Book of the day · 📖
An Immense World
How animal senses reveal the hidden realms around us
Ed Yong · Pulitzer-winning science writer
Random House / The Bodley Head, 2022 · ~5 min read
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The Hook
The world you see is not the world. It’s a rumour of the world, filtered through the narrow slit of your senses.
A dog reads the morning in smells; a songbird sees colours we have no names for; a bat paints the dark with echoes. Yong’s premise: no creature senses everything, and ours is just one bubble among millions — not the true or complete one.
4 / 10 →
The Core Idea
Every animal lives inside its own Umwelt — a private sensory world.
Umwelt, coined by the Baltic-German zoologist Jakob von Uexküll in 1909, is the slice of surroundings an animal can actually sense and experience. Yong’s whole book is an exercise in empathy: trying to step out of the human Umwelt and imagine what it is like to be a scallop, a knifefish, or a mantis shrimp.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 5 · The sensory bubble
A filter, not a window
Earth teems with sights, textures, sounds, vibrations, smells, tastes, electric and magnetic fields — but every animal can tap only a small fraction of it. Senses evolved not to reveal reality in full, but to deliver the narrow band of information a given creature needs to survive.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5 · Senses we lack
Channels invisible to us
Yong devotes whole chapters to senses humans simply do not have: infrared heat, the electric fields fish use to ‘see’ in murky water, and the magnetic fields that guide migrating birds and turtles. These aren’t exotic add-ons — they are the primary reality for the animals that possess them.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5 · No ‘best’ sense
Every Umwelt is a trade-off
There is no ranking of animals by sensory ‘superiority’. A sharper sense in one domain means blindness in another; each Umwelt is tuned to a way of life. Our instinct to frame animals’ lives in terms of our senses, Yong argues, is the main obstacle to understanding them.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5 · Senses combined
The world arrives all at once
Animals rarely use one sense in isolation. Yong’s closing chapters show how smell, touch, sound and sight are braided together into a single seamless experience — which is exactly why reconstructing another creature’s inner world is so hard, and so worth attempting.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5 · Sensory pollution
We are shrinking other worlds
One species — ours — is now disrupting animal senses on a planetary scale. Artificial light and human-made noise flood habitats and drown out the cues other creatures depend on. Yong ends on it as a moral point: to protect animals, we first have to grasp the worlds we are erasing.
10 / 10 ✓
The Takeaway
“Each is enclosed within its own unique sensory bubble, perceiving but a tiny sliver of an immense world.” — Ed Yong
