The hook
“Illiberalism is winning. What has surprised me is how weak liberalism has felt in response.”
Ezra Klein opens the episode confessing that even he — “a professional liberal” — cannot tell you what liberalism’s vision is or who its leaders are. To beat the illiberal tide, he says, you’ll need “a liberalism that is aspirational again. A liberalism that has moral imagination again. A liberalism that stands for more than ‘not this.’”

The core argument
Liberalism began as a virtue, not a political philosophy — and the history has been lost.
Rosenblatt’s thesis in The Lost History of Liberalism: today’s attacks on liberalism succeed because the tradition’s real story has been hollowed out, leaving only a thin, easy-to-mock version focused on individual rights and individual desire.
For almost 2,000 years, she writes, being “liberal” meant liberalitas: “demonstrating the virtues of a citizen, showing devotion to the common good and respecting the importance of mutual connectedness.”

Finding 1 / 7
“Liberalism” was coined in 1811 — as an insult
Rosenblatt: “It’s been shown that ‘liberalism,’ the actual word, was first a pejorative, a term of insult. It was coined in 1811 by the enemies of the liberals.” Catholic counter-revolutionaries wrote it with an accent — libéral — to mark it as foreign and dangerous.
They denounced liberals as anarchists, selfish, sexually deviant, “against the family.” Today’s post-liberals, she notes, “are actually reviving some of that language and using very old arguments.”
5 / 11 →
Finding 2 / 7
The Latin root liber meant both “free” and “generous”
In Rome the “liberal” citizen practised liberalitas: reciprocity, gift-giving, devotion to the commonwealth. Rosenblatt: “It’s really about having the freedom to voluntarily become the person that you should be.”
Cicero, Seneca, John Locke, John Donne, George Washington all wrote about liberality. It was an ethic citizens were expected to meet. Modern liberalism, she argues, has “completely elbowed out” this older meaning.
6 / 11 →
Finding 3 / 7
The Cold War stripped liberalism down to individual rights
Watching fascism and Communism use the state to forge citizens, post-war liberals recoiled. “A public education system that forms citizens started to have a scary ring to it,” Rosenblatt says. So liberals retreated to individual rights, property rights, the market — and quietly dropped the language of duty, character and the common good.
She also debunks the myth of liberalism as a uniquely Anglo-American tradition: the French Revolution gave birth to it, Germans transformed it, and the Anglo-American version is a late 20th-century reframing.
7 / 11 →
Finding 4 / 7
It took Bismarck’s Prussia to invent the welfare-state strand of liberalism
After the Franco-Prussian War shocked France, observers noticed German soldiers were healthier, vaccinated, schooled. Bismarck — not a liberal — was running an early state health-care system. English “new liberals” took the lesson: government had to intervene.
That’s how today’s confusing split was born: in Europe “liberal” still means laissez-faire and small government; in America it means F.D.R. and redistribution. Same word, opposite politics, both products of the 19th-century crisis.
8 / 11 →
Finding 5 / 7
A “liberal arts” education was supposed to forge citizens, not employees
Rosenblatt: the purpose was to “form freedom-loving and moral leaders — to give them the tools… through engagement with the classics.” Job training was a menial task, beneath the project.
Today, she notes wryly, defenders of the humanities try to argue that liberal arts will help you get a job: “Isn’t it funny… the whole discussion about what a citizen of a democracy means… has kind of dropped out.”
9 / 11 →
Finding 6 / 7
Liberals built the cage and also gave the keys
Rosenblatt doesn’t whitewash. “You have John Stuart Mill saying absolutely atrocious things about how despotism is OK when you’re dealing with barbarians, talking about British imperialism in India. You have Alexis de Tocqueville, who was OK, apparently, with burning silos in Algeria.”
But: women, abolitionists, Frederick Douglass — all later prised the system open using liberalism’s own language, telling the men: “You are like an aristocracy — an aristocracy of sex.” That’s the “time-bomb” quality Klein keeps asking her about.
10 / 11 →
Why now
The post-liberals are at the table — not at the door
Klein explicitly names Patrick Deneen — the Notre Dame political theorist who has called for “regime change” against the liberal order — as “close to JD Vance,” the sitting US Vice President. The Catholic counter-revolutionary script Rosenblatt traces from 1811 (“the left wants to destroy the family”) is now spoken from the White House complex.
Klein’s frame on the episode: with Trump elected a second time and “not working out,” the question for 2026 isn’t whether liberalism is being attacked — it’s whether liberals can rediscover “moral imagination” fast enough to answer.

The line that stays
“Liberalism is best when it criticizes power. That’s how it limits authority and allows human flourishing.”
— Helena Rosenblatt, on The Ezra Klein Show, 5 May 2026
