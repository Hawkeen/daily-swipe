The Hook
When an empire fell apart, one man turned the wreckage into poetry.
A 2020 BBC/CCTV documentary placed Du Fu in world literature alongside Dante and Shakespeare. He has been compared to Virgil, Horace, Shakespeare and Wordsworth — yet in the West he is still barely known.

Who he was
The poet-sage of the Tang
Du Fu (712–770), together with his elder friend Li Bai, is often counted the greatest of all Chinese poets. He lived through the Tang dynasty’s dazzling height and its catastrophic unravelling — and his poems became the record of both.

Finding 1 / 5 · The turning point
A golden age that ended in horror
Du Fu lived in the 8th century, a time of extraordinary cultural accomplishment that ended in horrendous warfare and the death of millions. The An Lushan Rebellion shattered the empire; throughout his life he experienced the highs and lows of the Tang, and they shaped everything he wrote.

Finding 2 / 5 · “Spring View”
“The nation is broken, though mountains and rivers remain”
Written in 757, shortly after rebels seized the capital Chang’an, “Spring View” (春望) sets enduring landscapes against a shattered state: “in the city’s spring, grass and trees grow deep.” Family letters, he writes, are now “worth ten thousand pieces.” It is among the most translated of all Tang poems.

Finding 3 / 5 · The thatched cottage
From his own ruined hut, a wish for all
In “Song of My Cottage Unroofed by Autumn Gales” (761), the wind tears the roof off his Chengdu cottage and his family is soaked. Yet the poem rises to a famous wish: “a mansion with a thousand, ten thousand rooms, a great shelter for all the world’s scholars” — he’d “happily freeze to death” in his broken hut if others could be warm.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5 · His range
Love, family, nature, war, suffering
Du Fu’s poems are about love and family and friendship, about mankind and nature, about war and human suffering. He celebrated the beauty of the natural world and bemoaned the passage of time — a span of feeling that earned him the title “poet-sage.”

Finding 5 / 5 · Rediscovery
Brought west by Michael Wood — and Ian McKellen
In “Du Fu: China’s Greatest Poet” (BBC/CCTV, 2020), Michael Wood retraced the poet’s journey from the Yellow River to the Yangtze gorges, with the verse read by Ian McKellen — introducing to Western audiences a figure China has loved for over a thousand years.
