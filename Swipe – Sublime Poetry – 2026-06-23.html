The hook
In a life of just 37 years, he became the standard the West measured itself against.
Raffaello di Giovanni Santi (1483–1520) achieved — in the Met’s words — “such profound success as a painter, designer, and architect that he was regarded as the pinnacle of artistic perfection for centuries after his death.” And the US had never held a comprehensive show of his work until now.
4 / 10 →
The big idea
Not just beauty — “sublime poetry”: intellectual heft married to emotional depth.
The exhibition’s framing is that Raphael, the son of a painter and poet, matched ambition with lyricism, a poetic sensibility that captivated his peers. It traces his full arc — from Urbino, to Florence where he emerged as a peer of Leonardo and Michelangelo, to his final prolific decade at the papal court in Rome.

Finding 1 / 4
The first US show of its kind
Astonishingly, Raphael had never been the subject of a comprehensive US exhibition. The Met has gathered more than 170 masterpieces — drawings, paintings, tapestries — from public and private collections across Europe and the US, many never shown together. The Met is the sole venue.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 4
Masterpieces on loan from across the world
Among the works: the Alba Madonna (c. 1509–11) from Washington’s National Gallery, the Portrait of Baldassare Castiglione (1514–16) from the Louvre, the Lady with the Unicorn (1505–06) from the Galleria Borghese, and the monumental Ecstasy of Saint Cecilia (c. 1515–16) from Bologna.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 4
How he painted women
The show pays particular attention to Raphael’s portrayal of women — from his use of nude female models for the first time in Western art, to his tender depictions of the Madonna and Child. It’s a thread that runs from life study to devotional image.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 4
New science on 500-year-old panels
Alongside the drawings and paintings, the exhibition presents recent scientific discoveries made using state-of-the-art technology — revealing how Raphael actually built his images, and giving even familiar masterpieces the vigour of fresh perspective.
9 / 10 →
Why this matters in 2026
Closing in days — then it disperses
The show runs only through 28 June 2026, after which the loans return to their home collections around the world. Critics called it a “blockbuster” (The New York Times), “extraordinary” (The Wall Street Journal) and “a priceless gathering” (The Art Newspaper) — an assembly unlikely to recur for a generation.
