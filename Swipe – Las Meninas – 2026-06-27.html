Las Meninas
“The Maids of Honour”
Diego Velázquez
Oil on canvas · 1656 · Museo del Prado, Madrid · ~5 min read
Swipe →

The Hook
Who is the real subject — the little princess, the painter, or you, standing where the king and queen should be?
Velázquez turned an ordinary court portrait into a meta-painting: a picture about the act of painting, and about the strange exchange of seeing and being seen.
4 / 10 →
The Big Idea
A court portrait that quietly questions what a portrait is.
Las Meninas (1656) shows a moment in the Spanish court — the Infanta Margarita Theresa, her maids of honour, court dwarfs, and Velázquez himself at a vast canvas. Its layered gazes, deep illusionistic space and ambiguous narrative blur the boundary between reality and representation.

Finding 1 / 5 · The mirror
The king and queen are where you stand
Detail: the mirror in Las Meninas. Public domain, via Wikimedia Commons.
On the back wall, a flat mirror reflects King Philip IV and Queen Mariana — implying the royal couple stands outside the painted space, exactly where the viewer stands. The mirror projects their double into the scene and pulls us into the picture.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5 · A painting about painting
The true subject is the brush
Velázquez paints himself mid-work before a huge canvas we see only from behind. By hiding what he is painting, he makes the act of painting itself the real subject — a portrait that turns to look at its own making.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5 · Seeing and being seen
Everyone is looking at us
Several figures gaze directly out of the canvas. The picture becomes a meditation on seeing and being seen — its complex composition and illusionistic space challenging the conventions of traditional portraiture.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5 · Two meanings
The king — and the dignity of art
Art historians read the painting on two levels at once: one political, tied to the king and the court; the other a statement of the idea Velázquez had of art itself — a claim for the status and intellectual dignity of the painter.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5 · The philosophers’ picture
Representation looking at itself
Las Meninas has fascinated thinkers far beyond art history — Michel Foucault devoted a famous analysis to it, reading the work as representation turning to examine itself, a touchstone for debates on perception, postmodernism and painting.

The Takeaway
Stand in front of Las Meninas and the painting quietly hands you a role: you are the one being looked at.
