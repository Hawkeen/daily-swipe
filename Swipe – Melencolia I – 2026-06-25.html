Melencolia I
Albrecht Dürer · copper engraving · 1514 · the most analysed image in Western art
Swipe →
3 / 9 →
The hook
A winged genius sits surrounded by every tool of knowledge — compass, scales, hammer, a magic square, a strange solid — and does nothing.
Why is the smartest figure in Renaissance art paralysed? Five centuries of scholars are still arguing.
4 / 9 →
The core idea
Melancholy as both curse and gift — the dark temperament of the creative mind.
Dürer fuses the medieval idea of melancholy as an affliction with the new Renaissance idea that it is the very temperament of genius. The brooding figure is read as a kind of spiritual self-portrait: knowledge and despair seated together, a meditation on creativity and the limits of human understanding.

The magic square
On the wall hangs a 4×4 grid — the first magic square printed in Europe. Every row, column and diagonal sums to 34, as do the four quadrants, the corners and the centre. The two middle cells of the bottom row read 15 and 14: the year. Linked to Jupiter, it was meant to counter the planet of melancholy, Saturn.

Dürer’s solid
Looming at the centre is a strange eight-faced block — a truncated rhombohedron with a faint human skull on its surface. Its exact geometry still puzzles mathematicians, who named it “Dürer’s solid.” Geometry, the most certain of sciences, rendered mysterious and unresolved.

Tools that lie idle
Scattered around the figure are the instruments of creation and measurement — compass, scales, hourglass, sphere, plane and saw. All the means of making and knowing are present; none is in use. Capability without the will to act: knowledge possessed, but creativity stalled.

Why 1514
The year was personal. Dürer’s mother, Barbara, died in 1514, and he was then deep in humanist philosophy and mathematics. Around this time he wrote, “what is beautiful I do not know.” Private grief meets the Renaissance discovery that even genius runs up against the limits of what it can grasp.
