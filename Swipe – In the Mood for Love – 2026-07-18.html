In the Mood for Love

Wong Kar-wai’s film about everything two people don’t do — and how restraint became the most acclaimed film of its century

Hong Kong / France · 2000 · with Maggie Cheung & Tony Leung Chiu-wai · ~6 min read

Swipe →

5 / 15 →

The Hook

A film shot for fifteen months without a screenplay became the fifth-greatest film ever made.

Hong Kong, 1962: a journalist and a secretary rent rooms in adjacent apartments and slowly discover that their spouses are having an affair — with each other. What follows is a love story told almost entirely through what is withheld.

6 / 15 →

The Core Idea

Desire filmed as discipline: the drama is everything the two leads refuse to do.

Wong even shot a sex scene between Chow (Tony Leung) and Su Li-zhen (Maggie Cheung) — and cut it, a decision critics have called perfectly in keeping with the film’s mood. The betrayed refuse to become the betrayers; the camera lingers instead on corridors, rain, noodle stalls and everything unsaid.

Finding 1 / 7 · The origins

A city remembered, a story borrowed

One of Wong’s initial inspirations was “Intersection”, a short story by the Hong Kong writer Liu Yichang. The setting is the Hong Kong of Wong’s own childhood — a territory full of mainland-born Chinese and their memories, saturated with the songs of 1930s and 40s Shanghai.

The film forms the middle of an informal trilogy: preceded by Days of Being Wild (1990), followed by 2046 (2004).

Finding 2 / 7 · The method

Fifteen months, no script

There was no screenplay: the actors received scenes with dialogue on the day they were to be shot, and were encouraged to improvise; Wong edited his story from the footage afterwards.

The painstaking method — plus an Asian financial crisis that interrupted the production’s funding — stretched the shoot to fifteen months. An earlier plan of three stories (one provisionally titled “A Story of Food”, about a woman and a man sharing noodles and secrets) was whittled down to this one.

Finding 3 / 7 · The look

Cheongsams and two cinematographers

Christopher Doyle, who favoured “jazz-like spontaneity”, began the shoot but had to leave as it ran over schedule; Mark Lee Ping Bin completed it. Doyle insisted on filming where real streets, buildings and clothes-lines could give the actors the energy of 1960s Hong Kong.

Maggie Cheung’s ever-changing qipao — the cheongsam — became her way into the character: a married woman carrying the elegance of her pre-revolutionary mainland youth, whose quiet strength Cheung felt was unlike her own spirit.

Finding 4 / 7 · The sound

A waltz borrowed from another film

The film’s signature music, Shigeru Umebayashi’s “Yumeji’s Theme”, was originally written for Seijun Suzuki’s film Yumeji — Wong repurposed the aching string waltz as the recurring pulse of the film.

Additional music came from Michael Galasso; the soundtrack’s slow repetitions mirror the film’s ritual of near-misses.

Finding 5 / 7 · The ending

A secret whispered into a wall at Angkor Wat

Image: Bjørn Christian Tørrissen, CC BY-SA, via Wikimedia Commons.

Chow tells a friend an old story: when someone had a secret, they would climb a mountain, carve a hollow in a tree, whisper the secret into it and seal it with mud. In the final scenes he travels to Cambodia and, as a monk looks on, whispers into a hollow in a wall at Angkor Wat — and seals it with mud.

Finding 6 / 7 · Cannes

A first for Hong Kong

The film premiered in competition at the 53rd Cannes Film Festival in 2000, where Tony Leung won Best Actor — the first Hong Kong actor ever to receive the honour.

Cheung later called the film the high point of her career; within four years she had all but retired from acting, despite winning Best Actress at Cannes for 2004’s Clean.

Finding 7 / 7 · The canon

Fifth-greatest film of all time

In the 2022 Sight and Sound critics’ poll — the decennial reference survey of world cinema — In the Mood for Love ranked fifth of all time with 141 votes: the highest-placed film of the 21st century.

In the BBC’s 2018 poll of 209 critics from 43 countries, it ranked 9th among the greatest foreign-language films.

Why this matters in 2026

The 25th-anniversary wave

For the film’s 25th anniversary it returned to cinemas — released in China on Valentine’s Day 2025 in regular and IMAX formats — and an extended cut with nine minutes of never-seen footage has been screening and arriving on disc.

The Criterion Collection has issued a full 4K UHD edition based on the 2020 restoration from the original negatives, supervised by Wong Kar-wai himself. In 2021, 92 seconds of unseen first-day footage (“Day One”) sold as an NFT for around $550,000.

The Takeaway

A love story built from everything left unsaid — whispered into a wall and sealed with mud.

Restraint, repetition, a borrowed waltz — and a quarter-century later, the film only grows in stature.
