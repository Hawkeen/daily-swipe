Ana Mendieta

Exhibition · The body written into the earth — Tate Modern’s first major survey in over a decade

Tate Modern, London · 15 Jul 2026 – 17 Jan 2027 · curated by Valentine Umansky

Swipe →

5 / 14 →

The hook

What if a sculpture were made of earth, fire and your own body — and survived only as a photograph?

Ana Mendieta made art by lying down in the land and leaving a trace. The object vanished with the tide or the wind; what remains is the image. Tate Modern has now gathered more than 120 of these works into the UK’s biggest Mendieta show in over a decade.

6 / 14 →

The core idea

The body and the earth in a single gesture.

Mendieta (1948–1985) is best known for her “earth-body” works: she outlined her own body in the landscape using natural elements — earth, fire, water, flowers. The result challenged the old boundaries between sculpture, photography and film all at once.

Finding 1 / 5 · The artist

A short, radical life

Born in Cuba in 1948 and sent to the United States as a child, Mendieta was active through the 1970s and 1980s before her death in 1985 at just 36. In barely 15 years she built one of the most influential bodies of work in late-20th-century art.

8 / 14 →
Finding 2 / 5 · The signature work

The Silueta Series

Her Silueta series explores presence and absence through the imprint of the female form — from a body strewn with white flowers to a silhouette pressed into riverbanks and shores. Because the works were ephemeral, they exist for us as photographs and films of a form that has already gone.

Finding 3 / 5 · The exhibition

120+ works, thematically arranged

The survey brings together over 120 works, arranged thematically through symbolic locations to trace key moments in her career. Alongside the iconic pieces are newly remastered films, early paintings and late sculptural works — many never before shown in the UK.

10 / 14 →
Finding 4 / 5 · The reading

“Neolithic” art, recreated

Critics reach for ancient words: reviewers described the works as visceral and “Neolithic,” and the Tate has recreated some of her ephemeral earth-pieces for the survey. It is art that feels less made than unearthed — a body written into the ground like a very old sign.

11 / 14 →
Finding 5 / 5 · The framing

Curated as a homecoming

Curated by Valentine Umansky, the show frames Mendieta not through the tragedy that has often overshadowed her, but through her sensual engagement with the natural world — foregrounding the films and materials, and letting the work speak on its own terms.

12 / 14 →
Why it matters now

On view through winter

The exhibition opened on 15 July 2026 and runs until 17 January 2027 — a rare chance to see the full arc of Mendieta’s practice in one place, with remastered films and works new to British audiences. For anyone in or near London this autumn, it is currently on.

The takeaway

“Tate Modern’s first exhibition dedicated to Ana Mendieta celebrates her sensual engagement with the natural world.”

— Wallpaper*

Mendieta turned disappearance into a medium: the sculpture is gone, but the trace of a body in the earth stays with you. This survey lets a short, fierce career be seen whole.
