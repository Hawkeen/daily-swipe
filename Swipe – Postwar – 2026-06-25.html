Book of the day · 📖
Postwar
Tony Judt · A History of Europe Since 1945 · Penguin Press · 2005
One continent, sixty years, told as the long aftermath of a single war.
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The hook
Europe rebuilt itself by forgetting the war — and only later defined itself by remembering it.
The miracle of recovery after 1945 ran on a strategic amnesia about collaboration, complicity and atrocity. The reckoning — above all with the Holocaust — came decades later, and reshaped what Europe believed it stood for.
4 / 10 →
The core thesis
The whole period can be read as the unravelling of the consequences of the Second World War — East and West, told in equal detail.
Judt called it “an avowedly personal interpretation” with no grand overarching theory. Yet it was the first major history of contemporary Europe to give the stories of Eastern and Western Europe equal weight — one continent, not two halves.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 5
1945 was a ruin, not a triumph
Judt opens not with victory parades but with a shattered continent: bombed cities, tens of millions displaced, populations “ethnically cleansed” into new borders. The Europe we now think of as stable and prosperous was, at the start of his story, a catastrophe still smoking.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
The social-democratic settlement
A central thread is the postwar bargain: the welfare state, public services and a managed economy that delivered the long boom. For Judt, this consensus — built across the political spectrum — was Western Europe’s real distinctive achievement, and its later erosion a genuine loss.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
One Europe, told in two halves
Most Cold War histories treated the communist East as a separate subject. Judt refused the split, weaving the Soviet bloc into the same narrative as the West — so 1989 reads not as a sudden surprise but as the closing of a circle opened in 1945.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
Memory returns — late
The book famously closes on a coda about the Holocaust: how the memory of the murder of Europe’s Jews, largely suppressed in the rebuilding years, became by the century’s end the moral foundation stone of the European project. Remembering, not forgetting, became the new admission ticket to Europe.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
History as a warning against myth
Running through Judt’s work is a suspicion of comforting national myths — the stories countries tell to launder their pasts. Honest history, he argued, is the antidote: a continent that misremembers how it got here is poorly equipped to keep its peace.
10 / 10 ✓
The takeaway
“Europe was able to rebuild itself politically and economically only by forgetting the past, but it was able to define itself morally and culturally only by remembering it.”
— Louis Menand, summarising Judt’s Postwar
Concrete next step
🇪🇺 When a politician invokes “our history” this week, ask: which parts are being remembered — and which quietly forgotten?
