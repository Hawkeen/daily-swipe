China’s Growth Machine
Overcapacity, the 15th Five-Year Plan, and a country that is two economies at once
Ones and Tooze · Adam Tooze & Cameron Abadi
Foreign Policy · recorded at ‘Summer Davos’, Dalian, June 2026 · ~5 min read
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The Hook
Is Chinese ‘overcapacity’ a threat — or a gift the world keeps refusing?
Western capitals treat China’s flood of cheap solar panels, batteries and EVs as a problem to be tariffed away. From a conference floor in Dalian, Adam Tooze makes the opposite case: for much of the planet, that abundance is exactly what’s needed.
4 / 10 →
The Core Idea
One country, two economies — and a plan built for the split.
Tooze’s frame: China runs an AI-and-clean-energy sector growing at double digits right alongside a property sector falling at the same rate. The 15th Five-Year Plan (2026–2030), with its talk of ‘new quality productive forces’, is the economy China is deliberately building for that imbalance.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 4 · Reframing
‘Overcapacity’ as global benefit
Tooze argues China should be unapologetic about churning out solar panels in massive quantities, because the world needs them. He reads cheap solar and batteries as a net gain — an industry that can put electricity into sub-Saharan villages that never had it.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 4 · The split screen
Double-digit growth beside double-digit decline
The central imbalance: an advanced-manufacturing and clean-energy boom expanding at double digits, next to a property sector contracting at roughly the same rate. Averaging the two hides the real story — a rapid rotation from old growth engines to new ones.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The plan
What the 15th Five-Year Plan bets on
The 2026–2030 cycle, unpacked at Summer Davos, prioritises innovation, consumption, industrial upgrading, green energy and continued opening to the world. Its wager is on ‘new quality productive forces’ — advanced manufacturing as the country’s next growth model.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 4 · Anti-involution
Calling a truce in the EV price war
In 2026 China switched to an ‘anti-involution’ stance on electric vehicles — setting minimum price floors and removing the EV purchase-tax exemption. The aim: rein in a ruinous domestic price war while still backing solar, EVs, hydrogen and ‘new-energy’ storage.
9 / 10 →
Why this matters in 2026
A five-year bet, made this summer
The World Economic Forum’s ‘Summer Davos’ ran in Dalian on 23–25 June 2026, with sessions like ‘15th Five-Year Plan, Unpacked’ framing how China’s next cycle will steer growth amid demographic shifts and shifting supply chains. This episode is a live read of that debate from the floor.
