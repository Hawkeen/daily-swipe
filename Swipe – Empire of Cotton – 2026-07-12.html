Book of the day · 📖
Empire of Cotton
A Global History — how a single soft fibre wove together slavery, empire and the modern industrial world
Sven Beckert · 2014 · ~6 min read
Swipe →
5 / 11 →
The Hook
Where did the Industrial Revolution really come from?
The schoolbook answer is British ingenuity — the spinning jenny, the steam engine. Beckert’s answer is darker: the whip, the gun and the cotton field. Before the factory could hum, armed European states had to remake the whole world of cotton by force.
6 / 11 →
The Core Thesis
Capitalism’s first engine was violence.
Beckert argues that state-backed force — slavery, land expropriation and colonial conquest — was the precondition for the Industrial Revolution, not a side-effect of it. Cotton is the thread he pulls to reveal it: no single commodity better exposes how global capitalism was actually built.
7 / 11 →
Finding 1 / 4 · The concept
“War capitalism”
Beckert’s central coinage. Long before factories, capitalism advanced through imperial expansion, slavery and land expropriation. The use of armed force by European states to reconfigure the world’s cotton industry, he argues, was a precondition for the industrial revolution itself.
8 / 11 →
Finding 2 / 4 · The myth
Not superior machines
Beckert flatly rejects the story of European technical genius. In his words, “Europeans became important to the worlds of cotton not because of new inventions or superior technologies, but because of their ability to reshape and then dominate global cotton networks.” Power came first; the clever machines came after.
9 / 11 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The human cost
A hunt for labour
Cotton, Beckert writes, “demanded quite literally a hunt for labor.” The system rested on the militarisation of trade, massive land expropriation, genocide against Native Americans and the enslavement of millions — the coercion that drove the plantations was inseparable from the boom in the mills of Lancashire.
10 / 11 →
Finding 4 / 4 · The handover
From the gun to the ledger
Beckert distinguishes “war capitalism” from the later “industrial capitalism,” in which states switched from open violence to quieter tools — law, diplomacy and bureaucracy — to protect the gains first seized by force. The two phases overlap; the ledger simply inherited what the gun had won.
