The hook
The Chinese state is still, in some real sense, running on software written in the 4th century BCE.
Kaiser Kuo's frame for the episode: anyone who writes about a society faces a question that's rarely posed but always there — how far back do you really need to go? For China, he argues, the Warring States is “a strong candidate for the deepest layer that genuinely matters.”
4 / 11 →
The core argument
A revolution, not an “eternal China”.
Meyer's To Rule All Under Heaven is the first proper one-volume narrative history of the Warring States in English for general readers. The episode's project is to find the line between taking the deep continuity of Chinese political life seriously and the “lazy essentialism that reaches for Han Feizi every time Xi Jinping makes a speech.” The answer, Kuo and Meyer argue, is that the period was a genuine revolution whose institutions were then consciously cultivated for two millennia.

Finding 1 / 5
260 years, seven great states
The Warring States runs roughly the 260 years between the death of Confucius and Qin's unification in 221 BCE. Kuo compares the task of narrating it to writing about the Thirty Years' War — then multiplying the actors by ten and the duration by nearly ten.
Any educated person in China, he notes, can still rattle off the seven-state mnemonic without hesitation: Qi, Chu, Yan, Han, Zhao, Wei, Qin.
6 / 11 →
Finding 2 / 5
The rise of the shi (士)
Why it's properly called a revolution: the period destroyed the Zhou-era hereditary aristocracy and replaced it with an educated, meritocratic class — the shi.
Those scholar-officials, Kuo says, are the ancestors of every Chinese scholar-official, every Communist Party cadre, every Gaokao striver since. The exam-and-bureaucracy reflex of Chinese governance starts here.
7 / 11 →
Finding 3 / 5
The world's first modern state
The era produced the centralized bureaucratic state — what Francis Fukuyama, in The Origins of Political Order, calls the world's first genuinely modern state, predating its European counterparts by something like 17 centuries.
It also produced the imperial throne, which then ran without fundamental redesign for about 2,132 years — until the boy emperor Puyi abdicated in 1912.
8 / 11 →
Finding 4 / 5
The “Legalist Qin” is a cartoon
One of the book's revisionist threads: the famous categories of the Hundred Schools were far more entangled — with each other and with the actual courts — than tidy textbooks suggest. Kuo likens the Jixia Academy to an “ancient Brookings.”
The Qin that unified the realm was not the cartoon-legalist regime of later caricature: it employed dozens of erudites in the Confucian classics, and the First Emperor's stelae on Mount Taishan invoke Confucian virtues — humaneness, rightness, filial piety — in entirely conventional terms.
9 / 11 →
Finding 5 / 5
Zhongguo as aspiration, not description
The era left a vocabulary of regional identity that's still alive: Yan around Beijing, Chu in the Hubei–Hunan area, the Zhao surname clustering in Shanxi. Meet a Zhao, Kuo jokes, and you can bet on Shanxi ancestry.
What the Warring States actually teaches about China in 2026, the two conclude, is that zhongguo — “the central state” — has long been an aspiration, not a description: the legacy is constant self-reinvention.

Why this matters in 2026
Recorded the day Trump landed in Beijing
Kuo opens with a confession: he's recording “just a few hours after President Trump touched down in Beijing to kick off a summit meeting with President Xi Jinping” — and is deliberately not talking about it, choosing a story set 2,247 years earlier instead.
The point lands harder for it: as commentators reach for Han Feizi to decode Xi, the episode is a careful argument for how the deep past actually shapes the present — and how it doesn't.
