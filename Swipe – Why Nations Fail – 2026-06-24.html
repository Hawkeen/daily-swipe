Book of the day · 📖
Why Nations Fail
Daron Acemoglu & James A. Robinson — The Origins of Power, Prosperity, and Poverty · 2012 · authors awarded the 2024 Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences
Why some countries are rich and others poor — in one word: institutions.
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The hook
One town, split by a fence. Same desert, same climate, same culture. On one side people earn about three times what they earn on the other.
Nogales, Arizona and Nogales, Sonora are the same city cut in two by the US–Mexico border. Geography can’t explain the gap. Culture can’t. So what can? Acemoglu and Robinson’s answer: the rules people live under — their institutions.
4 / 10 →
Core thesis
Prosperity depends above all on whether a country’s institutions are inclusive or extractive.
The long-run fate of a nation, they argue, turns less on geography, culture or policy fashion than on the nature of its institutions. Inclusive institutions spread power and opportunity broadly; extractive ones funnel wealth and power to a narrow elite — and lock in poverty.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 5
A natural experiment: Nogales
The two Nogaleses share geography, climate and ancestry — yet the Arizona side enjoys far higher incomes, better health and order, while the Sonora side struggles with crime and corruption. The only big variable that differs is which country’s institutions each half lives under. The fence isolates the cause.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
The same people, opposite fates
North and South Korea are the book’s starkest case: one people, one history, split in 1945. The South built broadly inclusive institutions and grew rich; the North chose authoritarian, centralised, extractive ones and slid into stagnation and famine. Culture was held constant; institutions diverged — and so did the outcomes.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
Inclusive vs extractive
Inclusive institutions enforce property rights, create a level playing field and reward investment in new skills and technologies — so the many have reason to participate. Extractive institutions are built to pull income and wealth from the many to benefit a few, through corruption, rent-seeking or coercion.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
Extractive economies kill the incentive to build
Where a small elite can seize the fruits of your effort, why invest, save or innovate? Nations with a long history of extractive institutions stay poor because entrepreneurs and ordinary citizens rationally hold back. Inclusive institutions create the security that makes investment and innovation worth the risk.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
It’s not destiny — it’s politics
Because institutions are made by people, they can change. Inclusiveness is not handed out by geography or fixed by culture; it is fought for and defended politically. In 2024 the authors’ body of work on why some nations prosper and others fail was awarded the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences.
10 / 10 ✓
The takeaway
“Inclusive economic institutions… that enforce property rights, create a level playing field, and encourage investments in new technologies and skills are more conducive to economic growth than extractive economic institutions that are structured to extract resources from the many by the few.”
— Acemoglu & Robinson, Why Nations Fail
Concrete next step
🌍 Pick any pair of neighbours that diverged — East/West Germany, Botswana/Zimbabwe — and ask the book’s question: which institutions were inclusive, which extractive, and who held the power to set the rules?
