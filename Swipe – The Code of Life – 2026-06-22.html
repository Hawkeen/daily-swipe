The hook
Bacteria invented a way to remember and destroy viruses. We turned it into scissors for the genome.
CRISPR began as a microbe's immune system. Then, Isaacson recounts, in 2012 Doudna and others figured out a more earth-shattering use: how to turn CRISPR into a tool to edit genes. It is now being used to treat sickle-cell anaemia, cancers and blindness.
4 / 10 →
The core argument
The digital revolution is giving way to the life-science revolution.
Isaacson's thesis is that we are entering an age in which “children who study digital coding will be joined by those who study genetic code.” To navigate it, he argues, “we will need not only scientists, but humanists” — people comfortable in both worlds, like Doudna. The power to edit our own evolution is arriving faster than our wisdom about how to use it.

Finding 1 / 4
From a microbe's memory to a pair of scissors
CRISPR is the system bacteria use to recognise and chop up viruses they have met before. The 2012 leap was to repurpose that machinery so it could be aimed at any chosen stretch of DNA and cut it. That single insight opened the door to deliberately editing the genome of living things — including us.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 4
It already cures a disease
This is not theory. Casgevy — the first CRISPR/Cas9 therapy ever approved by the FDA — treats sickle-cell disease. In the trial, 16 of 17 patients were free of the vaso-occlusive crises that define the illness, and the remaining one was free of related hospitalisations. A genetic disease, edited out of a body.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 4
No lone genius in the lab
One of Isaacson's running themes: almost all important innovations in science and tech aren't solo eureka moments. They are community efforts, with several teams circling the same idea at once. The CRISPR story is a race — collaborative and fiercely competitive at the same time — not a single flash of insight.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 4
The questions begin where the cures end
Curing disease is the easy part to agree on. The book presses the harder ones: “Should we allow parents, if they can afford it, to enhance the height or muscles or IQ of their kids?” Doudna herself described a nightmare in which “Hitler with the face of a pig” asks her to explain CRISPR — and warned the tool could become a toolbox for future Frankensteins.
9 / 10 →
Why this matters in 2026
From one patient to a pipeline
In 2025 an infant known as KJ at Children's Hospital of Philadelphia was treated with a fully personalised CRISPR therapy designed for his single mutation. By mid-2026, more than 50 CRISPR trials are recruiting worldwide, with base-editing therapies (which swap one DNA letter without cutting both strands) heading toward FDA filing.
Casgevy is now approved across the US, UK, EU, Canada, Switzerland and the Gulf — and CRISPR is expanding into cardiovascular disease, the world's leading killer.
