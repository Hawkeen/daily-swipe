The Sharps Who Actually Win at Prediction Markets
Who really makes money on Kalshi and Polymarket — and how
Odd Lots · Joe Weisenthal & Tracy Alloway, with Brian Golden, Daniel Reichman & Adam Iscoe
6 July 2026 · ~48 min episode
Swipe →
5 / 12 →
The Hook
On a prediction market, the house doesn’t take your money. The other bettors do.
Everyone can see the same odds on Kalshi and Polymarket. So who consistently comes out ahead? A very small group of “sharps” — and this episode goes inside how they operate.
6 / 12 →
The Core Idea
Profit comes from other people’s biases.
Guests Brian Golden and Daniel Reichman are part of a private Discord where serious prediction-market traders swap ideas and make real money. The episode digs into the systematic biases among traders, the remarkable lengths the winners go to in order to spot opportunities, and how they think about insider trading.
7 / 12 →
Finding 1 / 4 · Who wins
A handful take the pot
Prediction markets look democratic — anyone can bet on anything. But, as the episode’s title puts it, the people actually making money are a narrow group of sharps. The systematic mistakes of everyone else are what they get paid for.
8 / 12 →
Finding 2 / 4 · The back room
A private Discord for pros
Golden and Reichman belong to a members-only Discord where serious traders share tips and coordinate. It’s a reminder that even “wisdom of the crowd” markets have an inside track — small, connected groups moving faster and sharper than the retail flow.
9 / 12 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The edge
Edge is effort, not luck
The winners go to remarkable lengths to find and price opportunities the crowd misses. Their advantage isn’t a hunch; it’s legwork, discipline and a cold read of where other traders are systematically wrong.
10 / 12 →
Finding 4 / 4 · The grey zone
And what about insider trading?
The conversation doesn’t dodge the uncomfortable part: how these traders feel about insider information, and the still-unsettled ethics and rules of a fast-growing market. When you can bet on real-world outcomes, “knowing something first” gets complicated.
11 / 12 →
Why this matters in 2026
Prediction markets went mainstream
Kalshi and Polymarket have moved from niche to headline. Journalist Adam Iscoe recently profiled these traders for The New York Times Magazine, and Odd Lots aired this episode on 6 July 2026 — a sign that betting on real-world events is now a real corner of finance, biases and all.
12 / 12 ✓
The Takeaway
A prediction market pays you for being right when the crowd is wrong. The people who win aren’t luckier — they just do the work to spot everyone else’s systematic mistakes.
