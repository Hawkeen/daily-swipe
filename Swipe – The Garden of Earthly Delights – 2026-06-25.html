Creative topic · 🎨
The Garden of Earthly Delights
Hieronymus Bosch · oil on oak triptych · c. 1490–1500 · Museo del Prado, Madrid
Nearly four metres of Eden, ecstasy and Hell — in one frame.
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The hook
A 500-year-old painting still so strange we can’t agree whether it’s a warning — or a lost paradise.
Naked bodies, giant fruit, mutant creatures, a man with a tree for a torso. Bosch painted it around 1500, and art historians are still divided over whether its centre is a moral warning or a panorama of innocent delight lost.
4 / 10 →
How to read it
Read it left to right, like a story in three acts: Paradise, the human world, and Hell.
It’s a triptych — three hinged panels. The left shows the Garden of Eden; the vast centre, a pleasure garden of mostly nude figures and oversized fruit; the right, a dark and terrifying Hell. Most readings see a journey from innocence toward depravity.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 5 · Left panel
Eden: God joins Adam and Eve
The left wing shows Paradise — most likely the moment God presents Eve to Adam, sometimes called the Joining of Adam and Eve. It’s the calm before the fall, already laced with hints of the love and lust that will drive the rest of the painting.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5 · Centre
The “Strawberry Painting”
The huge central panel teems with nude figures in “amorous acrobatics” among oversized animals and fruit. So many strawberries appear that the work was once simply called the “Strawberry Painting” — the fruit read as a symbol of pleasure that tastes sweet but vanishes fast.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5 · Right panel
Hell — and the Tree-Man
The right wing turns to a nightmarish Hell, darker than anything before it. At its heart stands the iconic “Tree-Man”: a figure whose torso is a cracked eggshell housing a tavern of the damned, his legs hollow trunks — one of the most haunting inventions in Western art.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5 · Closed
Shut it: the world on day three
Image: Hieronymus Bosch, public domain (Museo del Prado), via Wikimedia Commons.
When the wings are folded, the outside shows the Third Day of Creation in grey-green grisaille — the Earth as a flat disc inside a glass globe, before colour, before the sun and moon. Open the panels and the world bursts into colour and chaos.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5 · The debate
Warning, or paradise lost?
Because of Bosch’s religious world, most readings treat the work as a warning against the perils of temptation. But the meaning of the central panel remains genuinely contested — and that openness is a large part of why the painting still grips us five centuries on.
