Paul Kennedy on Great Powers
ChinaTalk · with Jordan Schneider & Phoebe Chow
Guest: Paul Kennedy (historian, Yale)
Published 10 June 2026 · ~5 min read

3 / 11 →
The Hook
Great powers don’t fall to armies first — they fall to their balance sheets.
Kennedy’s 1987 classic argued that economic and industrial capacity, not military might alone, decides which powers rise and fall. The episode revisits that lens for today’s US–China rivalry.
4 / 11 →
The Big Idea
“Imperial overstretch”: power outruns the economy that pays for it.
Kennedy’s core idea is that great powers get into trouble when their global military commitments outrun the economic base that funds them. In 2026 he frames the world as increasingly tripolar — the US, China and India — and urges Washington toward “sensible retrenchment.”
5 / 11 →
Finding 1 / 5 · The engine of power
Economic base, then military might
Kennedy argues industrial and economic capacity — not battlefield prowess alone — ultimately sets a great power’s standing. His preferred historical analogy for US–China today is the Anglo-German rivalry of the early twentieth century.
6 / 11 →
Finding 2 / 5 · The North Star
Imperial overstretch, pointed at the US
The overstretch thesis remains, in his words, his “North Star” — and in 2026 he turns it on the United States: commitments in many regions risk exceeding the economy that has to sustain them.
7 / 11 →
Finding 3 / 5 · His prescription
“Sensible retrenchment”
Kennedy’s advice to American policymakers is to focus on the immediate neighbourhood — the Americas — to reduce the risk of overstretch in places like the Middle East. The risk: becoming a “hemispheric” power rather than a global one, ceding ground to Chinese and Indian influence.
8 / 11 →
Finding 4 / 5 · Physical power
“Shipbuilding is real”
If the US chases “deals” and finance while China focuses on physical production and infrastructure, Kennedy warns, it will lack the “hard punch” needed to back its assertions. Real industrial output, not paper, is what underwrites power.
9 / 11 →
Finding 5 / 5 · The new shape
From bipolar to tripolar
The variables, Kennedy suggests, have shifted toward a tripolar world dominated by the US, China and India — a more crowded board than the US–China framing alone implies, and one that reshapes his decline thesis.
10 / 11 →
Why this matters in 2026
An old warning, back at the centre of strategy
The realities of 2026 — a tripolar rivalry between the US, China and India, and live debate over US retrenchment from the Middle East — have pushed Kennedy’s decades-old warnings back to the forefront of global strategy. His call for retrenchment lands as the US weighs how thin its commitments can stretch.
