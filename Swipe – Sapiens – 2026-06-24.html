Book of the day · 📖
Sapiens
Yuval Noah Harari — A Brief History of Humankind · first published in Hebrew 2011, English 2014
How one unremarkable ape ended up running the planet.
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The hook
Only Homo sapiens can talk about things that have never existed — gods, nations, money. That, Harari argues, is why we run the world.
No other animal can convince millions of strangers it has never met to cooperate toward an invented goal. A chimpanzee can’t imagine a god, a currency or a company. We can — and once enough of us believe the same story, it becomes real enough to build empires on.
4 / 10 →
Core thesis
Three revolutions made us — and shared fictions are the thread running through all three.
Harari tells human history through three turning points: the Cognitive Revolution (~70,000 years ago), the Agricultural Revolution (~10,000 years ago) and the Scientific Revolution (~500 years ago). What ties them together is our unique talent for believing in “intersubjective” realities that exist only in our collective imagination.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 5
The Cognitive Revolution
Between roughly 70,000 and 30,000 years ago, something changed in the Sapiens mind. We gained a kind of language no other species has: the power to talk about things we have never seen, touched or smelled — spirits, ancestors, legends. Fiction wasn’t a side-effect of language. It was the breakthrough.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
We live in a dual reality
Ever since the Cognitive Revolution, Harari writes, Sapiens has lived in two realities at once: the objective world of rivers, trees and lions — and the imagined world of gods, nations and corporations. Money, religions and states are “intersubjective”: real only because enough people agree to believe in them, yet powerful enough to move armies and markets.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
Farming: “history’s biggest fraud”
Harari calls the Agricultural Revolution “history’s biggest fraud.” The average farmer worked harder than the average forager and got a worse diet in return; ancient skeletons show new ailments — slipped discs, arthritis, hernias. His provocation: “We did not domesticate wheat. It domesticated us.”
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
Money is mutual trust
For Harari, money is the most universal and efficient system of mutual trust ever devised. A coin or a banknote is worthless in itself; it works only because everyone trusts that everyone else will accept it. Economic and political systems, on this view, run on belief much the way religions do.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
Fictions scaled us to billions
Shared myths are the reason Sapiens cooperate in numbers no other species can manage. Strangers who will never meet pool their effort because they believe in the same nation, the same brand, the same god, the same law. The story is the social glue — and changing the story can reorganise the world.
10 / 10 ✓
The takeaway
“There are no gods in the universe, no nations, no money, no human rights, no laws, and no justice outside the common imagination of human beings.”
— Yuval Noah Harari, Sapiens
Concrete next step
💸 Look at the money in your wallet (or your banking app) and notice: its value is a shared story. Then ask which other “facts” in your day are really intersubjective fictions we agree to keep believing.
