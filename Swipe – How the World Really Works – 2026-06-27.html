How the World Really Works
The science behind how we got here and where we’re going
Vaclav Smil
Viking · 2022 · ~325 pp · ~5 min read

3 / 10 →
The Hook
You are not made of solar energy. You are partly made of oil.
Smil’s provocation: the abstract talk of a green, digital, dematerialised future ignores what actually keeps eight billion people alive — energy-hungry materials and the fossil fuels behind them. He wants to replace wishful thinking with numbers.
4 / 10 →
The Big Idea
Civilisation stands on four pillars — and on fossil fuels.
Smil argues that any honest discussion of the future must start from how the world really works: our way of life is built on four irreplaceable materials — ammonia, steel, concrete and plastics — whose production has no commercially available, mass-scale, low-carbon substitute today.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 5 · The four pillars
Ammonia, steel, concrete, plastics
Smil names these the four pillars of modern civilisation. Their making is energy- and carbon-intensive: together they account for about one-sixth of the world’s energy supply and roughly a quarter of all fossil-fuel consumption.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5 · The scale
Mountains of stuff, every year
The numbers are staggering: humanity pours about 4.5 billion tons of cement and uses around 1.8 billion tons of steel a year, while global plastics output has risen from 20,000 tons in 1925 to roughly 370 million tons in 2019. Cities, in Smil’s words, are “embodiments of concrete.”
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5 · The invisible pillar
Ammonia keeps ~4 billion alive
Synthetic ammonia supplies the nitrogen that fertilises the world’s crops. Smil calculates that nearly 4 billion people would not be alive without it — which is why he calls its synthesis perhaps the most momentous technical advance in history.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5 · We eat fossil fuel
Five tablespoons of diesel per tomato
Modern food is partly made of oil. Smil reckons each greenhouse-grown supermarket tomato carries the equivalent of about five tablespoons of diesel embedded in its production, and that roughly 210 g of fuel goes into every kilogram of bread on your table.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5 · The hard part
No quick exit from carbon
Smil is sceptical of easy timelines: full decarbonisation of the global economy by 2050, he argues, is conceivable only at the cost of an unthinkable economic retreat or through near-miraculous technical advances. The honest path begins by admitting how dependent we still are.
10 / 10 ✓
The Takeaway
“Nearly 4 billion people would not have been alive without synthetic ammonia.” — the kind of number Smil uses to ground every claim about our future.
