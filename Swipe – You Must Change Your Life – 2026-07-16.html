You Must Change Your Life

Stephen West on Peter Sloterdijk — the philosopher who says the human being is, above all, a practicing animal

Philosophize This! · Episode #249 · 14 July 2026 · part 2 of a Sloterdijk series (after #248 on modern cynicism, 27 June)

Swipe →

5 / 11 →

The Hook

A headless stone torso looks at you — and issues a command.

Sloterdijk takes his title from the final line of Rilke’s poem “Archaic Torso of Apollo”: you must change your life. For him this is the “absolute imperative” — the quintessential command of inner transformation, older and deeper than any moral code. The episode asks what it would mean to take that command seriously today.

6 / 11 →

The Core Argument

Change doesn’t come from belief or willpower — it comes from practice.

West works through the first half of Sloterdijk’s 2009 book (Suhrkamp; English translation by Wieland Hoban, Polity 2013): a criticism of views of change that are far too centred on belief and moral strength, and a reworking of how to see human beings at the level of anthropology — as creatures constituted by their repeated exercises, routines and disciplines.

7 / 11 →
Finding 1 / 4 · The concept

Anthropotechnics

Sloterdijk’s key coinage names the “techniques of individual and collective self-transformation” — the training programmes, spiritual exercises and regimens by which humans work on themselves.

The book is a planetary history of practice: what monks, athletes, ascetics and philosophers have in common is that they treat the self not as a given but as something produced by repetition.

8 / 11 →
Finding 2 / 4 · The reversal

Religion, reread as training

Under this lens, our view of religion changes: what matters in religious traditions is not primarily what people believe, but the systems of practice they sustain — prayer, fasting, ritual, repetition.

The episode extends the thought to culture at large: culture itself, Sloterdijk suggests, should be seen as a kind of monastic rule — a shared regimen that shapes who its members become.

9 / 11 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The polemic

Not power, but discipline

Sloterdijk deliberately refuses the familiar frame of history as a struggle between those who wield power and those subject to it — a version of history he dismisses as “leftist kitsch”.

Instead he reads human life through the networks of “discipline” by which we live our lives and construct our world — a quiet inversion of a century of critical theory.

Finding 4 / 4 · The series

From cynicism to practice

The episode is the second in West’s summer Sloterdijk arc: #248 (27 June) covered “The Trap of Modern Cynicism”, this one turns to his positive programme — and only gets through half the book, with the rest to come.

It also closes a loop with earlier swipes: where Sapolsky says we never chose our dispositions, Sloterdijk answers that dispositions are exactly what training can remake.
