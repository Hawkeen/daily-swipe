The hook
Imagine the natural sciences were destroyed — then rebuilt from scraps by people who had forgotten what science was.
MacIntyre opens After Virtue with that thought-experiment — “a disquieting suggestion” modelled on Walter Miller’s A Canticle for Leibowitz. His hypothesis: the language of morality we still use today is in exactly that state of disorder. We’re arguing in fragments of a moral tradition we no longer remember how to speak.
3 / 10 →
The core argument
The Enlightenment tried to justify morality without a shared idea of what humans are for. That project was doomed from the start.
Aristotle’s ethics rested on a telos — a picture of the proper end of a human life. Strip out the telos and what’s left of morality is just a vocabulary list with no grammar: rules and slogans nobody can rationally adjudicate.
MacIntyre says Hume, Kant, Kierkegaard and Marx all failed at the same task for the same reason. You can’t derive an “ought” from an “is” once you’ve thrown away the “is”.

Finding 1 / 5
What replaces real moral reasoning: emotivism
Emotivism is the doctrine that “all moral judgments are nothing but expressions of preference.” When I say “X is wrong”, I’m really just saying “boo to X” — and trying to make you feel the same.
MacIntyre’s diagnosis isn’t that people believe emotivism. It’s that we now behave as if it’s true: every kind of moral persuasion has quietly collapsed into manipulation.
5 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
Three characters who rule emotivist culture
The manager — focused on technique, efficiency and control, without ever asking whether the goals being served are good. Claims an authority far beyond what their expertise warrants.
The therapist — aims at adjustment and psychological effectiveness rather than truth.
The rich aesthete — treats life as a search for private satisfaction. Each, MacIntyre argues, is a working illustration that public moral language has gone hollow.
6 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
The constructive answer: practices, internal goods, narrative unity
A practice — chess, medicine, farming, fatherhood — is a cooperative activity with its own standards of excellence. Meeting those standards produces “internal goods” you can’t get any other way: forms of understanding only available to people inside the practice.
Set against them are “external goods” — money, status, power. Institutions chase external goods; that’s how they tend to corrupt the practices they were built to house.
7 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
The book’s final fork: Nietzsche or Aristotle?
If the Enlightenment failed, MacIntyre says, only two honest options remain. Nietzsche’s Übermensch: accept that values are invented and write your own. Or Aristotle: recover a tradition in which virtues, practices and a shared telos hang together.
He calls Nietzsche “the King Kamehameha II of the European tradition” — clear-eyed enough to see modern morality is incoherent, but his “solution” just replaces one set of fictions with another.
8 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
“The barbarians are not waiting beyond the frontiers”
The book ends with one of the most-quoted passages in modern philosophy: build local forms of community in which the moral life can be sustained “through the new dark ages which are already upon us.”
The famous closing line: “We are waiting not for a Godot, but for another — doubtless very different — St. Benedict.” The hope is in small communities of virtue, not in fixing politics from the top.
9 / 10 →
Why now
MacIntyre died last May. The argument keeps getting louder.
Alasdair MacIntyre died on 21 May 2025, aged 96, at South Bend, Indiana — prompting a wave of retrospectives across the Catholic press, Daily Nous, Duke and Notre Dame. Stephen West’s February episode is part of that retrospective wave.
And the diagnosis still bites. A 2026 study in Political Psychology calls “virtue signaling” a form of moral grandstanding as competitive display — the emotivist behaviour MacIntyre predicted, scaled to a platform economy.

The line that stays
“This time however the barbarians are not waiting beyond the frontiers; they have already been governing us for quite some time. … We are waiting not for a Godot, but for another — doubtless very different — St. Benedict.”
— Alasdair MacIntyre, After Virtue, closing paragraph (1981)
