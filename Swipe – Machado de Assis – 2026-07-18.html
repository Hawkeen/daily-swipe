The Hook

His readers were the slave-owning class — and they were delighted to see themselves in his books.

Only in the 20th century did critics realise just how much Machado had been satirising them all along. The descendant of slaves had held a mirror up to the elite of a slave society — and the elite had smiled back.

The Core Idea

A 19th-century Brazilian whose books read as if they were written yesterday.

The episode presents Machado as a writer who brings 1800s Brazil vividly to life yet transcends time and place — per the programme, Salman Rushdie has said his works “seem to have been written yesterday, not 100 years ago”. Formally playful, ironic, endlessly modern: a world author who happened to write in Portuguese, in Rio, under an empire that still kept slaves.

Finding 1 / 7 · The life

From the Morro do Livramento to the canon

Joaquim Maria Machado de Assis was born in Rio de Janeiro in 1839 to a poor family: his father a wall painter and son of freed slaves, his mother a Portuguese washerwoman from the Azores.

He barely attended school and never went to university — a self-taught man who later learned French, English, German and Greek on his own, and rose to the summit of Brazilian letters.

Finding 2 / 7 · The context

Writing inside a slave society

Machado was the grandson of freed slaves — and he built his entire career while slavery was still legal in Brazil. Abolition came only in 1888, when he was nearly fifty.

Many of his characters came from the slave-owning class who were also the buyers and readers of his books — the uncomfortable double bind at the heart of the episode.

Finding 3 / 7 · The masterpiece

A novel narrated by a dead man

The Posthumous Memoirs of Brás Cubas — on the episode’s reading list in two rival translations (Flora Thomson-Deveaux for Penguin; Margaret Jull Costa & Robin Patterson for Liveright, both 2020) — is his most famous formal gamble: its narrator writes from beyond the grave.

That two major English translations appeared in the same year says something about the ongoing race to claim him for anglophone readers.

Finding 4 / 7 · The other classic

Dom Casmurro, “the Brazilian Othello”

The episode’s reading list includes Helen Caldwell’s classic 1960 study “The Brazilian Othello of Machado de Assis” — on Dom Casmurro, the novel whose jealous narrator has kept readers arguing for over a century.

Alongside it: Roberto Schwarz’s influential reading of Machado as “a master on the periphery of capitalism” — the scholarship around him is a canon of its own.

Finding 5 / 7 · The stories

A master of the short story too

The episode’s reading list is thick with his short fiction in fresh English dress: “The Looking-Glass: Essential Stories” (trans. Daniel Hahn, Pushkin Press, 2022), “26 Stories” and “The Collected Stories of Machado de Assis” (trans. Jull Costa & Patterson, Liveright).

The collected edition alone gathers the range of a writer who worked as novelist, poet, playwright and short story writer across five decades.

Finding 6 / 7 · The institution

Founder and first president of the Academy

In 1897 Machado founded the Brazilian Academy of Letters and became its first president — the self-taught grandson of freed slaves now literally presiding over his country’s literature.

He remains, per the episode, one of the towering figures of Brazilian and world literature — a national institution whose irony always cut against institutions.

Finding 7 / 7 · The programme

A first for In Our Time

This is the first time a Brazilian topic has been the focus of the programme in its decades-long history — and it comes in Misha Glenny’s first year as host, after he took over from Melvyn Bragg in January 2026.

Three Brazilianists — from UCL, Oxford and Cambridge — walk through the life, the novels and the long afterlife of his reputation.

Why this matters in 2026

The Machado wave keeps building

The episode’s reading list points to what’s coming: a new Jull Costa/Patterson translation of his final novel, Memorial de Ayres, arrives from Liveright in August 2026, and Cambridge University Press publishes “A History of the Brazilian Novel” the same month.

A Penguin Book of Brazilian Short Stories is slated for January 2027 — anglophone publishing is betting on Brazil, with Machado at the centre.
