The Hook
Where do you stop, and the world begin?
Roughly half the cells in your body are not human. The fungi in a single teaspoon of forest soil could, if unrolled, stretch the length of a football field. Long before plants stood upright, towering fungal spires already rose 8 metres above a barren Earth. The boundary between self and ecosystem has always been a polite fiction.

Core Thesis
Fungi do not fit our categories — they break the categories.
Sheldrake argues that fungi force a quiet revolution in how we think about individuality, intelligence, and life itself. They have no brains, yet solve mazes. They have no eyes, yet hunt. They constitute roughly a third of all soil biomass and made plant life on land possible.
The book moves outward in concentric rings: perception in fungal hyphae, partnership in lichens, networks in forest soils, chemistry in human minds, industry in tomorrow's materials.

Finding 1 / 4
Mycelium is a verb, not a noun
A mycelial network is not really a thing. It is, Sheldrake writes, an "anarchic filigree" — a continually rearranging cloud of microscopic threads called hyphae, each with a sensing, deciding tip. A single network can contain billions of these tips, all processing information in parallel with no central brain.
Lynne Boddy's lab at Cardiff filmed mycelium solving the shortest-path Steiner-tree problem between scattered food sources — the same optimisation that engineers struggle with on real-world transport grids. Whatever "intelligence" is, it predates neurons by hundreds of millions of years.

Finding 2 / 4
The Wood Wide Web is real — and economic
In a landmark 1997 study, ecologist Suzanne Simard injected radioactive carbon-14 into birch trees and tracked it across a forest into shaded Douglas fir seedlings — through their shared mycorrhizal partners. About 90% of land plants depend on these fungal partnerships; the fungi receive sugars, the plants receive phosphorus, nitrogen and water.
Toby Kiers's work showed the trade is not generous: fungi hoard, ration, and reroute resources to the partners that pay best, behaving like underground markets. Sheldrake calls it "biological capitalism" — but a version where the currency is phosphate.

Finding 3 / 4
Lichens broke biology's most basic rule
For 150 years, every textbook defined a lichen as one fungus plus one alga. In 2016, Toby Spribille sequenced common lichens and found a third, sometimes fourth, fungal partner woven in. The "simplest" symbiosis biology knew about is, on inspection, a small society.
Lichens survive vacuum, cosmic radiation, and 200°C temperature swings — they have been flown on the outside of the ISS and returned alive. Sheldrake's point cuts deep: if the canonical individual is actually a coalition, what is a "self" anywhere else in nature?

Finding 4 / 4
Fungi hijack minds — including ours
The fungus Ophiocordyceps infects carpenter ants, marches them to the precise leaf height where its spores disperse best, then sprouts from their heads. Sheldrake recounts how it manipulates muscle directly while leaving the brain intact — the ant remains conscious of choices it can no longer make.
Psilocybin, the molecule in "magic" mushrooms, evolved independently in several fungal lineages, suggesting it serves a function we still do not understand. Roland Griffiths's Johns Hopkins trials found a single supervised dose produced lasting reductions in anxiety and depression in terminally ill patients — now the basis for FDA breakthrough-therapy designations.

Why This Matters in 2026
Fungi are quietly entering the climate stack.
The global mycelium-materials market reached $3.9 billion in 2026, driven by Ecovative's home-compostable packaging and Henning Larsen's "Growing Matter(s)" installation at Milan Design Week 2025. MycoWorks' insolvency in October 2025 — and its reincarnation as MycoWorks Denver under new owners — shows the sector is now mature enough to have its first real shake-out.
More consequentially: SPUN (the Society for the Protection of Underground Networks), founded by Sheldrake's collaborator Toby Kiers in 2021, has begun publishing the first global maps of mycorrhizal carbon storage. Their 2025 estimates suggest these networks shuttle ~13 gigatonnes of CO₂ equivalent per year into soils — roughly one-third of fossil-fuel emissions.
The book made fungi visible. Policy is starting to follow.
