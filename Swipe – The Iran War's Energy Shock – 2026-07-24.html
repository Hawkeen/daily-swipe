The Iran War’s Energy Shock

Adam Tooze on the deals that could end the war — and the strait everything hinges on

Ones and Tooze · Foreign Policy · Ep. 244 · 29 May 2026 · ~40 min

Swipe →

5 / 14 →

The hook

A strait just 33 km wide carries a fifth of the world’s oil. In 2026, it became a weapon.

Foreign Policy’s economics duo — historian Adam Tooze and editor Cameron Abadi — turn to the war between the US, Israel and Iran, and ask the question underneath the headlines: what would it actually take, in economic terms, to end it?

6 / 14 →

The core idea

This is a shooting war, but the leverage is economic.

Read through Tooze’s lens, the path out of the war runs through energy markets: a durable truce would likely trade sanctions relief for a reopened Strait of Hormuz. Oil is both the prize and the pressure point — which is why every escalation shows up first in the price of a barrel.

Finding 1 / 5 · The chokepoint

One-fifth of the world’s oil

The Strait of Hormuz is the conduit for roughly a fifth of the global trade in oil and liquefied natural gas. Its narrowest shipping lanes are only a few kilometres across — which is exactly what turns a regional war into a global energy event.

8 / 14 →
Finding 2 / 5 · The shock

Brent up more than 50%

The US and Israel launched their war on Iran on 28 February 2026. In the weeks that followed, Brent crude rallied by more than 50%, touching roughly $113 a barrel, with US benchmark WTI near $100. An old lesson returned: energy chokepoints price in fear long before any tanker is actually stopped.

9 / 14 →
Finding 3 / 5 · The tail risk

The $200 scenario

Analysts spelled out the downside: Macquarie Group warned oil could spike toward $200 a barrel if the conflict dragged on and the Strait of Hormuz stayed closed. A number like that is not just a market story — it is an inflation, growth and political story for every oil-importing economy.

10 / 14 →
Finding 4 / 5 · The way out

Sanctions relief for open sea lanes

The shape of a deal, the episode argues, is an exchange: a long-term truce in which Washington offers Iran some sanctions relief in return for keeping the Strait of Hormuz open. Talks were floated for Doha, Qatar — a reminder that even a war can be bargained down to a question of barrels and access.

Finding 5 / 5 · The format

Two data points that explain the world

Ones and Tooze always pairs a headline number with something unexpected — here, the war sits beside a segment on strength training. The trademark whiplash is the point: Tooze treats the economy of oil and the economics of the gym with the same encyclopaedic curiosity.

Why it matters in 2026

The ceasefire keeps breaking

Since the episode aired, the truce has proved fragile. In early July 2026, tankers were attacked in the strait and the US struck Iran again on 8 July, reversing a slide back toward pre-war prices as Brent jumped anew. The economics Tooze laid out in May are still, week by week, deciding how far this war can go.

The takeaway

“Two data points each week that explain the world: one drawn from the week’s headlines and the other from just about anywhere else Tooze takes us.”

— Ones and Tooze, Foreign Policy

The Iran war looks like a story of missiles and diplomacy. Follow the barrels, and it becomes a story about the price of oil — and about how a 33-km strip of water can move the whole world economy.
