Episode of the day · 🎧
Spain’s China Gambit
Pedro Sánchez, strategic autonomy, and the European turn to Beijing — with Mario Esteban Rodríguez
Sinica Podcast · hosted by Kaiser Kuo
22 April 2026 · ~5 min read
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The Hook
A sophisticated bid for strategic autonomy — or an increasingly one-sided pilgrimage?
Spain’s prime minister has now flown to China four years running. The episode asks whether Pedro Sánchez’s courtship of Beijing is a shrewd play for leverage between Washington and Beijing, a genuine push for European strategic autonomy, or something closer to supplication.
4 / 10 →
The Core Argument
Madrid wants to de-risk and engage at the same time.
Guest Mario Esteban Rodríguez — who directs the Center for East Asian Studies at the Autonomous University of Madrid and is a senior fellow at the Elcano Royal Institute — argues that Spain’s strategy tries to balance risk reduction and engagement, strengthening its strategic autonomy while staying aligned with the EU. It is a test case for how mid-sized European powers position themselves between the two giants.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 4 · The Tsinghua speech
“A country rebuilding its greatness”
At Tsinghua University, Sánchez defended multilateralism, called the EU–China trade deficit unsustainable, and described China as “a country rebuilding its greatness.” The words themselves signalled a deliberately warmer register toward Beijing than most EU capitals now use.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 4 · Autonomy, not alignment-flip
Staying inside the EU while leaning east
The episode frames Spain’s move less as abandoning the West than as pursuing strategic autonomy: keeping options open, reducing dependence, and bargaining harder — all while remaining anchored in EU positions. The open question is whether such balancing is sustainable when Washington and Beijing pull in opposite directions.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The deficit problem
Engagement with a trade gap attached
Sánchez paired his warmth with a warning that the EU–China trade imbalance is unsustainable — the same tension running through every European capital: how to deepen ties with China’s market while confronting a structural deficit and Beijing’s industrial power.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 4 · A European bellwether
Spain as a preview of Europe’s choice
The conversation situates Sánchez within a broader European turn toward Beijing — a signal of how the continent might recalibrate as US reliability is questioned. Spain, on this reading, is a small country testing an argument the whole EU may soon have to make.
9 / 10 →
The Takeaway
Whether it reads as autonomy or as pilgrimage, Sánchez’s China gambit puts a hard European dilemma into a single vivid case — and Sánchez chose his words carefully:
China is “a country rebuilding its greatness.” — Pedro Sánchez, at Tsinghua University
