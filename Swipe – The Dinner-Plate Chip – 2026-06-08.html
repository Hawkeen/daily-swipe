The hook
“We can’t build data centers fast enough to keep up with demand.”
Feldman tells Joe Weisenthal and Tracy Alloway that AI infrastructure is “behind demand, not ahead of it” — and points to a $25 billion backlog at his own company as the evidence. The same week, Cerebras priced its IPO above the expected range and shares opened more than 100% higher.
3 / 11 →
The core argument
Cut the wafer differently — and bet the company that bigger means faster.
Every other chipmaker dices a 300mm silicon wafer into hundreds of small dies. Cerebras leaves the wafer whole. The result is one chip the size of a dinner plate — and, by Cerebras’s account, inference that runs many times faster than on the leading GPU.
Feldman frames the bet on Odd Lots as a generational architectural choice: GPUs were never designed for AI inference; wafer-scale was.

Finding 1 / 7
A chip the size of a dinner plate — 58× bigger than average
The WSE-3 is 21.5 cm square, carved from a single 300mm silicon wafer with no inter-die interconnect. On board: 4 trillion transistors, 900,000 AI-optimised cores, 44 GB of on-chip SRAM at 5nm.
Feldman’s framing on Odd Lots: about 58× larger than the average chip, “and that sheer size enables blazing fast inference for AI queries.”
5 / 11 →
Finding 2 / 7
2,625× the memory bandwidth of Nvidia’s flagship B200
Cerebras reports the WSE-3 hits 21 petabytes per second of memory bandwidth versus 8 TB/s on the Nvidia B200 — and 250× more on-chip memory. The argument: AI inference is throttled by memory, not raw compute.
Independent firm Artificial Analysis benchmarked the CS-3 at 2,522 tokens/sec on Meta’s 400-billion-parameter Llama 4 Maverick. The B200 came in at 1,038.

Finding 3 / 7
The same five founders sold their last company to AMD for $334M
Feldman, Gary Lauterbach, Michael James, Sean Lie and Jean-Philippe Fricker co-founded the microserver pioneer SeaMicro in 2007. AMD bought it in 2012 for roughly $334 million, and Feldman ran AMD’s data-center server group.
In 2015 the same five regrouped to start Cerebras, with a $27 million Series A from Benchmark, Foundation Capital and Eclipse Ventures.

Finding 4 / 7
2026’s blockbuster AI IPO: $185/share, $56.4B valuation
On 13 May 2026 Cerebras priced its IPO at $185, above the expected range. The company sold 30 million shares to raise $5.55 billion, valuing it at $56.4 billion on a fully diluted basis. Shares opened up more than 100%.
Underlying numbers: $510 million revenue in 2025, up 76% year over year, with a $237.8 million net profit. CFIUS hurdles tied to Middle Eastern (G42) investors had to be cleared before the re-filing.

Finding 5 / 7
$20 billion of OpenAI capacity — and OpenAI owns warrants for ~10% of Cerebras
Cerebras’s anchor contract: OpenAI has committed roughly $20 billion to rent 750 megawatts of Cerebras computing capacity between 2026 and 2028. OpenAI also loaned Cerebras $1 billion.
In return, OpenAI holds warrants worth up to ~10% of the company — around $5 billion at the IPO midpoint, roughly half the gross profit Cerebras stands to make on the deal.

Finding 6 / 7
Two big problems Cerebras still has to solve
Software moat. Nvidia’s CUDA stack supports over 900 libraries and AI models. Cerebras has built its own software layer to compete — but Nvidia has a multi-year head start, and developers don’t rewrite stacks willingly.
Yield economics. Wafer-sized chips are far more expensive to fabricate, and if enough sections of one giant die come back defective, the whole chip has to be discarded. The architecture only pays off if AI demand really stays this hot.
10 / 11 →
Why now
The $4–8 trillion question hanging over the market
Goldman Sachs estimates $4–8 trillion of spending on AI chips, data centres and power over the next five years. The Cerebras IPO landed in the middle of a broader debate Wall Street has named the “CapEx Cliff”: can software revenue ever justify what hyperscalers like Amazon and Meta are spending on infrastructure?
Feldman’s answer on Odd Lots is unequivocal — backlogs at Cerebras, Nvidia, AMD and every major memory supplier all point the same way. The shorts say it’s 1999. He says it’s 1995.
