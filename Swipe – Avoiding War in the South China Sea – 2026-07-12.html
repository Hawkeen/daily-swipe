Episode of the day · 🎧
Avoiding War in the South China Sea
Ten years after the tribunal ruled against Beijing, why is the risk of conflict in this vital waterway still “stubbornly high”?
Pekingology (CSIS) · Henrietta Levin & Greg Poling · 9 July 2026
Swipe →
5 / 12 →
The Hook
Ten years ago today, an international tribunal threw out most of China’s claims to the South China Sea. Beijing has spent the decade ignoring it.
Why does Beijing care so much about small rocks in the middle of the ocean? And why has this sea become the sharpest flashpoint in US–China relations? CSIS’s top maritime expert walks through China’s strategy, its successes — and its failures.
6 / 12 →
The Core Thesis
The ruling was a landmark for international law — but law without enforcement has not lowered the temperature.
Levin and Poling examine the evolving nature of China’s maritime claims and how, a decade after the award, the risk of conflict in one of the world’s most vital throughways remains stubbornly high — with Chinese control expanding inside its neighbours’ exclusive economic zones.
7 / 12 →
Finding 1 / 4 · The ruling
Map: U.S. Central Intelligence Agency (1988), public domain, via Wikimedia Commons.
12 July 2016: the nine-dash line has no legal basis
An arbitral tribunal constituted under Annex VII of UNCLOS ruled unanimously for the Philippines: China’s claim to “historic rights” within its nine-dash line has no basis under the law of the sea — and none of the disputed Spratly features qualifies as an island generating a 200-nautical-mile exclusive economic zone.
8 / 12 →
Finding 2 / 4 · The defiance
A decade of building and pressing
China rejected the outcome from day one and has spent the last ten years stepping up island-building across the mineral-rich sea. It now actively controls areas inside the Philippines’ exclusive economic zone, including Scarborough Shoal — the very feature whose seizure in 2012 triggered Manila’s case.
9 / 12 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The stakes
Why small rocks matter so much
The episode digs into why Beijing invests so heavily in features most people could not find on a map: the South China Sea is a vital global throughway, and control over its reefs and shoals translates into leverage over the region — which is why it has become a major flashpoint in US–China relations rather than a local fisheries dispute.
10 / 12 →
Finding 4 / 4 · The guest
The man who watches the reefs
Greg Poling directs the CSIS Asia Maritime Transparency Initiative and the Southeast Asia Program — AMTI’s satellite tracking has documented the island-building campaign for years. His book “On Dangerous Ground: America’s Century in the South China Sea” traces the US role in the dispute back a hundred years. Host Henrietta Levin is a Senior Fellow with the CSIS Freeman Chair in China Studies.
11 / 12 →
Why this matters in 2026
The anniversary is today — and the gap is wider than ever
On this tenth anniversary, Manila marks “West Philippine Sea Victory Day” while Beijing denounces a “thoroughly illegal” tribunal, with a senior Chinese diplomat calling the ruling a “thorn” in relations with the Philippines. The US and its partners issued a joint statement reaffirming the award as final and binding. Ten years on, the law is settled — the sea is not.

The Takeaway
“Ten years after an international tribunal found the vast majority of China’s claims in the South China Sea to be unlawful, the risk of conflict in this vital global throughway remains stubbornly high.” — Pekingology, CSIS
