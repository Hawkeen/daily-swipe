The hook
"Textile factories of the early British Industrial Revolution generated great wealth for a few but did not raise worker incomes for almost a hundred years."
A century. Not a decade. Not a generation. A century of cotton mills running flat-out before the workers' wages started to follow the productivity line. That is the historical baseline against which Acemoglu and Johnson want you to evaluate every confident sentence ever spoken about AI lifting all boats.
3 / 10 →
The core argument
"There is nothing automatic about new technologies bringing widespread prosperity. Whether they do or not is an economic, social, and political choice."
The book overturns the techno-optimist default: that science advances, productivity rises, and living standards inevitably trickle outward. Acemoglu & Johnson read a thousand years of cases (the medieval plow, the mill, the steam engine, the assembly line, the chip) and find the opposite pattern. Technology is shaped by what powerful people want — and it generates riches, status, and further political voice for those already powerful, unless countervailing power forces a different split.

Finding 1 / 5
The productivity bandwagon is a fairy tale
The argument economists usually default to: when output per worker rises, competition for labour drives wages up with it. The bandwagon, the authors call it.
In competitive labour markets, wages are determined not by productivity (output per worker) but by the marginal productivity of labour — the extra output the next worker adds. If new tech mostly replaces workers rather than complementing them, that marginal contribution can collapse even while average productivity soars. The bandwagon stays parked.
5 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
The Luddites were not opposed to technology
The English textile workers who smashed mechanised looms in 1811–13 are remembered as the original anti-technology cranks. Acemoglu & Johnson follow the recent historiography that reads them more carefully.
They were resisting a specific distribution of the gains from mechanisation — in a context where the workers who bore the costs had no political mechanism at all for capturing any of the gains. That is a rational labour-market position. The error wasn't theirs; the error is in the cartoon version we tell about them.
6 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
Augmentation vs. automation — a forced choice
The book's central distinction: tech that raises the marginal productivity of workers (better tools, better information, new tasks they can now do) lifts wages and spreads gains. Tech that replaces workers with machines concentrates gains with capital owners.
Most of today's AI investment, they argue, is being steered down the automation track — because that is what investors and managers reward. The augmentation track exists; it isn't being built. That is a governance failure, not an inevitability.
7 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
The new AI panopticon
The book closes with a specific warning that has aged sharply since 2023: we need not only trade unions, civil society, and trustbusters, but also legislative and regulatory reforms to prevent the advent of a new panopticon of AI-enabled surveillance.
The same models that automate work also watch it — and watch the people who do it. The risk is not science fiction; it is what predictive scheduling, sentiment analysis, and biometric monitoring already do in warehouses and call centres today, with much weaker legal pushback than the Luddites once mustered.
8 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
Why this book got a Nobel-sized hearing
On 14 October 2024, Daron Acemoglu (MIT, Killian Professor of Economics), Simon Johnson (MIT Sloan), and James A. Robinson were jointly awarded the Nobel Memorial Prize in Economic Sciences "for studies of how institutions are formed and affect prosperity" — recognising the body of work that Power and Progress sits at the apex of.
The committee's framing reads almost like a précis of the book: prosperity depends on inclusive institutions that distribute power widely, not extractive ones that concentrate it. The book applies that lens specifically to technological choices.
9 / 10 →
Why this matters in 2026
The $700 billion year
In 2026, the five largest US cloud and AI infrastructure providers — Microsoft, Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Oracle — have committed to roughly $660–690 billion of capital expenditure on AI infrastructure, with Evercore and Bank of America placing the 2026 number as high as $800–900 billion. Goldman Sachs projects $1.6 trillion in annual AI capex by 2031.
Acemoglu & Johnson's argument lands directly on this moment. The capital is being allocated. The technological direction is being chosen — not by referendum, by a handful of boardrooms. The book is the case for treating that as a political question, while there is still a choice to make.

The line that stays
"Technology does not have a preordained direction, and nothing about it is inevitable."

Concrete next step
📖 Read the introduction (free preview on the publisher's page) before the rest of the book. It lays out the augmentation-vs-automation distinction cleanly; everything afterwards is historical case-work for that one frame. If you only have an evening, that chapter plus the AI chapter near the end gives you 80% of the argument.
