Book of the day · 📖
Age of Ambition
Chasing Fortune, Truth, and Faith in the New China
Evan Osnos · New Yorker staff writer
Farrar, Straus and Giroux, 2014 · National Book Award winner · ~5 min read
Swipe →
3 / 9 →
The Hook
What happens when a fifth of humanity suddenly starts to dream for itself?
Between 2005 and 2013, Osnos watched an old idea reappear across China: a belief in “the sheer possibility to remake a life.” His book follows that new sense of individual aspiration — and the Party that is determined to keep control of it.
4 / 9 →
The Core Idea
A collision of two forces: aspiration and authoritarianism.
Rather than write about the state from above, Osnos builds the story from individual lives — famous and unknown — grouped under three themes: Fortune (money and ambition), Truth (information and censorship) and Faith (meaning after ideology). The tension runs through every one.
5 / 9 →
Finding 1 / 4 · Fortune
Carried to class on her mother’s back — then a millionaire
Gong Haiyan was injured in a childhood accident, and her village school said it could not take a pupil who couldn’t walk; her mother carried her to the classroom on her back. She went on to build an online dating service and become a millionaire — Osnos’s emblem of the new possibility of remaking a life.
6 / 9 →
Finding 2 / 4 · Truth
A quarter of a billion visitors — and a censor watching
The blogger Han Han built a website that drew more than a quarter of a billion visitors. Osnos’s middle section, Truth, follows the running contest between this new appetite for information and expression and a state apparatus built to manage what people can say and know.
7 / 9 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The individual vs the Party
The blind lawyer who sued the state
Chen Guangcheng, a blind self-taught lawyer, brought a 2005 lawsuit against the enforcement of the one-child policy. He lost, was jailed, and on release in 2011 was placed under house arrest in his rural village — the sharp edge of the collision between individual conscience and Party power.
8 / 9 →
Finding 4 / 4 · Faith
After the ideology, a search for meaning
As thirty years of breakneck growth reshaped “the soul of the largest country on earth,” Osnos’s final section turns to the spiritual vacuum left by fading Communist faith — and the reach for religion, values and self-creation that has rushed in to fill it.
