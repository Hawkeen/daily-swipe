Creative topic · 🎨 · Photography

The Salt of the Earth

Sebastião Salgado photographed labour, migration and the planet itself — all in luminous black and white

Sebastião Salgado · Brazilian documentary photographer · 1944–2025 · ~5 min read

Swipe →

5 / 14 →

The hook

One photographer, 130 countries, 55 years — and not a single frame in colour.

Salgado made dramatic, unflinching black-and-white images of hardship, conflict and natural beauty, captured across 130 countries over 55 years. He gave up colour completely — and never looked back.

6 / 14 →

The core idea

A photograph can make us see — and seeing can change us.

Salgado’s philosophy centred on using photography as a tool for social change: he believed images could raise awareness of global issues and human dignity. “Photography,” he said, “is the language that gives people the opportunity to see what you saw.”

Finding 1 / 5 · Workers

The dignity of labour

His project Workers documented manual labour across the globe — miners, fishermen, shipbreakers — monumental images that insisted on the dignity and scale of human toil in an age that was hiding it away.

8 / 14 →
Finding 2 / 5 · Migrations

A planet on the move

In Migrations, Salgado turned to mass human movement and displacement — refugees and the uprooted — giving faces and scale to one of the defining crises of our time.

9 / 14 →
Finding 3 / 5 · Genesis

Letting nature speak

His late epic Genesis showcased pristine landscapes and indigenous communities — the parts of the planet still untouched. “In GENESIS,” he wrote, “my camera allowed nature to speak to me. And it was my privilege to listen.”

10 / 14 →
Finding 4 / 5 · Black & white

Why no colour

Salgado always preferred the chiaroscuro palette of black and white. “The only heritage we have is our planet,” he said, “and I have decided to go to the most pristine places… and photograph them in the most honest way I know… and of course it is in black and white, because it is the only thing I know how to do.”

Finding 5 / 5 · The belief

“We are animals, born from the land”

For Salgado, our link to the earth was spiritual, not sentimental. “We are animals, born from the land with the other species,” he said. “What made us survive all these hundreds of thousands of years is our spirituality; the link to our land.”

Why this matters in 2026

The year of the retrospectives

Salgado died in Paris on 23 May 2025, aged 81. A year on, the tributes are everywhere: Paris’s Hôtel de Ville staged a free retrospective from 21 February to 6 June 2026, and from 24 October 2026 the Museo Civico in Bassano del Grappa shows 160 of his photographs (through April 2027). His eye is having a very public afterlife.

The takeaway

“In GENESIS, my camera allowed nature to speak to me. And it was my privilege to listen.”

— Sebastião Salgado

Labour, exile, wilderness — Salgado spent a lifetime asking us to look harder at what we usually look past, and trusting that black and white could tell the truth in colour’s place.
