Book of the day · 📖
The Avoidable War
Kevin Rudd — The Avoidable War: The Dangers of a Catastrophic Conflict between the US and Xi Jinping’s China · PublicAffairs · 2022
By a former Australian PM and Mandarin-speaking China hand of 40+ years
The greatest danger isn’t hostility. It’s misunderstanding.
Swipe →
3 / 10 →
The hook
The US and China may stumble into war — not because they want one, but because neither understands the other.
Rudd names the real risk: “mutually assured noncomprehension and mirror imaging.” Each capital projects its own logic onto the other, and from that misreading flows the danger of catastrophic miscalculation.
4 / 10 →
The core argument
War is avoidable — through “managed strategic competition.”
Rudd argues the two giants can coexist without betraying their core interests if they build a framework with hard guardrails: clear limits on each side’s security policies, an honest acceptance that both will compete hard within those limits, and space for collaboration where interests align.
5 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 5
Why listen to Rudd
A former Australian prime minister who has studied, lived in and worked with China for more than forty years, Rudd is one of the very few who can speak to the mindset of the leadership on both sides — the people whose judgment will decide whether a war is fought.
6 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
The trap: mirror imaging
The central danger, Rudd writes, is “mutually assured noncomprehension and mirror imaging” — each side assuming the other thinks the way it does. Washington and Beijing read the same event and reach opposite, and dangerous, conclusions.
7 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
The fix: hard limits, not warm words
Managed strategic competition isn’t friendship. For it to work, Rudd argues, it must establish hard limits on each country’s security policies, recognise that both sides will maximise advantage within those limits, and encourage collaboration where it is in both nations’ interest.
8 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
The cure for misreading: strategic literacy
Rudd prescribes “mutual strategic literacy” — each side genuinely learning how the other thinks. So enlightened, senior negotiators with their leaders’ backing could agree on red lines and normalise competition in non-lethal arenas.
9 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
Competitive coexistence
The goal isn’t harmony but a world where the US and China “competitively coexist” — rivals held apart by mutual deterrence rather than drifting toward catastrophe. Continuing rivalry, yes; uncontrolled escalation, no.
10 / 10 ✓
The takeaway
“Our best chance of avoiding war is to better understand the other side’s strategic thinking and to conceptualize a world where both the U.S. and China are able to competitively coexist.”
— Kevin Rudd, The Avoidable War (2022)
Concrete next step
🕊️ Read it alongside the headlines: every Taiwan-Strait incident is a live test of whether “managed strategic competition” or “mutually assured noncomprehension” is winning.
