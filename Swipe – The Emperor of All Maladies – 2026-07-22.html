The Emperor of All Maladies

A Biography of Cancer — the 5,000-year story of a disease told with a cell biologist’s precision and a biographer’s passion

Siddhartha Mukherjee · Scribner · 2010 · Pulitzer Prize for General Nonfiction · ~6 min read

The hook

Cancer is not a modern invention. It has shadowed humanity for more than 5,000 years.

Mukherjee — a physician, researcher and award-winning writer — set out to do something odd: write the biography of a disease. The result is a chronicle of an illness humans have lived with, and perished from, since the first physicians put a name to it and, almost always, admitted they had no cure.

6 / 15 →

The core thesis

“In a sense, this is a military history — one in which the adversary is formless, timeless, and pervasive.”

Mukherjee examines cancer with a cellular biologist’s precision, a historian’s perspective and a biographer’s passion. The story he tells is one of human ingenuity, resilience and perseverance — but also, he insists, one of hubris, paternalism and misperception.

Finding 1 / 6 · The ancient enemy

“There is no treatment”

The book traces cancer back some 4,600 years to the Egyptian physician Imhotep, who had a name for the illness — and, against his own case notes, one stark line of therapy: there is no treatment.

For most of recorded history that verdict barely changed. Cancer was less a battle than a sentence.

Finding 2 / 6 · The recurring patient

Atossa, queen of Persia

Around 440 BC the Greek historian Herodotus recorded the first known breast-tumour excision — on Atossa, queen of Persia, cut by a Greek slave.

Mukherjee turns her into a device: he imagines Atossa seeking treatment in each successive era, a way to measure exactly how far medicine has — and hasn’t — come.

Finding 3 / 6 · The surgeon’s hubris

Halsted’s “radical” cure

In 1894 William Halsted described the radical mastectomy — removing the breast, the chest muscles and the axillary lymph nodes together. Convinced that more surgery meant more cure, his operation became the standard treatment for breast cancer for nearly a hundred years, long after evidence began to question it.

Finding 4 / 6 · The first chemotherapy

Farber’s poison that healed

In 1948 the pathologist Sidney Farber treated children with acute leukaemia using aminopterin, a folic-acid antagonist synthesised for him by the chemist Yellapragada SubbaRow. Of sixteen children, ten showed temporary remissions — the first proof that a drug could push back cancer.

His landmark paper was received, Mukherjee writes, with “skepticism, disbelief, and outrage.” It was also the birth of chemotherapy.

Finding 5 / 6 · The war declared

Nixon’s “War on Cancer”

The narrative pivots on the declaration of a “war on cancer” — Richard Nixon’s National Cancer Act of 1971, which poured public money and national ambition into a cure. It was a moment of soaring optimism, and, Mukherjee shows, of the overreach that followed when the enemy proved far more elusive than a moon shot.

12 / 15 →
Finding 6 / 6 · The targeted age

Gleevec, and precision at last

The book’s hopeful turn is the arrival of targeted therapy. Imatinib (Gleevec), designed to block a single faulty protein, transformed chronic myeloid leukaemia from a roughly three-year sentence into a near-normal lifespan for patients who keep taking it — an estimated 250,000 people now alive because of it. Proof that intelligently designed drugs could change the odds.

Why this matters in 2026

The next chapter: mRNA vaccines

The “targeted age” Mukherjee described is now personalising. Moderna and Merck’s neoantigen vaccine mRNA-4157 — which in the phase 2b KEYNOTE-942 trial cut the risk of melanoma recurrence or death by 49% — is heading for a phase 3 read-out in 2026.

On 16 July 2026 Moderna began a phase 1 trial of mRNA-4200, designed to encode seven common tumour antigens at once. A positive phase 3 in a common cancer would be the biggest milestone since the first checkpoint inhibitors.

Reception

A prize-winner, and a film

Published in 2010, The Emperor of All Maladies won the Pulitzer Prize for General Nonfiction. It was adapted by Ken Burns into a three-part PBS documentary in 2015 — carrying its “biography of cancer” to a mass audience and cementing Mukherjee, an oncologist at Columbia and a Rhodes scholar, as one of science writing’s clearest voices.
