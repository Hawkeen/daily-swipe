The Memory of Society
Photography · Sebastião Salgado, the economist who became the camera’s conscience
Brazil · 1944–2025 · ~6 min read
Swipe →
5 / 12 →
The Hook
He trained to read economies. He ended up reading faces.
Salgado came to the camera late, from a career in economics. In stark black and white, he spent decades photographing the people the global economy usually keeps out of frame — miners, migrants, the famine-struck — and gave them monumental dignity.
6 / 12 →
The Core Idea
Photography as witness — and as memory.
Salgado’s black-and-white images capture both the beauty and the destruction of the planet, the diversity of humanity, and the suffering of the poor. He treated the photograph not as decoration but as testimony: a record a society keeps of itself so it cannot look away.

Finding 1 / 4 · The turn
From spreadsheets to the Sahel
After leaving economics, Salgado became a social documentary photographer. His early work took him into the hardest places — the haunting images of starving people in the Sahel, and the miners of Brazil — setting the template for a career spent alongside those the world overlooks.
8 / 12 →
Finding 2 / 4 · Labour
“Workers”: an epic of manual toil
His 1993 project Workers is an epic portrait of the working class — hands, sweat and machinery — a global elegy for manual labour just as industry was being automated and offshored. Salgado photographed work as something heroic, not incidental.
9 / 12 →
Finding 3 / 4 · Displacement
“Migrations”: humanity in transit
In Migrations: Humanity in Transition (2000), Salgado recorded the displacement of people across more than 35 countries. It is one of the definitive photographic statements on refugees and mass movement — a subject only more urgent in the decades since.
10 / 12 →
Finding 4 / 4 · The Earth
“Genesis”: a vote of confidence in the planet
Begun in 2004, the eight-year project Genesis turned from human suffering to landscapes, wildlife and cultures untouched by modern industry — what Salgado called “a visual ode to the majesty and fragility of the Earth.” His later Amazônia carried that reverence deep into the rainforest.

Why this matters in 2026
A year of farewell exhibitions
Salgado died on 23 May 2025, and 2026 has become a year of tributes. Paris’s Hôtel de Ville showed nearly 200 of his photographs (to 6 June 2026), curated by his wife Lélia Wanick Salgado; from 24 October 2026 Bassano del Grappa in Italy stages a major retrospective — a moment to see the full arc of his work.
