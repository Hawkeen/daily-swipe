Agile Governance

Tsinghua’s Xue Lan on how China regulates what it can’t fully predict — recorded live at Summer Davos in Dalian

Sinica Podcast · host Kaiser Kuo · guest Xue Lan (Schwarzman College, Tsinghua) · published 1 July 2026 · ~6 min read

The hook

What happens when technology moves faster than the rules meant to govern it?

That is the question the episode puts to Xue Lan — one of China’s leading voices on AI policy and a co-architect of what Beijing calls “agile governance.”

6 / 13 →

The core idea

You can’t govern a technology you can’t predict with a slow, fixed rulebook — so regulate adaptively.

Xue lays out “agile governance” as a model for steering transformative technologies without either stifling innovation or sleepwalking into catastrophe. The regulator iterates as the technology moves, rather than waiting to write one perfect law.

7 / 13 →
Finding 1 / 5 · The pacing problem

Rules can’t keep up with the machines

The episode’s central frame is the “pacing problem”: the widening gap between how fast AI moves and how slowly regulation can catch up. It is not a Chinese problem but a global one — the question is who, if anyone, is getting the response right.

8 / 13 →
Finding 2 / 5 · What it means

Governance that iterates

“Agile governance” is an adaptive, iterative approach to regulating transformative technology — adjusting as the field develops instead of locking in rules that may be obsolete by the time they take effect. The aim is to avoid both extremes: throttling innovation on one side, courting catastrophe on the other.

9 / 13 →
Finding 3 / 5 · How China got here

From the 2017 plan to the tech crackdown

Xue traces China’s regulatory evolution: from the 2017 national AI plan, through the rules governing generative AI, to the 2021 crackdown on the tech sector. It is a record of a state repeatedly stress-testing how far and how fast to let a powerful technology run.

10 / 13 →
Finding 4 / 5 · Three models

China, the US and the EU, compared

The conversation sets China’s adaptive style against the American and European approaches to regulation — three very different bets on how to hold a fast-moving technology accountable without killing it.

11 / 13 →
Finding 5 / 5 · Can it travel?

Is Beijing’s model exportable?

The sharpest question of the episode: can an adaptive style forged inside China’s political system travel to other systems — including liberal democracies? Or does agile governance depend on a state that can move without the checks a democracy imposes?

Why this matters in 2026

Recorded as the regulation race went global

The talk was taped at the World Economic Forum’s Annual Meeting of the New Champions — “Summer Davos” — in Dalian, and released on 1 July 2026, just as the divergence between the Chinese, American and European ways of governing AI is becoming one of the decade’s defining contests. The pacing problem is global; the responses are not.
