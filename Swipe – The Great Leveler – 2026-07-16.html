Book of the day · 📖

The Great Leveler

Violence and the History of Inequality from the Stone Age to the Twenty-First Century

Walter Scheidel · Princeton University Press, 2017 · 536 pages · ~6 min read

Swipe →

5 / 12 →

The Hook

What actually reduces inequality? To judge by thousands of years of history: only catastrophe.

Scheidel, a Stanford historian of the ancient world, traces inequality from the Stone Age to today — and finds the same grim pattern everywhere: inequality declines when carnage and disaster strike, and increases when peace and stability return. As he puts it: inequality never dies peacefully.

6 / 12 →

The Core Thesis

Every major compression of inequality in the historical record was driven by violent shocks.

Ever since humans began to farm, herd livestock and pass assets to their children, economic inequality has been a defining feature of civilisation — and over thousands of years, only violent events have significantly lessened it. The book is the first to chart this role of violent shocks over the full sweep of human history, from the crises of the earliest civilisations to the world wars and communist revolutions of the twentieth century.

7 / 12 →
Finding 1 / 4 · The framework

The Four Horsemen of leveling

Scheidel names four forces that have repeatedly destroyed the fortunes of the rich: mass-mobilization warfare, transformative revolutions, state collapse and catastrophic plagues.

Across the full record, every single major compression of material inequality was driven by one or more of these four levelers — which is why the book’s cover carries Dürer’s Four Horsemen of the Apocalypse.

8 / 12 →
Finding 2 / 4 · The evidence

The world wars were history’s greatest levelers

The two world wars rank among the greatest levelers in history, producing the “Great Compression” — a massive attenuation of income and wealth inequality across developed countries in the mid-twentieth century.

The effect was so stark that the Financial Times reviewer Janan Ganesh joked: “Scheidel’s evidence is so persuasive that readers will find themselves cheering on the Black Death as a boost to median wages.”

9 / 12 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The provocation

The peaceful remedies barely register

The most unsettling part of the argument, as James C. Scott summarised it in the London Review of Books: “One by one Scheidel dismisses the non-catastrophic alternatives that have been the focus of virtually every peaceful movement for social justice: democracy, the extension of the franchise, education, economic growth, social democracy, trade unionism and the welfare state. Their effects, he demonstrates, have been comparatively trivial.”

Today the violence that reduced inequality in the past has diminished — a good thing, Scheidel stresses, but one that casts serious doubt on the prospects for a more equal future.

10 / 12 →
Finding 4 / 4 · The pushback

Where critics push back

Reviewers note that other measures tell a different story: absolute poverty, and inequality between countries or groups, offer many examples of violence making deprivation worse, not better. Others question the comparison itself: “from a policy perspective, violence is no alternative.”

And Martin Wolf drew the political moral in the Financial Times: “Scheidel suggests that inequality is sure to rise. We must prove him wrong. If we fail to do so, soaring inequality might slay democracy, too, in the end.”

11 / 12 →
Why this matters in 2026

The wealth-tax test is on the ballot

Scheidel’s question — can inequality be reduced peacefully? — is being tested right now. This autumn California votes on Proposition 40, a one-time 5% tax on the state’s billionaires, projected by its architects to raise about $100 billion, with 90% earmarked for Medicaid funding gaps.

The Washington Post reported this week (14 July 2026) that the left sees “a billionaire backlash — and an opening for a wealth tax”; polling finds the top 1% of US households hold 30% of net worth and a billionaire minimum tax draws 68% national support. Washington state, Maine, Minnesota and Rhode Island have all passed millionaire surcharges this cycle.

The Takeaway
