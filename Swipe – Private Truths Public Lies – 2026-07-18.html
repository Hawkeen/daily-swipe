Book of the day · 📖

Private Truths, Public Lies

The Social Consequences of Preference Falsification — why we say what we don’t believe, and what it does to the world

Timur Kuran · Harvard University Press · 1995 · 448 pages · ~6 min read

Swipe →

5 / 15 →

The Hook

Every time you praise a dinner you found bland, you are running the mechanism that keeps dictatorships alive.

Preference falsification, in Kuran’s definition, is “the act of misrepresenting one’s wants under perceived social pressures”. It happens constantly in everyday life — and, he argues, this small social lubricant scales up into one of the most consequential forces in politics.

6 / 15 →

The Core Thesis

Public opinion is not what people think. It is what people are willing to say — and the gap runs the world.

Drawing on economics, psychology, sociology and political science, Kuran builds a unified theory of how preference falsification “shapes collective decisions, orients structural change, sustains social stability, distorts human knowledge, and conceals political possibilities” — from communism’s fall to affirmative action to India’s caste system.

Finding 1 / 7 · The mechanism

Two preferences per person

Everyone carries a private preference and a public one. Whether you voice the private one depends on a trade-off: the inner psychological cost of lying about what you want versus the outer social cost of siding openly with an unpopular position.

Crucially, Kuran insists that choices and even desires “are not given and fixed” but are shaped by social pressure — above all, pressure imposed by other people.

8 / 15 →
Finding 2 / 7 · The consequence

Hated structures survive — and look stable

A common effect of preference falsification is “the preservation of widely disliked structures”. If everyone privately opposes a policy but publicly supports it, each opponent believes they are nearly alone — and the structure stands.

The second effect is subtler: “the conferment of an aura of stability on structures vulnerable to sudden collapse”. The stability is real-looking and entirely contrived.

Finding 3 / 7 · The avalanche

The revolutionary bandwagon

“When the support of a policy, tradition, or regime is largely contrived, a minor event may activate a bandwagon that generates massive yet unanticipated change.”

Each person has a different threshold for joining the opposition. As a few cross theirs, protest grows — lowering the cost for the next tier, whose defection lowers it further. A cascade nobody planned topples what everyone thought was solid.

Finding 4 / 7 · The proof case

1989: “Now out of never”

Image: Bundesarchiv, Bild 183-1989-1023-022 / Friedrich Gahlbeck / CC BY-SA 3.0 DE, via Wikimedia Commons. Leipzig Monday demonstration, 23 October 1989 — over 100,000 marched.

In his 1991 essay “Now Out of Never” (World Politics), Kuran used the theory to explain why the East European revolutions of 1989 surprised virtually everyone — including the experts. Communist regimes looked eternal precisely because preference falsification hid the breadth of private opposition, until the Leipzig marches revealed it and the bandwagon rolled.

Finding 5 / 7 · The deeper damage

Lies corrupt knowledge itself

“In distorting public opinion, preference falsification also corrupts public discourse and, hence, human knowledge.” When nobody voices doubts, the arguments against the status quo stop being made — and stop being heard.

The chilling corollary: structures held in place by falsified preferences “may, if the condition lasts long enough, achieve increasingly genuine acceptance”. The public lie eventually becomes a private truth.

12 / 15 →
Finding 6 / 7 · The range

Caste, affirmative action, communism

The book applies one theory to three very different puzzles: the unexpected fall of communism; the long “paucity of open opposition” to affirmative action in the United States; and “the durability of the beliefs that have sustained India’s caste system”.

Different continents, different politics — the same machinery of unspoken private truths and performed public support.

Finding 7 / 7 · The reception

“Inventive and sometimes astonishing”

Cass Sunstein, reviewing the book in the New Republic, called it “a terrific success” that bears “not just on revolutionary movements, but also on feminism, conformity, cognitive dissonance, the moral majority… Gorbachev, hippies and the caste system”.

He singled out what makes Kuran unusual among economists: the insistence that people’s choices and desires are “a function of social and psychological conditions” rather than fixed givens.

Why this matters in 2026

Pollsters are rediscovering Kuran

A 2025 study in Asian Affairs found that as Bangladesh grew more authoritarian under Sheikh Hasina (2009–2024), survey respondents became measurably more likely to falsify their stated satisfaction with the government — Kuran’s mechanism, captured live in polling data.

And in the US, a Pell Center national survey from June 2026 found nearly 1 in 5 Americans prefer authoritarian rule over democracy — a reminder that what people privately think about democracy itself has become an open empirical question.

The Takeaway

“A mass uprising results from multitudes of individual choices to participate in a movement for change; there is no actor named ‘the crowd’ or ‘the opposition.’”

— Timur Kuran, “Now Out of Never: The Element of Surprise in the East European Revolution of 1989”
