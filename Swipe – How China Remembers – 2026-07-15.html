How China Remembers

“How China’s view of its past informs its choices in the present” — historian Rana Mitter in conversation with Johannes Heller-John

MERICS China Podcast · Episode 272 · 13 July 2026 · 32:20

Swipe →

5 / 10 →

The Hook

Why does a rising superpower still talk about being invaded and occupied?

Because, Mitter argues, that memory is doing political work. In his words: “the reality of a China that was on the backfoot globally, invaded, occupied, and unable to make its own choices shapes the way that China’s elite and policy makers and wider population today think about Chinese place in the world.”

6 / 10 →

The Core Idea

To predict what Beijing does next, study what Beijing remembers.

The episode moves through four linked themes: how China remembers its own history, what the “New Chinese Nationalism” means, the current state of US–China relations, and where Europe sits in this geopolitical landscape. The interview was recorded on 13 May 2026 and released this Monday.

7 / 10 →
Finding 1 / 3 · The guest

The historian of China’s wartime memory

Rana Mitter holds the S.T. Lee Chair in US-Asia Relations at the Harvard Kennedy School and is one of the leading historians of modern China.

His prize-winning book China’s Good War: How World War II Is Shaping a New Nationalism (2020) — a Foreign Affairs Best Book of the Year — is the deeper study behind this conversation.

8 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 3 · The mechanism

From suppressed memory to “good war”

For most of its history the People’s Republic limited public discussion of the war against Japan — an experience of victimisation, and one in which Mao Zedong and Chiang Kai-shek had fought for the same goals.

From the 1980s, under Deng, the war was actively reinterpreted: to heal the divisions of the Cultural Revolution and to craft a new nationalism. Today the Party celebrates the “victory” — and deploys anti-Japanese sentiment as a potent vehicle for patriotism.

9 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 3 · The stakes

Memory as foreign policy — and Europe in between

Mitter’s broader argument: China’s reassessment of the World War II years is central to its newfound confidence abroad and to mounting nationalism at home.

That is what makes the episode’s closing questions — the state of US–China relations, and Europe’s position within that landscape — more than academic: the past is a live input into present-day strategy.
