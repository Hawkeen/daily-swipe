Book of the day · 📖
Debt
The First 5,000 Years
David Graeber
Melville House · 2011 · ~540 pp · ~6 min read
Swipe →
3 / 9 →
The Hook
What if money was never invented to fix barter — because the barter economy never really existed?
Graeber takes aim at the story every economics textbook tells: that money arose to replace an awkward, complicated barter system. He argues there is not a shred of evidence for it.
4 / 9 →
The Big Idea
Credit came first. Coins and barter came later.
Graeber inverts the textbook sequence of barter → money → credit. Credit — the ledger of who owes what to whom — is the most primitive form of commerce. Debt is probably the oldest means of trade; cash and barter are later developments that surface mainly when trust breaks down.
5 / 9 →
Finding 1 / 4 · The myth of barter
No shred of evidence
The commodity theory of money — money emerging from early barter — holds sway in neo-classical economics. Graeber points out it is an imaginary history: there is no historical or anthropological evidence that a barter economy ever existed and was then fixed by money.
6 / 9 →
Finding 2 / 4 · What actually came first
Ledgers of who owes whom
Credit, Graeber argues, is the original form of commerce — supplemented by barter or immediate monetary exchange only when social conditions deteriorate enough to make credit arrangements impossible. Cash and barter are usually limited to low-trust dealings with strangers or those deemed not credit-worthy.
7 / 9 →
Finding 3 / 4 · Money before markets
“Social currencies”
Money first appeared as social currency — used to rearrange relationships among people: marriage, funerals, blood money and other social functions — not to buy and sell things. Commercial money began as credit: tallies of loans in a shared unit of account, settled periodically.
8 / 9 →
Finding 4 / 4 · The primacy of debt
Debt has usually been the rule
Across history, debt has typically retained its primacy, with cash and barter the exceptions. Reframing money as a record of obligations rather than a thing turns “the economy” into a story about morality, trust and who owes what to whom.
9 / 9 ✓
The Takeaway
Before there were coins or markets, there were promises — debt is the oldest economic technology we have.
