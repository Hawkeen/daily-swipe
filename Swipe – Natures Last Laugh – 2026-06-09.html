The hook
Rome was brought down “as much by germs as by Germans.”
For 1,500 years we’ve blamed barbarians, Christianity, or imperial decadence. Kyle Harper — a classicist who learned to read ice cores and DNA — argues the real killers were three pandemics and a sky that turned cold. Rome’s very success built the highway its destroyers travelled on.
3 / 10 →
The core argument
The fate of Rome “was decided not just by emperors, soldiers, and barbarians but also by volcanic eruptions, solar cycles, climate instability, and devastating viruses and bacteria.”
Harper marries paleoclimatology, bioarchaeology, evolutionary biology and ancient genomics to the classical sources. The empire didn’t fall in 476 because the Visigoths got rowdy — it endured one of “the most dramatic sequences of climate change in the entire Holocene” while three intercontinental disease events tore through a population with no immunity. A story of nature’s triumph over human ambition.

Finding 1 / 5
The empire was built on a 350-year lucky streak of weather
Harper calls it the Roman Climate Optimum (c. 200 BC–AD 150): warm, wet, and unusually stable. Crops surged, cities swelled, the Mediterranean ran like one big economic engine. Rome “expanded and consolidated power” under skies it took for granted.
Then around AD 150 came the Roman Transitional Period — three centuries of climate disorganization. The luck was running out before any senator noticed.
5 / 10 →
Finding 2 / 5
AD 165: smallpox makes its global debut, inside the empire
The Antonine Plague (AD 165–180), known to Galen, “was probably the global debut of the smallpox virus.” It struck just as the Climate Optimum ended — soldiers returning from the Parthian frontier carrying a virgin pathogen into a hyper-connected Mediterranean.
This wasn’t a footnote. Harper argues it “began the deterioration of the Roman Empire” — a mortality shock the imperial economy never fully absorbed.
6 / 10 →
Finding 3 / 5
A century later, an unknown affliction sent the empire into a tailspin
In the mid-third century, the Plague of Cyprian — pathogen still uncertain, possibly a filovirus akin to Ebola — collided with civil war and currency collapse. Harper restored to history a pandemic that had “basically gone unnoticed in scholarship” before.
The empire survived, but reorganised: Diocletian, the tetrarchy, the long road to Christian late antiquity. The bones of the “crisis of the third century” turn out to be partly biological.
7 / 10 →
Finding 4 / 5
540 AD: volcanoes dim the sun, bubonic plague arrives
“A spasm of volcanic activity in the AD 530s and 540s brought on the most frigid spell in the entire Late Holocene” — the Late Antique Little Ice Age. Crops failed across Justinian’s reconquered Mediterranean.
Then in 541 the first pandemic of Yersinia pestis — the Justinianic Plague — arrived. Harper calls this the deepest blow: “arid, cold, and plagued, the empire lost its ability to weather foreign threats, field an army, and maintain its economy.”
8 / 10 →
Finding 5 / 5
Rome’s greatest achievement was the highway its destroyers travelled on
Harper’s sharpest line: “The main drama of disease history has been the constant emergence of untried germs from wild hosts, finding human groups linked in ever-larger pacts of mutually assured infection.”
The roads, ports, garrisons and grain ships that made Rome an unprecedented “efflorescence” also made it a perfect circulatory system for novel pathogens. Connectedness was the gift — and the trap.
9 / 10 →
Why now
A record-warm year. A virus jumping species. The two halves of Harper’s thesis, in the news at once.
The UN’s WMO confirms 2026 is virtually certain to be among the four warmest years on record — the fourth in a row above 1.4°C of pre-industrial warming. March 2026 shattered all-time US departure records by 9.35°F, 18 states setting monthly highs.
Meanwhile H5N1 has infected nearly 70 people in the US since April 2024 (one death); cumulative WHO data 2003–2025 puts the case fatality rate around 48%. Scientists are watching for sustained human-to-human transmission — the threshold Galen saw cross in AD 165.
