Book of the day · 📖
Determined
A Science of Life Without Free Will — the neurobiologist’s case that nobody is the author of their own choices
Robert M. Sapolsky · 2023 · ~6 min read
Swipe →
5 / 11 →
The Hook
Did you choose to read this? Sapolsky says: not in any way that counts.
A Stanford neurobiologist and primatologist spends 400 pages dismantling the last hiding places of free will — and concludes that the sense of authorship you feel over your own decisions is a comforting fiction the brain tells itself.
6 / 11 →
The Core Thesis
You are your luck — all of it.
Sapolsky argues our choices are fully determined by genetics, hormones, upbringing, culture and circumstance — none of which we selected. In his words, we are “the cumulative biological and environmental luck, over which we had no control, that has brought us to any moment.”
7 / 11 →
Finding 1 / 4 · The position
A “hard incompatibilist”
Sapolsky calls himself a hard incompatibilist: we almost certainly lack free will — and moral responsibility — whether or not the universe is strictly deterministic. Even if some events are random, randomness isn’t choice; a dice-roll in your neurons doesn’t hand you authorship.
8 / 11 →
Finding 2 / 4 · The regress
Turtles all the way down
Point to a free choice and Sapolsky asks where the intention came from — then where that came from. Each answer traces back through this morning’s hormones, this decade’s culture, childhood, the womb, your genes: an unbroken chain of causes you never chose.
9 / 11 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The stakes
Justice without blame
Much of the book examines the ethical consequences of justice and punishment in a model of behaviour with no free will. If no one truly deserves blame or credit, Sapolsky argues, we should still restrain the dangerous — but as a matter of protection, not retribution or moral desert.
10 / 11 →
Finding 4 / 4 · The counter-case
“A prime example of scientism”
Critics push back hard. Historian of science Jessica Riskin argues science can’t disprove free will because it isn’t a scientific question but a philosophical one — and to treat it as settled by biology is, she writes, “a prime example of scientism.” The debate is very much live.
11 / 11 ✓
The Takeaway
“We are nothing more or less than the sum of that which we could not control — our biology, our environments, their interactions.” — Robert Sapolsky
