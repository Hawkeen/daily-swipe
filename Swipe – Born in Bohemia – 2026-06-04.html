The hook
The dollar is older than the United States — and never really belonged to it.
Brendan Greeley — former US economics editor at the Financial Times, now a PhD candidate in financial history at Princeton — argues that America didn’t invent its money, doesn’t actually control it, and that the dollar had “empowered and destroyed nations long before it washed up on colonial shores.”
3 / 11 →
The core argument
Money is a product — not a national symbol, not a magic ring of confidence.
In Greeley’s reading, the dollar is the world’s reserve currency because it has spent five centuries being useful to merchants and bankers across borders. The state on the coin keeps changing — Bohemian, Spanish, colonial American, federal — but the role stays the same.
Reviewer Mark Blyth in the publisher’s praise: Greeley’s “key insight is that money is not some kind of magical barter-replacement device or a ring of confidence. Rather, it’s a product.”

Finding 1 / 7
The first dollar was a German word for a coin from a Czech mine
In the 1520s, a high-quality silver coin was minted in Sankt Joachimsthal — today Jáchymov in Czechia. The coins were called Joachimsthalers, shortened to thalers. They were originally dividends paid out to investors in the local mine.
With a steady supply from Bohemia, the thaler became a reliable medium of exchange among bankers and merchants and was eventually copied by Spain. “Dollar” is simply the English mangling of thaler.
5 / 11 →
Finding 2 / 7
Spanish “pieces of eight” crowded the pound out of British America
Once Spain hit silver in its American colonies on industrial scale, it cranked out its own version of the thaler — the real de a ocho, the “piece of eight.” These Spanish silver dollars then flowed through British colonial trade in such volume that they crowded out the official Pound Sterling.
American colonists kept their books in pounds and shillings but settled accounts in dollars. The currency Americans called their own arrived already battle-tested by Spain, China and the global silver trade.
6 / 11 →
Finding 3 / 7
Colonial Maryland was already printing “dollars” in the 1760s
When colonial officials in Maryland in the 1760s ran short of metal and printed temporary bills of credit, the bills were denominated in dollars — not pounds. The American dollar as a unit of account existed roughly a generation before the United States did.
Greeley uses moments like this to show that paper-money experiments in the colonies didn’t create the dollar; they ratified a unit the merchant world had already settled on.
7 / 11 →
Finding 4 / 7
Hawarden, Iowa printed its own dollars in the Great Depression
When federal banks failed in the early 1930s, the small town of Hawarden, Iowa kept commerce alive by issuing local scrip — community-backed paper that circulated alongside dollars. Greeley reports this strand by talking, as he puts it on the show, to “nice people in Iowa” with boxes of the surviving notes.
It’s the same pattern as Joachimsthal and Maryland: when official money fails, communities mint their own. Money is whatever a network of users agrees to honour.
8 / 11 →
Finding 5 / 7
The biggest dollar market is the one Washington doesn’t touch
Most of today’s dollars don’t sit in the Federal Reserve’s system — they sit in the multi-trillion-dollar eurodollar market: dollar-denominated deposits and credit created by banks outside the United States. As reviewer David McWilliams notes, Greeley walks from medieval Bohemia “right up to today’s multitrillion-dollar eurodollar market.”
The implication: a huge share of how the dollar actually works is run by foreign banks for foreign customers. America issues the unit; the world manufactures the money supply.
9 / 11 →
Finding 6 / 7
Money wanders. Sovereignty doesn’t catch up.
Greeley, in a line picked out by the Foreign Policy review: “Money is still today a frantic, unending, global competition, with different assets wandering across borders to serve as money without any regard to sovereignty at all.”
That cuts both ways. The dollar enriches Wall Street — central banks held nearly $6.7 trillion in dollar reserves as of 2023, more than three times any other currency. But Greeley argues it “spawns a vast financial industry that enriches the wealthy, even as the rest of the country’s industries suffer.”
10 / 11 →
Why now
The de-dollarization debate is finally a 2026 fight
The dollar’s share of global reserves has slid from 72% in 2000 to roughly 58% in 2026. Russia now does about 90% of its intra-BRICS trade in national currencies. BRICS central banks bought over 1,100 tons of gold in 2025 — the largest single-year increase in seven decades.
BRICS Pay, designed to bypass SWIFT, is slated to launch by end-2026. Trump has threatened a 10% tariff on any country associated with BRICS. The paradox Greeley’s history sets up: coercing the world to use the dollar may accelerate the very flight he describes.
