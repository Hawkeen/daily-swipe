Book of the day · 📖
Seeing Like a State
James C. Scott · How Certain Schemes to Improve the Human Condition Have Failed · Yale University Press · 1998
Why the grandest, most scientific plans to better human life so often end in ruin.
Swipe →

The hook
Why do grand, well-funded, “scientific” schemes to improve human life — new cities, model farms, rational forests — so often end in disaster?
Scott’s answer: they fail for the same reason, in the same way — and the pattern repeats across centuries and continents.
4 / 9 →
The core thesis
To govern, a state must first make society legible — simple enough to measure, tax and control. The trouble starts when it mistakes the map for the territory.
When that drive for legibility fuses with high-modernist confidence and the power to impose it from above, planners override the messy local knowledge that real societies run on — and the scheme collapses.

Finding 1 / 4
Legibility: the state’s way of seeing
Permanent surnames, censuses, cadastral maps, uniform languages, standard units of measure — all are tools to make a tangled society readable from the centre. Originally built to tax and conscript, they quietly reshape the world into the simplified version officials can actually see.
6 / 9 →
Finding 2 / 4
The parable of scientific forestry
Scott opens with 18th- and 19th-century German forest management: messy woodland was cleared and re-planted as tidy rows of timber trees, optimised for yield. The first generation thrived — then the soil and ecosystem, stripped of their underbrush and diversity, paid a serious price and the forest began to die. The blueprint that ignored the living system destroyed it.
7 / 9 →
Finding 3 / 4
High modernism vs. the living city
Scott sets Le Corbusier’s utopian, functionalist, top-down cities against Jane Jacobs’ faith in the unplanned complexity of real neighbourhoods. Tellingly, the planned city of Brasília only works because of the unplanned “Free City” of squatters that grew up to serve it. Order designed on paper needs disorder to survive.
8 / 9 →
Finding 4 / 4
Mētis: the knowledge planners erase
Against the planner stands mētis — the practical, local, hard-won know-how of people who actually live and work in a place, which can’t be boiled down to a formula. Schemes that neglect and destroy this knowledge are the ones that turn harmful, even catastrophic. The “illegible” small farmer often understands the system better than the blueprint does.

The takeaway
“Designed or planned social order is necessarily schematic; it always ignores essential features of any real, functioning social order.”
— James C. Scott, Seeing Like a State
Concrete next step
🌲 Next time you meet a sweeping “rational” plan — in policy, tech or your own org — ask: whose local knowledge does this simplification quietly erase?
