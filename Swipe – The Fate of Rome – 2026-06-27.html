Book of the day · 📖
The Fate of Rome
Climate, Disease, and the End of an Empire
Kyle Harper
Princeton University Press · 2017 · ~440 pp · ~6 min read
Swipe →
3 / 9 →
The Hook
What if Rome wasn’t only conquered by barbarians — but by germs and a shifting climate?
Harper argues Rome’s fate was decided not just by emperors, soldiers and barbarians, but also by volcanic eruptions, solar cycles, climate instability and devastating viruses and bacteria.
4 / 9 →
The Big Idea
Nature was a protagonist in Roman history, not a backdrop.
Against narratives that treat the environment as a static stage, Harper synthesises climate science and genomics to argue that Rome’s ecological context was deeply influential in its sociopolitical history — shaping the empire’s rise and helping to unmake it.
5 / 9 →
Finding 1 / 4 · The first pandemic
The Antonine Plague (after AD 160)
An outbreak that was probably smallpox struck the empire in the late second century. Harper counts it as serious but not the “neutron bomb” the later plague would be — claiming on the order of seven to eight million lives and proving less socioeconomically disruptive.
6 / 9 →
Finding 2 / 4 · The great mortality
The Justinianic Plague (541 CE)
The bubonic plague first appears in the records in 541 CE. Harper links its emergence to climate cooling, which he argues created the conditions that tied the bacterium to black rats and their fleas — and finally to humans.
7 / 9 →
Finding 3 / 4 · The unseen actors
Volcanoes, the sun, and an unstable climate
Harper’s wider point is that the fate of Rome was decided not only by human actors but by volcanic eruptions, solar cycles and climate instability — forces traditional histories left out of the story entirely.
8 / 9 →
Finding 4 / 4 · The pushback
Critics say the story is too neat
Harper’s thesis has drawn sharp response. Reviewers argue he overlooked genuine scholarly controversy — for instance presenting an oversimplified, catastrophic narrative of the Antonine plague — a reminder that “collapse” histories invite caution.
9 / 9 ✓
The Takeaway
Rome did not simply fall to men with swords: a changing climate and new diseases were among the forces that decided its fate.
